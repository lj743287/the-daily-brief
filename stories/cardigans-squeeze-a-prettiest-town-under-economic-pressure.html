Cardigan’s squeeze: a ‘prettiest town’ under economic pressure

Shops, cafés and restaurants in the west Wales town report rising costs and lower footfall. Local owners warn of a fragile high street unless costs ease and visitors return.

Cardigan’s handsome main streets and riverside setting have long drawn visitors to west Wales, helping independent shops and cafés to flourish. Today, business owners describe a more fragile picture as higher costs collide with uneven footfall, leaving even well‑loved venues on tighter margins.

Energy bills and wholesale prices have moderated from their peaks but remain significantly above pre‑pandemic norms, according to traders. For small hospitality businesses with long hours and refrigeration, those costs continue to bite alongside higher wages and supply chain pressures that are hard to pass on to customers.

Business rates and rents are another strain. Some owners say partial reliefs have not kept pace with valuations, while others face lease renewals that reflect a property market buoyed by demand for second homes and holiday lets. The result, they argue, is a risk of empty units if trading conditions do not improve.

Footfall has become more seasonal, with wet weather and squeezed household budgets thinning out mid‑week trade. Retailers report cautious spending patterns even when streets are busy, while cafés and restaurants say bookings are later and more sensitive to small price changes than before.

Local efforts to keep the high street vibrant are under way. Events and markets aim to spread custom beyond peak weeks, and there is growing emphasis on local sourcing to retain value in the area. Businesses say targeted support on energy efficiency, rates and planning could help stabilise trading through the winter months.

Despite the pressures, Cardigan’s strengths remain clear: a compact centre, a strong cluster of independents and a sense of place that is hard to replicate. The challenge, traders say, is to bridge to a steadier cost base and a more predictable flow of visitors so that the town’s character is sustained rather than hollowed out.
