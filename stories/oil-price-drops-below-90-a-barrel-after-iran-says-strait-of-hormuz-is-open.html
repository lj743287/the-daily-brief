Oil price drops below $90 a barrel after Iran says strait of Hormuz is open

Brent crude fell below $90 a barrel after Iran said the Strait of Hormuz was open for commercial shipping during the ceasefire. The move eases immediate pressure on energy markets, but prices remain sensitive to any renewed disruption.

Oil prices fell after Iran said the Strait of Hormuz was open for commercial shipping during the ceasefire, easing some of the immediate pressure on energy markets. Brent crude dropped below $90 a barrel, a sign that traders were ready to respond quickly to even a temporary reduction in risk.

The strait is one of the world’s most important oil chokepoints, so any threat to shipping can rattle markets far beyond the Gulf. When Iran signalled that vessels could transit the waterway, traders pushed prices lower and gas markets followed.

The relief may be short-lived. The ceasefire is only temporary, and any renewed disruption could send prices back up just as fast. That means consumers, airlines and governments are still exposed to volatility.

The drop also reflects how closely energy markets are now tied to the wider conflict. As long as the war remains unresolved, shipping, fuel costs and inflation expectations will remain sensitive to political headlines.

For now, the opening of the strait has taken some heat out of the market, but it has not removed the underlying risk.
