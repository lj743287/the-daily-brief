OpenAI urges firms to test four-day weeks in the AI era

The company says shorter weeks could absorb productivity shocks as automation scales. It’s a provocation to policymakers and employers to consider work-time, incomes and safety nets together.

OpenAI has urged employers to test four-day working weeks as artificial intelligence spreads through offices and factories, arguing that shorter schedules could help absorb sudden productivity shifts. The suggestion is framed less as a edict than as a provocation: a call for companies and policymakers to consider work-time, incomes and safety nets together, rather than treating automation as a purely technical upgrade.

The timing matters. Generative AI tools are being embedded into customer support, coding, marketing and research, with early evidence that they can accelerate some tasks while displacing or reshaping others. In past technology waves, aggregate productivity gains arrived unevenly and often later than expected, while the distribution of benefits proved politically fraught. A managed reduction in hours, its advocates say, could cushion dislocation and share time savings more broadly. It remains, however, a proposal to be tested rather than a settled recipe.

Experiments with four-day weeks in recent years—from pilots in the UK to public-sector trials in Iceland and company-led schemes elsewhere—have shown promising but not uniform results. Many participating organisations reported stable or improved output, better staff retention and higher wellbeing; others struggled with coverage, handovers or client expectations and reverted to old patterns. The evidence base is growing yet still limited, with selection effects and sector differences complicating sweeping conclusions.

The policy implications stretch beyond scheduling. Shorter weeks without pay protection risk squeezing lower-wage and shift-based workers, while unmanaged intensification could simply compress stress into fewer days. If governments want to test hour reductions at scale, they may need to pair them with pay floors, training entitlements, portable benefits and modernised social insurance. Debate will also turn to how any AI-driven productivity dividend is shared—through time, wages, tax, or a mix—and whether incentives are needed to encourage experimentation without disadvantaging early movers.

For employers, the operational question is whether AI can reliably unlock enough efficiency to sustain fewer hours without degrading service or safety. Trials that work typically redesign workflows, invest in tools, measure outputs rather than presence, and involve staff in rota and process changes. Some sectors—software, professional services, back-office functions—may find it easier to pilot than round-the-clock healthcare, logistics or hospitality. Global teams face legal and time-zone constraints, and the risk of merely squeezing the same workload into fewer days remains a real concern if processes are not rethought.

What happens next will be empirical. Expect more controlled pilots across geographies, closer scrutiny from investors and regulators, and better measurement of productivity, wellbeing and inequality effects. As jurisdictions weigh new rules on high-risk AI and labour protections, the four-day week is likely to serve as a testbed for a broader social compact in the automation era. The open question is whether the AI dividend accrues primarily to margins, wages or time—and whether employers and governments can coordinate fast enough to make experimentation safe, fair and informative.
