Mauritius vows to ‘decolonise’ Chagos after UK shelves handover law

Port Louis pledged to “spare no effort” to regain control after UK legislation stalled amid shifting US support. The dispute, long framed by international legal rulings in Mauritius’ favour, is entwined with security access and regional alignments.

Mauritius has pledged to spare no effort to regain control of the Chagos archipelago after the UK shelved legislation intended to enable a transfer, amid signs of shifting US support. The move revives a long‑running dispute entwining decolonisation claims with strategic military access.

In 2019 the International Court of Justice issued an advisory opinion that the UK should end its administration of the islands, and the UN General Assembly urged London to facilitate the process. Negotiations in recent years have explored a handover that might preserve defence arrangements.

Washington’s position is pivotal because the largest island, Diego Garcia, hosts a key US base. Reports of hesitation in the US capital have complicated the UK’s legislative push, reflecting broader security calculations in the Indian Ocean.

Mauritius is likely to escalate diplomatic efforts at the UN and seek backing from regional bodies, while keeping channels open to London and Washington. The legal and political momentum built by prior international rulings remains central to its case.

For the Chagossian community, any settlement raises practical questions of return, resettlement, citizenship and compensation after decades of displacement. Their voices will be critical to the legitimacy of future arrangements.

Next steps will be shaped by parliamentary timetables in London, evolving US priorities and the security environment in the region. Any breakthrough will almost certainly hinge on explicit guarantees that reconcile sovereignty with continued access for defence partners.
