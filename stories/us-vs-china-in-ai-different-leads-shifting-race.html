US vs China in AI: different leads, shifting race

The US and China dominate different layers of the AI stack, from chips and frontier models to data access and deployment. Both sides aim to prevent dependence on a rival’s ecosystem, and the contest could be reshaped by export controls, compute scaling and safety regulation.

The United States and China dominate different layers of the artificial intelligence stack, shaping a contest that is less a single race than a set of overlapping competitions. From advanced chips and frontier models to data access and deployment, each side is investing to avoid dependence on the other’s ecosystem, while export controls, compute scaling and emerging safety rules could yet reorder the field.

At the hardware level, the US and its allies retain a clear lead in cutting‑edge accelerators and manufacturing know‑how, with Nvidia’s GPUs and TSMC’s most advanced processes central to training large models. China is pushing domestic chip design and fabrication to reduce exposure to sanctions, but remains constrained at the highest performance nodes. That gap matters most for training state‑of‑the‑art systems, even as cheaper, locally produced silicon can power widespread inference.

In models, US‑based labs have set the pace on general‑purpose systems, attracting capital, talent and compute. Chinese firms have developed competitive large language models for Mandarin and regional markets, but face tighter rules on content and access to top‑tier hardware. Benchmarks are an imperfect guide and rapidly evolving, yet they underscore a picture of US strength at the frontier and Chinese depth in tailored, fast‑iterating applications.

Data and deployment patterns diverge just as sharply. China’s super‑apps, industrial platforms and public‑sector programmes give it enormous channels for AI rollout across logistics, finance, retail and city management. The US, meanwhile, leans on enterprise software, cloud platforms and developer ecosystems that speed diffusion across sectors globally. Different data governance regimes shape what can be collected and how systems behave, with consequences for performance and trust.

Policy tools are now strategic levers. US export controls seek to slow China’s access to high‑end compute, while subsidies, standards work and safety frameworks aim to support domestic capability. China has introduced rules for generative AI and security reviews for high‑impact systems, tying market access to compliance and content controls. Beyond both, Europe’s AI Act and global standard‑setting will influence how models are assessed, audited and deployed across borders.

Several forces could shift the balance. Breakthroughs in algorithmic efficiency, novel architectures or packaging could ease compute bottlenecks. Tighter safety regulation or liability regimes may raise costs for general‑purpose models and favour domain‑specific systems. And as both sides harden supply chains, AI ecosystems may diverge further, with interoperability and market access becoming competitive assets in their own right.
