Britain could adopt single market rules without MPs’ vote as part of UK-EU reset

Ministers are preparing a potentially significant shift in the UK-EU relationship, with parliamentary scrutiny likely to become a major flashpoint.

Ministers are considering a significant shift in the UK's relationship with the European Union, with Britain potentially adopting some single market rules without a vote in the Commons. That would mark a notable step in the government's wider effort to reset ties with Brussels after years of post-Brexit friction.

The issue is politically sensitive because it goes to the heart of sovereignty and parliamentary scrutiny. Supporters of the approach may argue that closer alignment would reduce trade barriers and practical frictions, while critics will say it hands too much influence to EU rules without enough democratic oversight.

If the plan advances, the main flashpoint is likely to be Parliament itself. Ministers may see regulatory alignment as a pragmatic way to improve trade, but they will still need to manage accusations that they are restoring EU influence by the back door.
