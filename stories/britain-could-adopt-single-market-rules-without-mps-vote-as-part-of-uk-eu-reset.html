Britain could adopt single market rules without MPs’ vote as part of UK-EU reset

Ministers are preparing legislation that could allow closer alignment with EU single market rules without a full parliamentary vote. The proposal is likely to sharpen political tensions over sovereignty, trade and the government’s post-Brexit strategy.

Ministers are preparing legislation that could allow Britain to align with EU single market rules without requiring a full parliamentary vote. The proposal would deepen the government’s planned reset with Brussels, but it also risks reopening one of the most sensitive arguments left by Brexit: how far the UK should accept rules shaped in Europe without direct domestic consent.

Supporters of closer alignment argue that the UK has already paid a high economic price for friction in trade, particularly for smaller exporters that face extra paperwork and regulatory divergence. They see the move as a practical way to reduce barriers and stabilise relations with the EU, rather than as a reversal of Brexit itself.

Critics will see something else: a transfer of influence over standards, made easier by executive action and harder for MPs to scrutinise. That tension between economic pragmatism and constitutional control is likely to define the political row if the legislation progresses, especially in a Parliament where post-Brexit sovereignty remains an emotive issue.
