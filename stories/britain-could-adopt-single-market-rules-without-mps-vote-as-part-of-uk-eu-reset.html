Britain could adopt single market rules without MPs’ vote as part of UK-EU reset

Ministers are considering legislation that could deepen alignment with EU rules, raising fresh questions over parliamentary scrutiny and sovereignty.

Ministers are considering legislation that could allow Britain to adopt single market rules without a fresh vote by MPs, as part of a wider reset with the EU. The idea would deepen regulatory alignment, but it immediately raises questions about parliamentary scrutiny and sovereignty.

The proposal reflects a broader shift in post-Brexit politics: the practical benefits of closer alignment are increasingly being weighed against the symbolic costs. For businesses, more consistent rules could reduce friction; for critics, the democratic issue is whether such changes should be made explicitly in Parliament.

How far the government goes will depend on the final shape of the reset and the political appetite for confrontation. Any move seen as softening Brexit too quietly is likely to meet resistance, even if it improves trade relations.
