Britain could adopt single market rules without MPs’ vote as part of UK-EU reset

Ministers are weighing a closer EU alignment that could revive arguments over sovereignty, scrutiny and the practical limits of post-Brexit policy.

Ministers are considering a closer alignment with single market rules that could take Britain a step nearer to the European Union’s regulatory orbit, possibly without a direct vote in Parliament.

The idea is politically sensitive because it revives old arguments about sovereignty, accountability and the degree to which post-Brexit Britain should tie itself to Brussels in order to ease trade and reduce friction.

Supporters will argue that closer alignment could make business easier and improve market access. Critics are likely to say it would amount to rule-taking by another name, especially if MPs are bypassed.

What happens next will show whether the government is prepared to spend political capital on a pragmatic reset with Europe, or whether the limits of that project remain as important as its ambitions.
