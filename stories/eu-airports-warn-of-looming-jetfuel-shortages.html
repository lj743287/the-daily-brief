EU airports warn of looming jet‑fuel shortages

Airports Council International Europe reported rising concern over jet‑fuel availability as Gulf supplies tighten. Airlines face potential schedule adjustments, longer routings and higher costs if constraints persist.

European airports have warned that jet‑fuel supplies could tighten if restricted traffic through the Strait of Hormuz persists, according to Airports Council International Europe cited by the BBC. The alert underscores how quickly an oil shipping disruption can ripple into aviation markets, where kerosene stocks are lean and logistics complex.

Airports and airlines are preparing mitigations. Carriers are planning more fuel tankering on outbound legs from well‑supplied hubs, introducing contingency stops on longer routes, and reviewing schedules to match uplift capacity. Some airports are coordinating closely with suppliers to smooth deliveries and avoid localised stockouts during peak periods.

Europe produces aviation fuel but relies on imports from the Middle East and India to balance demand, and refinery yields cannot be shifted overnight. Blending component availability, refinery maintenance and shipping constraints all play a role, meaning even modest delays through Hormuz can leave certain hubs exposed while others remain comfortable.

Operational impacts could include route adjustments to avoid higher‑risk airspace, with longer flight times nudging up fuel burn. Higher fuel bills typically pressure airline margins and, if persistent, can feed into fares. In parallel, ground handling and refuelling operations may need to adapt to more variable delivery schedules.

Authorities are watching closely. National energy agencies and aviation regulators are monitoring stock levels, and industry bodies are urging pragmatic steps such as temporary flexibility on trucking hours for fuel distribution where needed. Safety standards remain the priority, with operational choices guided by robust risk assessments.

The outlook hinges on maritime conditions. A sustained improvement in transit security would begin to ease pressures, but any rebound in supply would lag as shipping queues clear. If constraints endure into the summer travel season, progressive tightening and more frequent operational adjustments are likely.
