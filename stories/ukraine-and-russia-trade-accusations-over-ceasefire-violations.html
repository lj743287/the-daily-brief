Ukraine and Russia trade accusations over ceasefire violations

Kyiv and Moscow each accuse the other of hundreds of breaches during an Easter truce, with President Volodymyr Zelensky warning of a ‘symmetrical’ response. The fragile pause underscores how contested frontlines and mutual distrust could quickly return fighting to pre-truce levels.

Kyiv and Moscow accused each other of hundreds of breaches during an Easter truce, with Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky saying his country would deliver a symmetrical response to attacks. The claims, issued by both sides’ military authorities, could not be independently verified.

The front lines remain highly contested, with artillery exchanges and drone activity reported along segments of the eastern and southern theatres. While both sides announced restraint in principle, neither appears willing to accept conditions that might compromise battlefield positions.

Civilian areas near the front continue to face risks from shelling and debris, and emergency services reported sporadic disruptions to power and water networks. Local authorities urged residents to follow safety guidance as the security situation remains fluid.

Analysts say both militaries have used the lull to rotate units, repair equipment and fortify positions. Threats of symmetrical retaliation raise the prospect of an escalatory cycle once the truce window closes or is deemed ineffective on the ground.

International calls to consolidate the pause have had limited traction, reflecting the lack of trust and the absence of a broader political framework. Public expectations inside Ukraine are muted, with communities near the front braced for a rapid return to active hostilities.

With verification mechanisms weak and incentives to probe for advantage strong, the pause risks unravelling quickly. Attention in the coming days will focus on the tempo of long-range strikes and local ground assaults that could set the tone for the next phase of fighting.
