Nigerian airstrike targeting jihadists reportedly kills civilians

Amnesty International and local media say more than 100 civilians died after a strike hit a market in Yobe state. Authorities acknowledged a misfire but gave few details, prompting renewed scrutiny of targeting, accountability and civilian protection in counter‑insurgency operations.

Amnesty International and local media report that an airstrike intended for jihadist targets struck a market in Yobe state, killing more than 100 civilians. Authorities have acknowledged a misfire but released few details, and the air force has not publicly addressed the allegations beyond confirming an operation in the area.

The incident comes amid Nigeria’s long campaign against Boko Haram and Islamic State West Africa Province, insurgencies that often operate near civilian populations. The complexity of targeting in dense or mixed‑use areas has repeatedly raised concerns about the conduct of air operations and the safeguards in place to minimise harm.

Rights groups and community leaders are calling for a transparent, independent investigation and public accountability. Past inquiries into civilian casualties have rarely produced comprehensive findings, feeding frustration about impunity and undermining public confidence in the security forces.

The humanitarian impact is acute. Markets are economic lifelines for rural communities, and strikes on such hubs disrupt food access and livelihoods. Local reports describe extensive casualties and significant numbers of wounded, with pressure on already stretched medical facilities.

Under international humanitarian law, parties to conflict must distinguish between combatants and civilians and take all feasible precautions to avoid or at least minimise incidental loss of civilian life. Questions will focus on intelligence quality, weapon selection, timing and post‑strike assessment procedures.

Abuja is likely to announce a formal probe amid domestic and international scrutiny. The risk now is that militants exploit public anger for recruitment or propaganda, while communities demand stronger protection and compensation. Donors and partners may also press for improved targeting practices and civilian‑harm mitigation training.
