Licensing plan could curb coasteering in parts of Pembrokeshire

A new scheme in a coasteering heartland could restrict activity in some areas to protect the environment and manage safety. Guides and businesses say they need clarity to plan for the season.

Pembrokeshire’s rugged cliffs helped make coasteering a Welsh export, drawing adventure groups to bays and headlands each season. A new licensing scheme under consideration would formalise where and when commercial activity can take place in sensitive areas, citing environmental protection and safety management.

Officials say the intention is to avoid overcrowding at popular sites, reduce disturbance to wildlife and improve coordination among operators and landowners. Proposals under discussion include capped group sizes, timed access windows and training standards for guides, building on voluntary codes that have operated for years.

Businesses broadly accept the need for clear rules but want certainty on how any limits will be set and enforced. Guides point out that weather and sea conditions can shift plans at short notice, and argue that a rigid system could push groups into less suitable locations if flexibility is not built in.

The scheme would sit alongside existing frameworks. Commercial providers already work under landowner permissions on some stretches, and adventure activity licensing covers safety management for certain client groups. The new approach would target specific coastal sites where pressure is greatest during peak months.

Environmental groups say formal licensing could help protect nesting seabirds and intertidal habitats without banning activity outright, provided rules are responsive to seasonal patterns. Operators, meanwhile, stress the sector’s local economic value and want timelines that allow them to plan staff and bookings.

Consultation and pilot work are expected before any full roll‑out. The test will be whether a system designed for busy coves and headlands can remain light‑touch, fair and workable on changeable coasts—supporting both conservation goals and a home‑grown industry that has become part of Pembrokeshire’s identity.
