Greece to ban social media for under‑15s from next year

Athens joins a growing European cohort tightening youth online access over mental‑health and safety concerns. Platforms will need to adapt age‑assurance and parental controls ahead of enforcement.

Greece will prohibit under‑15s from using social media from next year, part of a drive to address youth mental‑health and online safety harms. The move adds a national layer to EU‑wide duties on platforms, placing the onus on companies to keep younger users out and to provide stronger protections for older teens.

Athens joins a cohort of European governments tightening youth access. France and Spain have advanced age‑limit and parental‑consent regimes, and several countries are exploring curfews or default restrictions for minors. The EU’s Digital Services Act already requires the largest platforms to assess and mitigate risks to children, but leaves age‑gating specifics to national authorities.

Enforcement will hinge on age‑assurance. Platforms are being pushed towards privacy‑preserving checks that are accurate enough to be meaningful while avoiding the collection of sensitive identity data. That balance is difficult in practice, and cross‑border services must reconcile different national thresholds inside the single market.

Industry will need to rework sign‑up flows, tighten parental controls and expand teen‑specific experiences. Pushback is likely on free‑expression and proportionality grounds, and civil society groups warn that bans can exclude vulnerable young people from support networks or drive them to unsupervised corners of the internet.

Families and schools will be drawn into the policy’s practicalities. As with other age‑limits, workarounds—from shared devices to borrowed credentials—are common. Policymakers are therefore coupling restrictions with digital‑literacy programmes and guidance for parents, aiming to reduce harm rather than chase perfect compliance.

Key details to watch include the definition of covered services, penalties for non‑compliance, and how Greek rules will interact with EU enforcement of child‑safety and privacy law. Early metrics on effectiveness, transparency reports from platforms, and any court challenges will indicate whether the policy becomes a European template or a cautionary tale.
