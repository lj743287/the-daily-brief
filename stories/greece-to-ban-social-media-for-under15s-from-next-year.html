Greece to ban social media for under‑15s from next year

Athens will prohibit under‑15s from using social networks and introduce controls for older teens, joining European peers moving to curb online harms. The step will test enforcement, verification tools and platform compliance.

Greece will prohibit under‑15s from using social networks from next year and introduce tighter controls for older teenagers, joining a group of European countries moving to curb online harms, according to the government’s plans reported by national and international media.

Athens frames the step as a child‑protection measure targeting exposure to abuse, harassment and addictive design, and as a response to concerns about the mental health impacts of heavy social media use. It follows initiatives in France and Spain, which have pressed platforms and app stores to harden age checks and parental oversight.

Enforcement will hinge on verification. Robust age assurance without collecting excessive personal data remains a technical and legal challenge, and past experience suggests under‑age users can slip through self‑declaration systems or repurposed ID checks. The policy will test whether platforms can deploy more privacy‑preserving, reliable methods at scale.

Platforms operating in the EU already face obligations under the Digital Services Act to assess and mitigate risks to minors, and Greece’s move is likely to intensify demands for stricter default settings, parental tools and content controls. Legal debate is expected over proportionality, privacy and the feasibility of blanket prohibitions where services are available across borders.

The announcement comes amid wider restrictions on phone use in schools and growing political attention to the design of feeds, notifications and recommender systems. While research on causality between social media and adolescent mental health is contested, European policymakers have shifted towards precautionary interventions for younger users.

Detailed legislation and secondary rules, including penalties, exemptions and the role of app stores, are still to be set out. As timelines firm up, the rollout will reveal whether coordinated verification across platforms and devices is possible, and whether national curbs can meaningfully reduce under‑age use without driving it to harder‑to‑supervise corners of the internet.
