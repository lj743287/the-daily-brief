UK farmers warn food costs will stay high even if Hormuz reopens

Producers say input costs are already locked in, meaning supermarket prices will reflect the energy shock for months. The warning highlights food inflation’s persistence risk.

UK producers say supermarket prices are likely to reflect the recent energy shock for months, as key inputs have already been contracted at higher levels.

Diesel, fertiliser, animal feed and greenhouse heating costs agreed earlier in the year are set to roll through supply agreements even if Gulf shipping improves soon.

Processors and hauliers face fuel surcharges and tighter delivery schedules, forcing choices between margin compression and further price increases down the chain.

Weather setbacks and labour availability continue to weigh on yields and harvest timing, with energy‑intensive horticulture particularly exposed to sustained high utility bills.

Retailers are using fixed‑price deals and promotions to smooth volatility, but warn scope is limited until elevated costs unwind from existing contracts.

Policy options include targeted support for energy‑intensive glasshouses and logistics, yet the near‑term outlook suggests food inflation risks remain persistent.
