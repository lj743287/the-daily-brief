UK farmers warn Iran ceasefire too late to stop higher food costs

Farmers say the conflict has already fed through to energy, freight and fertiliser costs, limiting any quick relief for food prices.

UK farmers say the Iran conflict has already pushed up the costs of energy, freight and fertiliser, meaning any ceasefire is unlikely to bring immediate relief to food prices. The warning reflects the way global shocks work through agriculture long after the headlines move on.

Farming is energy-intensive and heavily exposed to transport and input prices. When oil and gas become more expensive, so do haulage, packaging and fertiliser, and those costs can take time to unwind even if the broader crisis eases.

That leaves consumers with another reminder that food prices often respond slowly to geopolitical events. A ceasefire may calm markets, but it cannot quickly reverse the bills that have already been passed along the supply chain.
