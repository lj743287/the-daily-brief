Jo Malone hopes ‘sense will prevail’ in brand-name lawsuit

Estée Lauder’s challenge to a Zara collaboration highlights the complexities of personal brands post-sale. The dispute underscores how IP rights and non-compete terms shape career moves in beauty and fashion.

Estée Lauder has filed legal action against British perfumer Jo Malone and Spanish retailer Zara over a fragrance collaboration, escalating a long-simmering tension between the owner of the Jo Malone London brand and its original founder. Malone has said she hopes common sense will prevail, as the case tests how far a creator can trade on their personal reputation after selling the rights to their name.

The dispute turns on intellectual property that changed hands when Estée Lauder acquired the Jo Malone business years ago. While Malone left and later founded a separate company, Jo Loves, Estée Lauder retains control of the Jo Malone trademarks and the commercial use of her name in beauty categories. That division—between a person’s identity and a corporate brand—regularly causes friction when founders re-enter adjacent markets.

In the current case, Estée Lauder argues that the Zara tie-up risks confusing consumers and diluting the distinctiveness of the Jo Malone London brand. Any defence is likely to emphasise that the collaboration was clearly labelled under different branding and that references to Malone related to her authorship rather than to the Estée Lauder-owned mark. The court will have to balance trademark rights and passing-off risks against legitimate descriptive use of an individual’s name.

The controversy echoes other fashion and beauty battles involving eponymous founders who sold their labels—high-profile cases that have led to careful disclaimers and strict licensing boundaries. In practice, outcomes often hinge on how products look on shelf, the prominence of house marks, and whether ordinary shoppers would reasonably believe there is a connection to the original, sold brand.

For Estée Lauder, the stakes are brand equity and the clarity of a global portfolio built on recognisable names. For Zara, the case touches a fast-growing push into beauty and fragrance, where collaborations can confer instant credibility. For creators, the episode is a reminder that non-compete clauses expire but trademark ownership does not, shaping the scope of future partnerships and how they are messaged to consumers.

Next steps could include an injunction hearing, negotiations over labelling and marketing, or a broader settlement that defines how Malone’s name can be referenced in commerce. However it resolves, the case will be closely watched across the sector, setting a practical line on how personal fame can be leveraged after a brand sale—and what retailers must do to avoid crossing into another company’s intellectual property.
