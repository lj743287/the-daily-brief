Tankers urged not to pay new Iranian levies for Hormuz passage

Industry groups advised operators to avoid any tolls sought by Iran amid legal and sanctions risks. The guidance highlights a fraught compliance backdrop that could further thin supply if shipowners decide to wait out clarity.

Tanker operators have been urged by leading maritime industry groups to refuse any new levies sought by Iran for transiting the Strait of Hormuz, amid warnings that payments could breach sanctions and invalidate insurance. The guidance underscores a fraught compliance backdrop in one of the world’s most critical energy corridors, raising the prospect of shipowners pausing voyages until legal clarity emerges.

Advisories circulated to shipowners and charterers emphasise that any tolls demanded by Iranian authorities could expose payers to US, UK and EU sanctions risks if funds are channelled to designated entities. Protection-and-indemnity insurers and war risk underwriters have also cautioned that unauthorised payments may jeopardise cover, potentially leaving vessels and cargoes uninsured in the event of detention, collision or pollution incidents.

The legal footing for unilateral charges in international straits is contested. Under widely observed principles of transit passage, coastal states are not permitted to levy transit tolls, although pilotage and port dues can be charged in certain circumstances. Iran, which borders the chokepoint alongside Oman, has previously taken a restrictive view of navigation rights. Any new “fee” framed as a safety or administrative charge would be scrutinised closely by flag states and maritime lawyers, with operators caught between compliance obligations and operational imperatives.

The immediate market question is whether the advisory chills available tonnage. Some owners are expected to seek enhanced indemnities, widen laycans or postpone fixtures rather than risk breaching sanctions or losing cover. War risk premiums and spot freight rates for Middle East loadings could remain elevated, while refiners in Asia and Europe weigh the likelihood of delays against limited alternatives such as regional pipelines and non‑Gulf loadings, which add time and cost and cannot fully substitute Hormuz flows.

Security considerations remain central. Iran has previously detained foreign‑flagged tankers during periods of heightened tension, and naval patrols by outside powers have expanded at times to deter interference with shipping. If operators decline to pay and Iranian agencies attempt enforcement, the risk of standoffs at sea would rise. Western governments are expected to update maritime advisories, with the possibility of fresh designations or guidance designed to ring‑fence the financial channels linked to any levy.

Attention now turns to formal positions from flag administrations, underwriters and the International Maritime Organization, as well as any statement from Oman on the status of transits. Regulators could publish clarifications or FAQs that shape how charter parties are drafted, what banks will process, and whether insurers tighten terms further. Early signals to watch include changes in fixture volumes out of the Gulf, demurrage claims and crude differentials for prompt cargoes—all of which will indicate whether legal caution or commercial urgency sets the pace of global energy flows through Hormuz in the days ahead.
