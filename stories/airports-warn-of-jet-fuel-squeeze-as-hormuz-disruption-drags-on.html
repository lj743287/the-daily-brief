Airports warn of jet fuel squeeze as Hormuz disruption drags on

Europe’s airport operators have raised alarm over jet fuel availability if the Strait of Hormuz remains closed, highlighting an immediate operational risk for carriers and hubs. With supplies from Gulf refineries curtailed and rerouting options slow to materialise, contingency plans are being drawn up. Any prolonged disruption is likely to add costs for airlines and, ultimately, passengers.

Airports Council International (ACI) Europe said members were increasingly concerned about jet fuel supplies, pointing to the strait’s pivotal role in global energy flows. Stocks at major hubs can buffer short disruptions, but planners are preparing for tighter rationing if alternative deliveries fail to keep pace.

The aviation fuel supply chain is finely tuned, with a significant share sourced or transiting through Gulf facilities. Rerouting via longer sea lanes or alternative suppliers carries lead times and higher freight costs, while pipeline and storage capacity in Europe can become bottlenecks when supply is constrained.

Airlines are responding with a familiar toolkit: fuel tankering where safety and weight limits permit, additional refuelling stops on long-haul routes, and payload adjustments that can restrict cargo. Each step imposes operational trade-offs that ripple through schedules and on-time performance.

Higher energy costs typically feed into fares through surcharges or reduced capacity growth. While carriers with robust hedging programmes will have partial protection in the near term, hedges roll off, and smaller or newer airlines may have less cover against sudden spikes.

Regulators and governments are in contact with operators about releasing strategic reserves and easing logistics bottlenecks if needed. Environmental obligations remain in view, but policymakers face difficult choices if keeping flights moving requires prioritising fossil fuel deliveries over other uses.

Airport operators are revisiting supply contracts and allocation frameworks to balance equity among carriers if rationing intensifies. Ground operations, from de-icing to ancillary power, also depend on reliable fuel and energy inputs, adding to the overall resilience challenge.

Exposure is uneven across the industry. Cargo operators and long-haul heavyweights are particularly sensitive to fuel constraints, while point-to-point low-cost carriers could see outsized impacts at secondary airports with thinner storage buffers and fewer alternative suppliers.

The ultimate impact hinges on duration. A short disruption can be managed with inventories and scheduling tweaks; a longer one risks capacity reductions and higher fares across key markets. For a sector still rebuilding balance sheets, elevated fuel volatility is an unwelcome test of resilience.
