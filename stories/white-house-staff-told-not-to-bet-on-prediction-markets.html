White House staff told not to bet on prediction markets

Officials were warned off wagering on politics and conflicts as such platforms grow. The guidance points to ethical and market-manipulation concerns at the intersection of finance, tech and public service.

White House staff have been told not to place bets on prediction markets, particularly on politics and conflicts, as the platforms surge in popularity. The move reflects rising concern over ethics and the risk of market manipulation at the junction of finance, technology and public service.

The guidance reminds officials that they hold sensitive information and must avoid real or perceived conflicts of interest. Wagering on outcomes that officials might influence risks breaching standards and undermining trust in government. It also points to the rapid expansion of online venues, including crypto‑linked sites, where bets on elections, policy decisions and geopolitical flashpoints are increasingly common.

Prediction markets occupy a grey zone between gambling and financial derivatives. In the US, regulators have repeatedly scrutinised contracts tied to political outcomes, and some operators have restricted American users. At the same time, mainstream investors and companies track these odds more closely as inputs to trading and risk management, lending financial heft to what were once niche indicators.

The business stakes are significant. For firms exposed to policy risk, from defence to energy, market prices on these platforms can shape expectations and investment decisions. If officials were found to be trading on or steering such markets, it would raise insider‑information concerns akin to those policed in securities. Recent controversies in other countries over figures betting on election‑related events underline how quickly such behaviour can erode confidence and distract from governance.

Supporters of prediction markets contend that real‑money prices aggregate dispersed information more efficiently than polls or punditry, offering clearer signals for decision‑makers and the public. Critics counter that normalising speculation on wars and disasters is distasteful and risks perverse incentives, especially where participants have influence. Within government, even the appearance of private gain from public duties is typically deemed unacceptable, sharpening the case for strict guardrails.

The White House stance is likely to be reflected in updated ethics training across agencies and could prompt tighter access controls on work devices and networks. Market operators may face added pressure to verify users and curb politically sensitive contracts, while US regulators continue to define the boundaries of permissible event betting. Other governments and international bodies, confronted with the same growth, may follow with their own guidance, shaping an increasingly fragmented global rulebook.
