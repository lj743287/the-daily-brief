White House staff told not to bet on prediction markets

Officials were warned off wagering on geopolitics and policy outcomes amid the growth of online prediction platforms. The guidance reflects conflict‑of‑interest risks and concerns about market manipulation.

White House staff have been instructed not to place wagers on online prediction markets, amid concerns about conflicts of interest and the risk of manipulation on platforms that allow bets on geopolitical events and policy outcomes.

The guidance follows rapid growth in real-money markets where users stake funds on topics ranging from elections to war developments, drawing scrutiny as some traders seek to monetise real-time news flows. A Guardian investigation recently highlighted how market positions can interact with narratives on conflicts, raising ethical questions for public officials.

US federal ethics rules already bar employees from using non-public information for private gain, and agencies regularly warn staff against outside activities that could undermine impartiality. Separately, the Commodity Futures Trading Commission regulates event contracts and has previously taken enforcement action against unregistered platforms offering such markets to US residents.

Officials worry that staff with access to information or policy levers could have an unfair advantage, or that the mere perception of trading on inside knowledge could erode trust. There is also concern that public statements or decisions might be seen as intended to move market prices rather than serve the public interest.

Platforms have said they are expanding compliance measures and dispute-resolution processes, and some operate outside the US or restrict certain markets. The White House guidance does not directly change the legal status of prediction markets, but it signals a tightening of expectations for federal employees at a time when liquidity and attention around such venues are increasing.

Further clarity may come from the CFTC’s ongoing stance on political-event contracts and from Congress, where the broader role of prediction markets in price discovery versus speculation is being debated. For now, the directive sets a clear boundary for government staff while regulators and courts determine which kinds of event-based trading can lawfully operate in the US.
