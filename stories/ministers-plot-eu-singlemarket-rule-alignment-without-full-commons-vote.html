Ministers plot EU single‑market rule alignment without full Commons vote

A proposed UK–EU reset bill would allow dynamic alignment with EU rules where deemed in the national interest. The plan is set to face opposition from Brexit hardliners but reflects a push for closer ties after the Iran war’s economic shock.

Ministers are preparing legislation that would allow the UK to align dynamically with elements of European Union single‑market rules where this is judged to be in the national interest, according to reports in the Guardian.

Under the plan, ministers could update domestic regulations to mirror EU changes without a full vote in the House of Commons, with streamlined scrutiny intended to reduce trade friction and provide regulatory predictability.

The move reflects a broader push for closer ties with European partners after the economic shock linked to the war in Iran, which has sharpened concerns over supply chains, energy security and inflationary pressures.

Brexit hardliners are expected to oppose the approach, arguing it would dilute sovereignty and re‑entangle the UK in Brussels’ rule‑making. Supporters inside government present it as pragmatic alignment to support growth and competitiveness.

Business groups are likely to focus on how far any alignment could simplify conformity assessments and border checks across sectors such as manufacturing and services, while watchdogs will examine the limits and accountability of delegated powers.

Any EU–UK reset bill would face a demanding passage through Parliament and the House of Lords, with committees expected to probe safeguards, reporting requirements and the criteria used to determine the ‘national interest’.
