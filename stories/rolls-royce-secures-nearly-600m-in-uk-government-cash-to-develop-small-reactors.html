Rolls-Royce secures nearly £600m in UK government cash to develop small reactors

Rolls-Royce has secured up to £599m from Britain’s national wealth fund to accelerate small modular reactors at Wylfa on Anglesey, a key test of the UK’s nuclear industrial strategy.

Rolls-Royce has secured up to £599m from Britain’s national wealth fund to help develop small modular reactors at Wylfa on Anglesey. The deal is a significant moment for the company’s nuclear ambitions and for the government’s wider attempt to revive a domestic nuclear supply chain.

Small modular reactors are being presented as a way to build nuclear power more quickly and flexibly than with traditional large-scale plants. Supporters say they could provide cleaner, more reliable power and create industrial jobs; sceptics note that the technology is still unproven at scale and faces technical, financial and regulatory hurdles.

Wylfa has long symbolised Britain’s stop-start approach to nuclear development. Backing the site again suggests ministers are willing to bet that public capital can help push the project beyond the planning stage. If it works, it could strengthen the case for a new generation of British nuclear manufacturing.

But the sums involved also underline the risks. Public money can accelerate development, yet it cannot remove the possibility of delay or cost overruns. The success of this investment will depend on whether Rolls-Royce can convert political support into deployable reactors on time and at a price the state can live with.
