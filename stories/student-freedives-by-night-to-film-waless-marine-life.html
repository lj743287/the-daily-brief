Wales Feature

Student freedives by night to film Wales’s marine life

A young filmmaker documents nocturnal biodiversity off the Welsh coast, highlighting fragile habitats. The project aims to build public interest in marine protection.

A Welsh student is documenting the country’s nocturnal seas by freediving after dark, using low‑light cameras to reveal behaviours that day‑time swimmers rarely witness. The project focuses on inshore reefs and kelp forests where much of the action begins once the sun has set.

Night dives have captured crustaceans on the move, cuttlefish hunting in the shallows and anemones fully extended to feed. In late spring and summer, bioluminescent plankton can lend a ghostly glow to disturbed water, offering striking footage and a way to engage new audiences with local marine life.

Filming without scuba tanks demands careful planning: short, quiet dives minimise disturbance and allow closer approaches to shy species. The student works with experienced safety cover and shore support, choosing sites with gentle entries and exits and avoiding strong currents or swell.

Many of the sequences are filmed within or adjacent to marine protected areas off Pembrokeshire and the Llŷn Peninsula. The emphasis is on showing the richness of familiar bays and coves, and on how small actions—like anchoring carefully and avoiding trampling eelgrass—can protect fragile habitats.

The project has already fed material to community screenings and schools, with a view to building pride in local waters as well as support for protection measures. Short clips on social media have reached audiences who might never have considered Welsh seas a destination in their own right.

As the work continues, the diver plans to partner with conservation groups to monitor seasonal events such as spider crab gatherings and cuttlefish breeding. The hope is that vivid, place‑based storytelling can help turn abstract debates about marine protection into a shared sense of stewardship.
