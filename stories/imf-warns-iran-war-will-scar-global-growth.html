IMF warns Iran war will scar global growth

Kristalina Georgieva cautioned that even a durable peace would leave lasting economic damage. The Fund sees a downgrade to global growth and a persistent hit to living standards from supply shocks and fragmentation.

The International Monetary Fund has warned that the conflict involving Iran will leave lasting marks on the world economy even if a durable peace takes hold. Managing director Kristalina Georgieva has cautioned that supply shocks, higher risk premia and renewed fragmentation will weigh on the Fund’s growth outlook and on living standards.

The channels of scarring are broad. Elevated energy and shipping costs deter investment, complicate inventory management and force companies to reconfigure supply chains. Financial conditions tend to tighten when uncertainty rises, raising the hurdle rate for new projects and stretching corporate and sovereign balance sheets.

Emerging markets with high energy import dependence are most vulnerable in the near term, facing balance‑of‑payments stress and food price pressures. Countries already grappling with climate shocks and fragile fiscal positions have less room to absorb volatility, raising the risk of slower growth and higher poverty rates.

Advanced economies are not immune. Persistently higher headline inflation from energy can keep central banks cautious for longer, while the green transition may become more expensive if critical mineral supply chains and project financing are disrupted. Prolonged uncertainty also depresses cross‑border investment and trade.

Debt dynamics are a growing concern. Higher rates and slower nominal growth complicate restructurings in lower‑income and frontier markets, where common frameworks have moved slowly. The IMF is likely to press for quicker creditor coordination and targeted liquidity support to prevent avoidable crises.

Attention now turns to the Fund’s forthcoming forecasts and to G20 and IMFC meetings for signs of coordinated policy support. Shipping security, sanctions enforcement and energy market management will shape the trajectory, while sovereign spreads and capital flows offer early signals of how deeply the scars may run.
