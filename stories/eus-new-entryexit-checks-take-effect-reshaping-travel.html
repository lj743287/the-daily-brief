EU’s new Entry/Exit checks take effect, reshaping travel

The fingerprint and photo system alters border processing for UK and other non-EU passengers. Airports, rail and ferry operators face operational tests as the scheme beds in ahead of peak travel periods.

The EU’s long-delayed Entry/Exit System has taken effect, replacing passport stamping with biometric checks for UK and other non‑EU passengers entering the Schengen area. The change, which applies to travel to 29 European countries, marks a significant overhaul of Europe’s border processing and will test the resilience of airports, rail terminals and ferry ports as the scheme beds in ahead of peak travel periods.

Under the new regime, first‑time entrants will have fingerprints and a facial image captured alongside passport details, with enrolment carried out at self‑service kiosks or by border officers. Subsequent trips will involve biometric verification rather than manual stamps. Authorities and operators are advising travellers to allow additional time while the process becomes familiar and systems are optimised.

The sharpest operational challenges are expected at juxtaposed border controls, where French checks occur on UK soil at London St Pancras, the Port of Dover and Eurotunnel in Folkestone. Operators have installed new kiosks, reconfigured queuing areas and increased staffing, but processing vehicles and foot passengers through biometric capture could slow flows at busy times. Continental airports and seaports are also re‑routing passenger streams, with airlines updating check‑in guidance and boarding cut‑offs.

For the travel industry, the stakes are commercial as well as logistical. Any sustained increase in processing times reduces terminal throughput and timetable resilience, with potential knock‑ons for aviation slots, train paths and ferry sailings. Airports, rail and ferry operators have invested in hardware and IT integration to support the roll‑out; if teething problems persist, they risk dampening short‑break demand and lifting operating costs just as the sector prepares for seasonal surges.

The scheme covers non‑EU, non‑Schengen nationals on short stays; EU citizens are unaffected, and travel to the Republic of Ireland is unchanged as it is outside Schengen. Data collected under EES are held in an EU‑wide system governed by the bloc’s privacy framework, though civil society groups have raised concerns about retention and redress mechanisms. The UK is meanwhile expanding its own electronic travel authorisation, reflecting a broader global shift towards digitised borders and advance risk screening.

Focus now turns to how quickly processing stabilises and how EES interfaces with Europe’s forthcoming pre‑travel authorisation for visa‑exempt visitors. Border agencies expect early weeks of iterative software fixes and rostering adjustments, while carriers are urging customers to check guidance, arrive earlier than usual and keep documents to hand. Performance at known choke points over the coming months will determine whether promised long‑term efficiency gains outweigh short‑term friction for passengers and operators.
