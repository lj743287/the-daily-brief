Starmer urges US and Iran to ‘find a way through’ after talks falter

The prime minister warned against escalation and pressed for a return to negotiations. The appeal aligns with European calls for restraint as energy and shipping risks mount.

Keir Starmer has urged the US and Iran to “find a way through” after talks ended without agreement, warning against escalation and calling for a return to negotiations. The prime minister’s message aligns with European appeals for restraint as diplomatic channels strain under rising regional tension.

The stakes are global. Shipping through the Strait of Hormuz remains vulnerable to disruption, with oil and insurance markets sensitive to any sign of renewed confrontation. Higher energy costs would feed through to inflation and growth prospects in Europe and beyond, adding to a fragile outlook.

UK officials are working with European partners and Gulf states to keep lines of communication open and to shape maritime security responses that lower risks rather than raise them. London has consistently framed its position as backing de‑escalation and the resumption of talks to contain the crisis.

According to the Guardian, the UK does not intend to take part in any US‑led blockade of Hormuz floated by Donald Trump, though it is willing to consider roles such as mine‑removal that are distinct from a blockade posture. Such a stance reflects an attempt to narrow the UK’s military footprint to defensive, de‑risking tasks while keeping diplomatic pressure focused on negotiation.

Downing Street has also stressed a legal distinction between facilitating defensive and offensive operations, a line that becomes harder to maintain as rhetoric hardens and scenarios on the water blur. That framing nevertheless speaks to a domestic consensus that any UK involvement must be limited, lawful and aimed at reducing rather than inflaming the crisis.

Attention now turns to whether back‑channel talks can be revived, how regional partners calibrate their own security measures, and what signals commodity markets send in the coming days. In Westminster, ministers are likely to face questions on contingency planning for UK shipping and energy security, as well as on the thresholds that would govern any future military support.
