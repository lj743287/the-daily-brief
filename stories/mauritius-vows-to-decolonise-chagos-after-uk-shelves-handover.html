Mauritius vows to ‘decolonise’ Chagos after UK shelves handover

Mauritius pledged to intensify efforts to regain control of the Chagos Islands after the UK paused enabling legislation. The dispute, long backed by UN resolutions and international legal opinions favouring Mauritius, now hinges on geopolitical calculations involving the US base on Diego Garcia.

Mauritius has vowed to step up its push to “decolonise” the Chagos Islands after the UK paused enabling legislation seen as a step towards a transfer of control. The decision injects fresh uncertainty into decades-long efforts to settle the status of the Indian Ocean archipelago, administered by London as the British Indian Ocean Territory and home to the US military facility on Diego Garcia.

The legal and diplomatic backdrop largely favours Port Louis. In 2019 the International Court of Justice advised that the UK should end its administration of the islands, and the UN General Assembly urged a withdrawal within months. Subsequent international rulings have treated Mauritius as the coastal state for related maritime matters. The UK has maintained that it recognises the sensitivity of the case and has been engaged in talks with Mauritius, but the shelving of legislation suggests negotiations have encountered political or security headwinds.

At the heart of the impasse is Diego Garcia, a critical US base established under a 1966 UK–US agreement that was extended to 2036. Washington’s priority is unbroken, secure access; Mauritian officials have repeatedly signalled they would preserve the base under a long-term lease if sovereignty is recognised. London’s caution is widely read as an attempt to lock in allied assurances and legal protections for the facility at a time of heightened Indo-Pacific competition, balancing international law arguments with defence imperatives.

The human stakes remain acute for the displaced Chagossian community, removed in the late 1960s and 1970s to make way for the base and now scattered across Mauritius, the Seychelles and the UK. While compensation schemes and expanded citizenship routes have addressed parts of their claims, many continue to demand a right of return and a say over the islands’ future. UK officials have raised environmental and infrastructure constraints to permanent resettlement; Mauritius says sustainable return is feasible and should proceed alongside decolonisation.

Regional dynamics add pressure. India has backed Mauritius’s position, and the African Union frames the dispute as an unfinished chapter of decolonisation. Control of the archipelago touches shipping lanes, surveillance of the Indian Ocean and undersea cables, while previous British moves such as the creation of a marine protected area were found by an international tribunal to have breached Mauritius’s rights. For the UK, the case tests its claims to uphold a rules-based order; for small states, it is a barometer of whether international legal opinions carry practical force.

Attention now turns to whether the pause signals a tactical delay or a broader rethink in London. Mauritius is likely to intensify lobbying at the UN and in allied capitals, and could explore further legal avenues or interim management arrangements that preserve US access while asserting sovereignty. The UK faces choices over when to revisit legislation, how to structure any tripartite guarantees with the US and Mauritius, and whether to advance concrete frameworks for Chagossian resettlement. Signals to watch include a restart of formal talks, clarifications on the base’s legal terms beyond 2036, and any joint measures on fisheries, conservation and policing around the archipelago.
