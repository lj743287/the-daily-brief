Will UK petrol and diesel prices start to fall?

Retail prices rose with the conflict but could ease if the ceasefire holds and flows normalise. Any relief will likely lag wholesale moves, and volatility remains elevated.

UK pump prices climbed during the conflict but could ease if the ceasefire endures and energy flows normalise, the BBC reports. Any relief will be uneven and delayed, reflecting how retail prices lag wholesale benchmarks and how logistics adjust after a shock.

The price build‑up matters. Retail prices reflect wholesale fuel benchmarks, duty, VAT, distribution and retail margins, alongside the cost of mandated biofuel blending. Sterling’s exchange rate against the dollar also influences import costs, so currency moves can amplify or offset changes in crude and refined product prices.

Adjustment takes time. Supermarkets often move faster than smaller independents because of buying power and turnover, but both groups typically pass through falls more slowly than rises. The industry argues this reflects stock replacement costs and volatility; motoring groups and the competition regulator continue to scrutinise behaviour.

Variation across the UK is normal. Motorway sites and rural forecourts tend to price higher due to operating costs and lower volumes, while competitive urban areas move more quickly with wholesale shifts. Temporary local tightness in supply can create short‑lived divergences from national trends.

The outlook is volatile. If secure shipping corridors through Hormuz restore confidence and if sterling holds firm, wholesale prices should soften, allowing gradual declines at the pump. Conversely, persistent disruption or refinery outages could keep prices elevated into the summer driving season.

Signals to watch include wholesale petrol and diesel benchmarks, refinery maintenance schedules, competition authority updates, and any government decisions on fuel duty. Consumers’ experience will depend as much on local market dynamics as on headline moves in crude.
