After Iran talks falter, the big question is what happens next?

Diplomatic efforts to steady relations with Tehran have stalled, with negotiators reporting limited progress and no clear pathway to a comprehensive de‑escalation. The impasse has prompted a fresh round of contingency planning in Western capitals and the Gulf, where policymakers are bracing for a drawn‑out period of risk.

The focus has swung to the Strait of Hormuz, the chokepoint through which a significant share of the world’s oil and refined products travel. The EU airline industry has warned of potential fuel shortages if the strait remains effectively closed, while shipping bodies are urging tanker operators not to pay any tolls demanded by Iran for transit, arguing that compliance could entrench an unstable precedent.

In London, Sir Keir Starmer urged Washington and Tehran to 'find a way through', framing the moment as a test of diplomatic stamina and restraint. Officials across allied governments are weighing narrowly tailored pressure and incentives aimed at reducing the temperature while maintaining the credibility of sanctions regimes.

Markets and insurers are already recalibrating exposure to regional routes, with traders watching for signs of tightness in jet fuel and shipping premiums. The coming days are likely to determine whether back‑channel contacts can reopen space for compromise or if the stand‑off hardens into a summer of disruption.
