After Iran talks falter, the big question is what happens next?

With efforts to calm tensions with Iran stalling, attention is shifting to the immediate risks and the narrow diplomatic path available to avoid escalation. The stakes are high, not only for Washington and Tehran, but for regional capitals and global markets exposed to any miscalculation.

The Strait of Hormuz remains the critical pressure point. The EU airline industry has warned of potential jet fuel shortages if the waterway stays closed or restricted, and carriers are already drawing up contingency plans to manage supply and routing risks. Refiners and traders are assessing alternative flows, conscious that even short‑lived disruption can ripple across supply chains.

On the water, shipowners and charterers have been urged not to pay any Iranian tolls for passage, underscoring a fraught legal and commercial landscape. Insurers are scrutinising cover, war‑risk premiums are in focus, and energy markets are volatile as participants game out scenarios from prolonged disruption to a fragile reopening.

Seeking to steady nerves, political leaders have called for restraint and a route back to talks. Labour leader Keir Starmer urged the United States and Iran to find a way through, echoing wider appeals for de‑escalation. For now, back‑channel contacts and shuttle diplomacy appear the most viable tools to lower the temperature and reopen space for substantive dialogue.
