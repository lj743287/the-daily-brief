After Iran talks falter, the big question is what happens next?

The latest effort to ease tensions with Tehran has run aground, leaving a dangerous vacuum around the world’s most vital oil and fuel corridor. With the diplomatic track stalling, attention has turned to the Strait of Hormuz, where any prolonged disruption would reverberate through energy and transport markets.

Industry bodies in Europe warn that a sustained closure could trigger jet fuel shortages and force carriers to ration or reroute operations. Shipping groups, meanwhile, are being urged not to pay any transit tolls demanded by Iran, a stance that raises complex legal, sanctions and insurance questions for operators assessing risk.

Energy traders are already gaming out contingencies, from longer voyages around Africa to accelerated stockpiling, all of which would increase costs and travel times. Even partial interruptions can ripple quickly into higher fares, pricier freight and renewed pressure on inflation forecasts.

Political calls for restraint are growing, with Starmer urging Washington and Tehran to find a way through before miscalculation hardens into crisis. The next inflection points to watch include back‑channel talks, potential convoy protections and whether any narrow, temporary reopening can stabilise markets long enough for diplomacy to resume.
