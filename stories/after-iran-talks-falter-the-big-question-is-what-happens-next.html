The talks ended without a breakthrough, leaving the ceasefire fragile and the wider confrontation unresolved. For now, both sides appear to have stepped back from immediate escalation, but neither has agreed the terms that would make the truce durable.

That leaves a familiar but unstable pattern: a pause that reduces the risk of instant conflict, while preserving the grievances that caused the crisis in the first place. Iran will want proof that pressure has eased; the US will want assurances that Tehran cannot simply wait out the moment and resume leverage later.

The danger is that this becomes a prolonged holding pattern, with enough restraint to avoid all-out war but not enough trust to produce a real settlement. In that scenario, shipping, energy prices and regional security would remain vulnerable to sudden shocks whenever either side tests the other's resolve.
