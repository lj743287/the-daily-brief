Wales Explainer

What does the Senedd do?

Ahead of the next election, a primer on how Wales’ parliament makes laws, spends money and scrutinises ministers. Understanding its remit is key to evaluating manifestos and accountability.

The Senedd is Wales’ parliament. It makes laws on devolved matters, approves the budget that funds public services, and holds ministers to account. As the next election approaches, understanding its remit helps voters judge which promises can credibly be delivered from Cardiff Bay and which depend on cooperation with Westminster or local authorities. It also clarifies how power is shared across the UK, where Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland have distinct institutions within a single state.

On law‑making, the Senedd can pass Acts in areas devolved to Wales, including health and social care, education, local government, the environment, agriculture, culture and the Welsh language, economic development and most transport within Wales. Policing and justice, foreign affairs, defence, most welfare benefits and macro‑tax policy remain reserved to the UK Parliament. When Westminster legislates in devolved fields, the Senedd is asked for consent under the Sewel convention; it can refuse, though the convention is political rather than legally binding. Senedd Acts require Royal Assent before becoming law.

Most of the money the Welsh Government spends is provided by a block grant from the UK Treasury, adjusted each year by the Barnett formula. On top of that, Wales raises some of its own revenue through devolved taxes and the Welsh rates of income tax, and it has limited borrowing powers for investment and cash‑flow. Ministers set out draft spending plans that the Senedd can scrutinise, amend and ultimately approve. Funding then flows to the NHS, councils, transport and other bodies that deliver services on the ground.

Scrutiny is the other core function. Members of the Senedd question the First Minister and cabinet ministers, examine proposed laws line by line, and investigate performance through subject committees and public inquiries. The Public Accounts machinery and Audit Wales track value for money, while independent commissioners and ombuds services contribute to the accountability ecosystem. The Presiding Officer ensures rules are followed, and the Senedd can summon documents and witnesses, recommend changes, or in extremis withdraw confidence in ministers.

The system itself is changing before the next election. Wales is moving to larger multi‑member constituencies and a more proportional list system, with a bigger chamber intended to strengthen scrutiny and law‑making. Constituency boundaries are being overhauled, so many voters will find themselves in new areas. The reforms aim to align the Senedd more closely with modern devolved government, though debates continue about how lists affect local representation and how best to reflect the country’s political diversity.

For voters, the key is matching manifestos to the Senedd’s powers. Pledges on the NHS, schools, buses, local roads, housing and climate policy largely sit within Cardiff Bay; promises on policing, immigration, most benefits or defence do not. Parties must also explain trade‑offs: higher spending may require higher devolved taxes, cuts elsewhere or tighter eligibility for programmes. Next term, pressure points will include post‑Brexit farm support, the cost of public services, climate and nature targets, and intergovernmental disputes where Welsh ministers seek influence without full control.
