Wales Explainer

What does the Senedd do?

As Wales approaches its seventh election since 1999, this guide sets out the parliament’s powers over health, education, transport and more. It also explains where responsibilities remain with Westminster.

The Senedd is Wales’s national parliament. Since 1999 its role has evolved from approving secondary regulations to passing primary laws and setting much of the nation’s budget. Welsh ministers are accountable to the Senedd, which scrutinises legislation, spending and delivery across devolved services.

Health and social care are fully devolved. The Welsh Government funds and runs NHS Wales, sets priorities for services, and determines policy on areas such as primary care, waiting‑time targets and public health. Social care policy, including charging frameworks and integration with the NHS, is also determined in Wales.

Education and skills policy are likewise devolved. The Curriculum for Wales, teacher training requirements, school funding frameworks and qualifications are set in Cardiff Bay. Further education, apprenticeships and most aspects of higher education funding and student support also fall within the Senedd’s remit.

Transport powers include buses, local roads and the strategic trunk road network in Wales. The Welsh Government oversees rail services through its franchise and Transport for Wales, though rail infrastructure and most rail regulation remain reserved to Westminster. Planning, housing policy and most elements of local government are devolved, including council tax policy.

Economic development, agriculture, fisheries and the environment are key Senedd responsibilities. Ministers set climate targets for Wales, design farm support schemes to replace EU programmes, regulate waste and recycling, and manage many energy consents onshore and in Welsh waters. Culture, sport and the Welsh language, including funding for national institutions and promotion of Cymraeg, are also devolved.

The UK Parliament retains control over defence, foreign affairs, immigration, most social security, policing and criminal justice, broadcasting and the macro‑economy. Wales raises some revenue through devolved and assigned taxes, including the Welsh rates of income tax and devolved land and landfill taxes, with the remainder provided via the block grant. Policy often requires cooperation between Cardiff and London where responsibilities intersect.
