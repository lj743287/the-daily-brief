Stock markets are too high and set to fall, says Bank of England deputy

A Bank of England deputy has warned that stock markets may be overvalued and vulnerable to a fall, an unusually blunt intervention from a senior monetary official. The warning lands as investors weigh inflation, interest rates and the knock-on effects of the Iran war.

A senior Bank of England deputy has warned that stock markets may be too high and vulnerable to a fall, making an unusually blunt intervention from inside the UK central bank.

The warning comes as investors are balancing inflation, interest rates and the knock-on effects of the Iran war. That mix matters because higher energy prices and geopolitical shocks can quickly change expectations about company earnings and borrowing costs.

Central bankers are generally careful not to sound alarmist about market levels, so the deputy's remarks stand out for their directness. They suggest concern that valuations may not reflect the risks now building in the global economy.

For households and pension savers, any sharper market drop could feed into confidence, retirement balances and access to credit, even if the effects are not immediate.

The Bank is unlikely to want to be seen as calling a crash, but the message is clear enough: markets may be pricing in too much optimism at a time when uncertainty remains high.

Investors will now watch whether other officials echo the warning, and whether current valuations begin to correct if conflict-driven inflation persists.
