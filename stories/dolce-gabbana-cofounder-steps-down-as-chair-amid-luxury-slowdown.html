Dolce & Gabbana co‑founder steps down as chair amid luxury slowdown

The transition comes as the brand grapples with higher debt and softer global demand for high‑end goods. Investors are watching for strategy changes to restore growth.

A co‑founder of Dolce & Gabbana has stepped down as chair, as the Italian fashion house navigates softer global demand for high‑end goods and a heavier debt burden. The change comes at a delicate point for the sector, which has cooled after a post‑pandemic surge.

Luxury spending has softened in key markets, with a slower recovery in China and more cautious aspirational consumers in the US and Europe amid higher borrowing costs and lingering inflation. Several peers have tempered guidance in recent quarters, and brands outside the very top tier have felt the pressure most acutely.

Leadership transitions in such conditions tend to prompt scrutiny of strategy. Investors will look for signals on product cadence, pricing discipline and the balance between wholesale and direct‑to‑consumer channels, as well as any shift in emphasis between couture, ready‑to‑wear and accessories, where margins can be more resilient.

Financial flexibility is in focus. Elevated leverage limits room for manoeuvre if trading remains subdued, raising the importance of cash generation, inventory control and selective investment. Management may consider tightening store networks, phasing capex or exploring monetisation of licences to strengthen the balance sheet.

Brand equity remains a central variable. Dolce & Gabbana has faced reputational challenges in the past that affected parts of its Asia business, and the current downcycle raises the bar for effective clienteling and digital engagement. Reconnecting with growth in China, while broadening demand in the Middle East and Europe, will be pivotal.

Near‑term milestones include the appointment and first moves of the new chair, any updates on debt refinancing, and the trajectory of wholesale orders into the second half. The broader outlook for luxury will hinge on the path of energy‑driven inflation, travel flows and whether the recent normalisation in demand gives way to a renewed premiumisation cycle.
