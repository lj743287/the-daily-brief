Streeting denies changing pay deal for resident doctors as strike nears end

The health secretary rejected claims of shifting terms ahead of a planned end to a six‑day walkout in England. The dispute remains a central test of the government’s NHS recovery strategy.

Wes Streeting has denied changing the terms of a proposed pay deal for resident doctors as a six‑day strike in England nears its scheduled end at 06:59 on Monday, the BBC reports. The dispute remains a pivotal test of the government’s plan to stabilise the NHS after years of industrial unrest.

The latest walkout has led to significant disruption across elective care and placed fresh pressure on urgent and emergency services, with trusts re‑prioritising capacity to protect critical and time‑sensitive treatment. Hospital leaders say restoring predictability for patients will require sustained calm in the months ahead.

Union leaders have accused ministers of shifting the goalposts during talks; the health secretary rejects that characterisation and insists the offer on the table has been consistent. Both sides say they remain open to negotiations once the strike concludes, but there is little sign yet of a bridging formula.

The context is a health service still dealing with backlogs, workforce shortages and rising demand. While agreements have been reached with other staff groups in recent months, resident doctors argue that years of below‑inflation awards and unsocial hours have eroded pay and morale, making recruitment and retention harder.

Politically, the government needs a credible path to lower waiting lists and improve access heading into a period of local and devolved elections. Prolonged industrial action risks undercutting that narrative and compounding operational challenges heading toward winter planning.

Attention will turn to whether formal talks resume this week, the scope for staged or conditional settlements, and how any package interfaces with the pay review body process. NHS England is expected to focus on rescheduling delayed care at pace, while ministers face pressure to demonstrate that negotiations are moving beyond stalemate.
