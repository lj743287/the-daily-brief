UK inflation rises after Iran war pushes up fuel prices

UK inflation has risen as the Iran war pushed up fuel prices, offering the first official sign of how Middle East conflict is feeding through to household costs. The figures raise pressure on ministers and the Bank of England as they judge the outlook for borrowing and living standards.

UK inflation has risen after the Iran war pushed up fuel prices, giving the first official indication of how the conflict is feeding into the cost of living.

The figures matter because fuel is a direct input into transport, heating and food distribution, so even a relatively narrow shock can spread quickly through household budgets and business costs.

For ministers, the rise adds pressure at a time when they are already trying to reassure voters that living standards are improving. For the Bank of England, it complicates the argument over when and how fast to cut rates.

The UK's exposure is not only economic but political. When fuel rises, the effects are felt quickly by drivers, commuters and firms that rely on transport, even if the geopolitical cause is far away.

The key uncertainty is how long the Iran war keeps energy markets elevated. If prices stay high, the impact on inflation could become more persistent rather than temporary.

That would make the next phase of policy decisions harder, with both the government and the central bank facing weaker room for manoeuvre.
