The decade‑long saga of trying to make Fury v Joshua

Repeated near‑misses, rival belts and competing networks have delayed the superfight. Even with fresh momentum, final hurdles on purse splits and timing remain.

For the better part of a decade, a Fury v Joshua super‑fight has hovered over British boxing without materialising. The obstacles have been less about desire than about timing, rights and the gravitational pull of heavyweight belts.

Their ascents ran on parallel tracks under different promoters and broadcasters, complicating efforts to synchronise schedules. Each man pursued his own path to titles and global markets, narrowing the overlap where a fight could land cleanly.

A series of derailments hardened the stalemate. Arbitration steered Fury back to a trilogy with Deontay Wilder, while Oleksandr Usyk’s emergence upended Joshua’s route, bringing rematches and mandatories that absorbed prime calendar slots.

Commercial complexities multiplied the challenge. UK and US broadcast rights, pay‑per‑view models, site‑fee bidding and risk‑sharing structures had to align, and rival networks were not inclined to cede flagship content without hard guarantees.

Recent results and rhetoric have introduced fresh momentum, but familiar hurdles remain. Purse splits, sequencing of potential rematches and competition among prospective host cities are all live issues that can stretch talks beyond feasible dates.

The clearest indicators of progress will be quiet: formalised broadcaster cooperation, confirmed sanctioning body positions and a locked‑in venue. Until those appear, confident declarations are best read as negotiating pressure rather than proof of a breakthrough.
