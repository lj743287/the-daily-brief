UK farmers say Iran ceasefire may be too late to prevent higher food costs

Producers warn of lingering input-price pressures even if energy markets stabilise, pointing to fertiliser and transport costs. That raises the risk of renewed food inflation as supermarkets and suppliers haggle over contracts.

UK farmers and food suppliers say that even if a ceasefire involving Iran were to calm global energy markets, it may come too late to prevent a fresh round of price pressures working through the grocery aisle. Their concern is that costs already locked into fertiliser, fuel and haulage over recent months will filter into contracts now being hammered out with supermarkets, reviving food inflation just as households have started to feel some relief.

Producers point to a familiar chain of transmission: fertiliser, made using natural gas, rose in step with heightened Middle East tensions and remains embedded in farm budgets for the current growing season; diesel for tractors and refrigerated transport has been volatile; and insurance and rerouting for shipping through sensitive waterways have added to freight bills. Even if oil and gas prices stabilise on a truce, many of these increases have already been incurred, they argue.

There is some countervailing evidence. Wholesale energy benchmarks have eased from last year’s extremes, and the rate of food price inflation in the UK has been slowing for months. Some analysts expect a quicker pass‑through from calmer energy markets to input costs than producers allow, particularly if Europe’s ammonia plants run more consistently and global fertiliser trade normalises. But farmers say order cycles and pre‑season purchases mean the benefits will arrive with a lag, not in time to offset costs for spring and early summer output.

The timing matters because retailers and suppliers are in a new round of price negotiations. Grocers, under pressure to defend market share and maintain the pace of shelf‑price cuts, are pushing back on increases; suppliers say margins have been pared to the bone after two years of shocks from energy, feed and labour. The result could be a patchwork of outcomes: some ranges trimmed or reformulated, sharper promotional activity on staples, and stickier prices where ingredients or logistics remain tight.

The stakes extend beyond the checkout. If food prices re‑accelerate, the UK’s disinflation trend could falter, complicating the Bank of England’s path to lower interest rates and prolonging the squeeze on real incomes. The risk is heightened by the UK’s reliance on imported produce and inputs, from winter vegetables to fertiliser components, leaving the sector exposed to currency moves and global supply disruptions as well as domestic weather and labour availability.

What happens next hinges on geopolitics and growing conditions as much as on spreadsheets. A sustained de‑escalation around Iran would help temper energy and marine insurance costs; smoother Red Sea and Gulf shipping would ease freight; and steadier fertiliser plant utilisation in Europe and North Africa would filter through later in the year. Closer to home, spring weather, harvest yields and seasonal worker supply will shape output. Absent clear improvements on several fronts, farmers caution that cost pressure will keep building into autumn, even if headline energy prices settle sooner.
