Home Office to close 11 asylum hotels as part of pledge to end hotel use

The government will announce closures this week, advancing a plan to phase out hotels for asylum accommodation. Ministers face pressure to balance cost, community tensions and the speed of asylum processing.

The Home Office is to close 11 asylum hotels this week as part of a pledge to phase out hotel use for accommodating people seeking asylum, with ministers promising to end the practice by the end of the parliament.

The move is intended to reduce costs, ease community tensions around long‑term hotel placements and replace short‑term provision with more stable accommodation. Hotels became a mainstay during and after the pandemic as arrivals outpaced available housing, drawing criticism from local residents and support groups alike.

Officials say closures will be sequenced to minimise disruption, with residents moved into dispersal housing, larger dedicated sites and other forms of accommodation as capacity comes online. The strategy hinges on faster asylum decisions to shorten stays and lower demand.

The pace will be closely watched. Local authorities have warned about pressures on schooling, health services and housing stock if transfers are rushed, while charities have urged the government to avoid large‑scale moves that could separate families or interrupt access to legal advice.

Parliament and watchdogs are likely to push for transparency on the criteria used to select hotels for closure, the destinations for those moved and the effect on the asylum backlog. Ministers will also face questions about funding for councils and the safeguards in place for vulnerable people.

Further closures are expected if small‑boat arrivals remain suppressed and throughput improves, but legal challenges to large sites and seasonal fluctuations in Channel crossings could complicate the timetable. The department is due to set out additional steps on accommodation and processing in the coming weeks.
