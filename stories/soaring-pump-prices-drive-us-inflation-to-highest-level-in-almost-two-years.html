Soaring pump prices drive US inflation to highest level in almost two years

US inflation has reached 3.3% as higher pump prices linked to the Iran war feed through to the wider economy, increasing pressure on consumers and policymakers.

US inflation has risen to 3.3%, the highest level in almost two years, as soaring pump prices linked to the Iran war feed through the economy. The latest figures underline how a foreign conflict is now showing up directly in American household costs.

The immediate pressure is on drivers, but the effects do not stop there. Higher fuel prices tend to work through transport costs and supply chains, making it harder for policymakers to judge whether the inflation surge is temporary or the start of something more persistent.

For the White House and the Federal Reserve, the challenge is that the shock is both geopolitical and domestic. Officials can do little to change the oil market quickly, but they will be judged on whether they can prevent a supply shock from becoming a broader inflation problem.

The numbers also raise political risks. As with previous oil shocks, voters often feel the pain first at the pump and only later in the rest of the economy. That can quickly turn a distant war into a kitchen-table issue.
