US inflation jumps as pump prices surge

Annual inflation accelerated to its highest in almost two years, driven by energy costs linked to the Iran war. The data complicate the policy outlook as households absorb fuel spikes and expectations shift on interest‑rate cuts.

US inflation has accelerated to its highest rate in almost two years as surging fuel costs filter through to household budgets, the BBC reports. The latest data show energy as a key driver, with petrol prices reacting quickly to heightened risks around Gulf supply routes.

The pickup complicates the policy outlook. The Federal Reserve has signalled it needs more confidence that inflation is returning sustainably towards target, and a renewed energy impulse risks delaying the timing of any rate cuts. Market pricing has shifted towards a slower path of easing as bond yields remain sensitive to inflation surprises.

While petrol prices move swiftly with wholesale benchmarks, other components adjust more slowly. Transport services, freight charges and some goods categories can reflect energy pressures with a lag, potentially keeping headline readings sticky even if crude prices stabilise. Conversely, underlying measures have been cooling, albeit unevenly, in recent months.

Households face an uneven burden. Lower‑income consumers, who spend a larger share of income on fuel, utilities and groceries, feel the squeeze most quickly, while small businesses with thin margins face difficult pass‑through decisions. Airlines and logistics groups also see immediate effects, with fuel hedging only partly mitigating the shock.

The US picture sits within a wider energy backdrop. European aviation fuel markets are bracing for tightness, UK petrol and diesel remain volatile, and several Asian economies heavily reliant on Gulf oil face cost pressures. Exchange rates can amplify or cushion domestic impacts, adding an extra layer of uncertainty for central banks.

What happens next depends on maritime security in the Strait of Hormuz, OPEC+ supply signals, and any recourse to strategic reserves. Base effects later in the year may help, but a prolonged shipping disruption would keep energy‑linked inflation elevated and sustain a cautious tone in policy debates.
