Germany’s AfD adopts ‘radical’ manifesto ahead of key polls

The far‑right party sharpened its platform as it rides high in opinion polls in Saxony‑Anhalt. The move intensifies debate over migration, EU policy and democratic norms ahead of regional and national contests.

Germany’s far‑right Alternative for Germany (AfD) has adopted a sharpened manifesto as it rides high in polling in the eastern state of Saxony‑Anhalt, intensifying debate over migration, the European Union and democratic norms ahead of regional and national contests.

The platform hardens the party’s positions on immigration controls, national sovereignty and cultural issues, aiming to energise its base while courting voters disenchanted with mainstream options. Opponents warn that the programme challenges constitutional principles and Germany’s post‑war consensus.

Mainstream parties maintain a cordon sanitaire against the AfD, vowing not to enter coalitions. Whether that stance holds after regional votes will shape governance in parts of the east, where fragmented parliaments have complicated coalition arithmetic.

The party’s branches have faced scrutiny from domestic intelligence over suspected extremism, a designation that carries legal and reputational risks. How that oversight interacts with campaigning and fundraising will be watched closely by both supporters and critics.

Polls suggest the AfD continues to benefit from discontent over cost‑of‑living pressures, energy policy and migration. The question is whether a harder platform broadens its appeal beyond core constituencies or narrows it by alienating swing voters.

Upcoming ballots will test those calculations. Gains could force complex coalition talks among its rivals; setbacks would prompt soul‑searching about strategy and messaging. Either way, the manifesto sets a combative tone for a heated electoral season.
