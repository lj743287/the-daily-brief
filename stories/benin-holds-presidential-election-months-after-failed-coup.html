Benin holds presidential election months after failed coup

The vote follows a thwarted putsch and concludes a decade under President Patrice Talon, who is stepping down. Investor attention will focus on policy continuity in one of West Africa’s more stable economies and on post-election cohesion.

Benin is voting in a presidential election held just months after authorities said they foiled an attempted coup, a fraught backdrop to a poll that will conclude President Patrice Talon’s decade in office. The contest, taking place under heightened security, is viewed as a measure of the country’s ability to manage a delicate transition in a region unsettled by repeated military takeovers.

Officials have given limited details about the alleged putsch, saying they detained suspects within the security forces and opened investigations. Supporters of the government argue the episode underscores the need for vigilance in a turbulent neighbourhood. Some opposition figures and civil-society activists contend that the threat has been used to justify tighter controls, a claim the authorities reject. Independent confirmation of what transpired has been scarce.

Mr Talon’s tenure has been marked by an assertive economic reform agenda and controversy over political freedoms. Earlier electoral cycles drew criticism over the exclusion of some opposition figures and the narrowing of civic space, though subsequent legislative polls saw broader participation. This presidential race is widely viewed as a test of whether the political opening will be sustained as power changes hands.

Investors and lenders will be watching for signals of policy continuity in one of West Africa’s steadier economies. Benin has pursued infrastructure upgrades, an industrial zone strategy and port modernisation while benefiting from the currency stability of the West African monetary union. The outlook, however, is sensitive to security pressures in the north, fluctuations in cotton prices and shifts in trade with Nigeria, its largest neighbour and key market.

Regional dynamics weigh heavily. Benin borders Sahelian states that have experienced coups and rising insurgent violence, and it has increased deployments in its northern departments following attacks linked to militant groups. Election observers from regional and international bodies are expected, while rights groups say they will monitor any curbs on media or the internet, given past concerns around West African polls.

Benin’s presidency is decided by a two-round system, with provisional results expected in the coming days and a run-off if no candidate clears 50%. Attention will turn quickly to whether the losing side accepts the outcome, how the incoming administration signals its economic and security priorities, and whether regional partners see the process as reinforcing a democratic anchor on the Gulf of Guinea.
