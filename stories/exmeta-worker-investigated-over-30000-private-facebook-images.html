Ex‑Meta worker investigated over 30,000 private Facebook images

Meta said the former employee is suspected of downloading tens of thousands of user photos. The case renews questions about internal controls and data security.

A former Meta employee is under investigation for allegedly downloading tens of thousands of private Facebook images, the company said, confirming the individual had been dismissed and that law‑enforcement agencies were notified, according to the BBC.

Meta indicated the photos were accessed through misuse of internal tools, and said initial checks had not found evidence of widespread distribution. The scale nonetheless underscores the sensitivity of insider incidents at large platforms.

Despite role‑based access controls and logging, internal privileges for debugging, customer support and abuse response can create pathways to misuse. Limiting access to the minimum necessary, coupled with real‑time anomaly detection, is a persistent challenge.

Regulators may examine whether Meta’s safeguards met GDPR standards for data minimisation and security. Formal breach‑notification obligations turn on the risk posed to affected users, including the likelihood of exposure or misuse.

Users routinely store sensitive media on social networks and expect strong internal governance. Vetting, training and deterrence, alongside technical controls, are central to reducing insider risk.

Meta is likely to tighten tool permissions, expand auditing and cooperate with investigators. Any enforcement actions will depend on the facts established about intent, dissemination and jurisdictional scope.
