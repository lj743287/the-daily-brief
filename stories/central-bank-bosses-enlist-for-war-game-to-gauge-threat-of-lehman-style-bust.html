Central bank bosses enlist for war game to gauge threat of Lehman-style bust

Senior officials from the UK, US and EU will take part in a Washington exercise on Saturday to test how they would respond to the collapse of a globally significant bank. The scenario shows how concerned regulators remain about financial stability.

Top central bankers and finance ministers from the UK, US and EU are due to take part in a Washington war game on Saturday designed to test their response to the collapse of a globally significant bank. The exercise is a reminder of how seriously regulators still treat the risk of another Lehman-style shock.

The scenario is meant to expose how authorities would coordinate under pressure if a major institution began to fail. Officials involved include the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank and the Bank of England, where governor Andrew Bailey is expected to take part.

That matters because the memory of the 2008 financial crisis still shapes regulation, liquidity planning and cross-border crisis management. Even after years of tighter oversight, policymakers remain wary of hidden vulnerabilities in the global banking system.

The exercise is not a prediction of imminent trouble, but it does show that unease has returned to the top of the system. Regulators are trying to understand whether they could move quickly enough if a crisis hit.

What happens in the drill will not calm markets on its own, but it may reveal how prepared the major economies really are for a fast-moving banking failure.
