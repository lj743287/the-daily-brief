Wales Explainer

How will the new Senedd voting system work?

Wales is overhauling its electoral system ahead of next year’s Senedd election. Here’s what will change for voters, parties and constituency representation.

Wales is replacing its mixed system of constituency and regional seats with a new, more proportional model for the next Senedd election. The reform expands the chamber and changes how Members of the Senedd (MSs) are elected, with the aim of better matching seats to votes and easing workloads as policy responsibilities have grown since devolution.

Under the law passed in Cardiff Bay, the Senedd will increase from 60 to 96 members. Wales will be divided into 16 larger constituencies, each returning six MSs. Seats in each of those constituencies will be allocated proportionally using the D’Hondt formula from party lists, replacing the previous combination of first‑past‑the‑post and regional top‑ups.

For voters, the process will be simpler at the ballot box: you will cast a single vote for a party (or an independent) in your six‑member constituency. Parties submit ranked lists of candidates for each area, and seats are shared out in line with the overall vote in that constituency rather than being awarded to the single highest‑placed individual.

Because representatives are elected from lists, vacancies will be filled by the next eligible person on the party’s list rather than by a by‑election. Independents will be able to stand as individual lists, and if an independent seat becomes vacant the law sets out how it is to be treated, including circumstances where a seat may remain unfilled.

The new electoral map broadly pairs the 32 Westminster constituencies created by the recent boundary review to form the 16 Senedd constituencies. Final names and precise lines are determined by the Welsh boundary body, and the intention is to keep communities grouped sensibly while making electorates more equal across the country.

A separate bill to require gender‑balanced lists has been brought forward by the Welsh Government, but elements of that proposal have been subject to legal scrutiny over devolved competence. Parties can still choose to order their lists to improve diversity. There is no formal vote threshold, but in practice the six‑seat constituencies and D’Hondt allocation will shape how easily smaller parties and independents can gain representation.
