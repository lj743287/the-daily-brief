Wales Explainer

How will the new Senedd voting system work?

Electoral reforms reshape constituencies and representation, with implications for party strategies and voter choice. The changes aim to balance proportionality with local accountability.

Wales will use a new voting system to elect the Senedd at the next election, reshaping how power is shared in Cardiff Bay and how voters are represented locally. The overhaul is designed to produce results that more closely match parties’ vote shares while keeping a clear link between Members of the Senedd (MSs) and communities. It will also change how parties campaign and how voters make their choice, with implications for the balance of power after polling day.

The current Additional Member System — a mix of first-past-the-post constituencies and regional lists — will be replaced by a fully proportional model based on multi-member constituencies and party lists. The number of MSs will rise from 60 to 96. Wales will be divided into 16 larger constituencies, each electing six MSs. Voters will cast a single vote for a party (or an independent candidate), and seats in each constituency will be allocated to reflect the overall share of votes there.

Under the new arrangements, parties submit a ranked list of candidates for each of the 16 constituencies. When votes are counted, seats are distributed using a proportional formula commonly known as D’Hondt, which aims to translate votes into seats fairly across the six places available. Independents can stand and will be treated as their own “list”. This means there is no separate regional “top-up” and no second vote — the proportionality happens directly within each multi-member constituency.

The constituency map will change too. Instead of the current 40 single-member seats and five regions, the new 16 constituencies are larger areas that broadly pair up the new UK Parliament constituencies in Wales. That simplifies the landscape — your Senedd area should be easier to match with Westminster boundaries — but it also means bigger patches and several MSs sharing responsibility for local casework, scrutiny and community engagement.

Politically, the incentives shift. Closed party lists elevate internal selection contests, because list order can decide who is elected when a party wins multiple seats. Larger, six-seat districts tend to help smaller parties more than first-past-the-post, though there is still an effective hurdle for very small vote shares. For voters, the system reduces pressure to vote tactically for a single local winner and puts more emphasis on choosing the party whose programme they want to endorse. Personal followings still matter, but party brand and list positioning will be decisive.

Between now and polling day, expect voter education campaigns and detailed rules to be finalised on how lists are compiled and ballots conducted. Proposals intended to improve diversity — including gender balance requirements for party lists — have been advanced, though their final shape depends on legal and procedural steps. The first test will be the next Senedd election, when the new system’s promises of greater proportionality and clear local accountability will be measured against turnout, representation and how effectively multiple MSs serve their shared constituencies. Voters can prepare by checking their new constituency and understanding that they will cast one vote for a party or independent.
