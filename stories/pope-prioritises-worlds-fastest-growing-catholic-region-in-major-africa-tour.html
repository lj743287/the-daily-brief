Pope prioritises world's fastest-growing Catholic region in major Africa tour

Pope Leo XIV is heading to Africa, where the Church sees the continent as central to its future and to its growth across a region that remains strategically important for global Catholicism.

Pope Leo XIV is heading to Africa as part of a major tour aimed at drawing attention to the continent’s importance to the Catholic Church’s future. The visit reflects a long-running shift in global Catholicism: the Church is growing fastest in parts of Africa, even as its traditional centres in Europe continue to weaken.

For the Vatican, the trip is about more than symbolism. Africa has become central to the Church’s demographic future, its missionary priorities and some of its most difficult political questions, from conflict and poverty to the relationship between religion and state.

The tour will also be watched as a test of how Leo wants to define his papacy internationally. By foregrounding Africa, he is signalling that the Church’s future will not be written only in Rome or the West, but in regions where the Catholic population is expanding most quickly.

The practical challenge is that Africa is not one Catholic story but many. Different countries face different pressures, and the Pope’s message will need to balance celebration of growth with attention to the insecurity and inequality that still shape daily life for many believers.
