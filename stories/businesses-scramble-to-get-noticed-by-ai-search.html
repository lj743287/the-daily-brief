Businesses scramble to get noticed by AI search

Firms are reworking websites to feed structured data to AI assistants as traditional SEO playbooks erode. The shift could reshape customer acquisition, brand control and the value chain of search.

As AI assistants answer more queries directly, fewer users click through to the open web. That shift is forcing companies to rethink how they present information so they can be cited, sourced or integrated into AI‑generated replies—an upheaval akin to the early days of search‑engine optimisation but with different rules and gatekeepers.

The immediate response has been technical. Firms are enriching pages with structured data—schema.org markup for products, services and FAQs; machine‑readable pricing and availability; richer sitemaps—and exposing clean APIs so assistants can fetch fresh facts. Some are negotiating data‑licensing deals or feeds with aggregators to ensure their content is included and attributed.

Attribution and analytics are the pain points. When an assistant composes a summary, referral paths are opaque and brand presence can be diluted. Companies are experimenting with ways to anchor identity in machine‑readable facts, from verified profiles and product identifiers to provenance metadata, while pressing platforms to standardise citation and reporting.

The pattern of winners and losers is emerging. Sites with deep, authoritative, well‑structured content fare better than thin affiliate pages designed for traditional rankings. Local businesses may gain if assistants privilege verified, up‑to‑date feeds over directory scrape‑ups, but marketplaces can still crowd out direct‑to‑consumer brands unless those brands provide high‑quality data and post‑sale support signals.

A cottage industry of “assistant optimisation” has appeared, promising to tune content for large language models. Alongside legitimate hygiene practices sit grey‑area tactics—prompt‑injection tricks embedded in pages, or synthetic reviews—that platforms are moving to neutralise. Legal risk rises where manipulation crosses into deception or infringes data‑use terms.

The trajectory points to convergence on canonical data formats, verified product and business graphs, and signed content that assistants can trust. Regulators are watching value flows and bargaining power, with debates over fair compensation for training and for answer‑box use. Many firms are building their own retrievable knowledge stores so that, whether the query lands on a public engine or a private copilot, the answer still comes from them.
