Rights groups condemn US plan for migrant ‘camp’ at Guantánamo Bay

Dozens of organisations urged Congress to oppose proposals to house Cuban migrants at the base in any future crisis. They warned of legal and human rights risks, citing past controversies surrounding detentions at the facility.

Dozens of human rights organisations urged US lawmakers to oppose proposals to house Cuban migrants at the Guantánamo Bay naval base in any future crisis, warning of legal and human rights risks, the Guardian reported.

The groups cited the facility’s contentious legacy and concerns about due process, arguing that offshore detention could expose vulnerable people to indefinite confinement without adequate judicial oversight.

Their letter followed remarks last month by a senior defence official signalling contingency planning for a potential migrant surge, although no final policy has been announced.

Advocates pointed to 1990s precedents, when Haitians and Cubans were held at the base, as evidence that large‑scale offshore processing can result in prolonged detention and barriers to legal counsel.

Supporters of tougher measures argue that offshore holding areas may deter dangerous sea crossings, while opponents counter that the US should expand lawful pathways and bolster onshore asylum processing.

Any move to revive migrant facilities at Guantánamo would likely face court challenges and intensify debate in Congress over the scope of executive power in immigration enforcement.
