Japan raises interest rate to highest for 31 years

The Bank of Japan has lifted rates to their highest level in 31 years, as policymakers respond to inflation pressures linked to the Iran war and rising oil costs, a move that matters well beyond Tokyo because of its effect on global borrowing and markets.

The Bank of Japan has raised interest rates to their highest level in 31 years, lifting borrowing costs as policymakers respond to inflation pressures linked to the Iran war and higher oil prices. The move matters far beyond Tokyo because Japan’s policy stance influences global markets, funding conditions and expectations for other major central banks.

Japan has been edging away from near-zero rates since 2024, and this latest step suggests officials are still more concerned about persistent inflation than about the risk of tightening too quickly. That is notable in a country long defined by low inflation and ultra-loose monetary policy.

The immediate backdrop is the jump in energy costs caused by the conflict in the Middle East. Higher oil prices feed through into transport, food and business costs, so the Bank of Japan is trying to stop imported inflation becoming embedded in the broader economy.

What happens next will depend on whether oil prices keep falling after the US-Iran deal and whether domestic price pressures stay elevated. For global investors, the significance is that Japan is no longer an outlier sitting at the zero bound, but a central bank that can still move markets with every adjustment.
