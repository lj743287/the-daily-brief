Tanker firms told not to pay Iranian levies for Hormuz transits

Industry advisories urged operators to avoid any payments in exchange for safe passage, citing legal and sanctions exposure. The guidance adds another barrier to restarting normal crude and product flows.

Tanker operators have been advised not to pay any Iranian levies in exchange for safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz, according to industry guidance carried by the BBC. Advisories warn that such payments could pose legal and sanctions risks.

The rationale is twofold: potential breaches of sanctions regimes and the danger that insurers would decline cover if operators are seen to facilitate unauthorised tolls. Protection and Indemnity clubs typically set strict conditions around conflict zones and prohibited payments.

Operationally, the guidance is another reason for owners and charterers to delay restarting normal sailings. Masters face conflicting incentives between commercial obligations and safety, while charter parties may need to be amended to reflect the heightened risk environment.

Reports of alleged toll demands come amid legal ambiguity. While coastal states control territorial waters, the international law framework governing straits used for international navigation is complex and contested. Operators are therefore seeking clarity from flag states and legal counsel.

The practical effect is to keep more crude and product barrels off regular schedules, tightening near‑term availability. Regional price differentials have widened at times, and floating storage can build as ships wait for instructions that satisfy both legal and insurance constraints.

Next steps could include coordinated guidance from governments, insurers and industry bodies, and the exploration of escorted corridors. Until a coherent regime is in place, many firms are likely to err on the side of caution, prolonging the drag on Gulf energy exports.
