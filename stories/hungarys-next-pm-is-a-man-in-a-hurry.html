Hungary’s next PM is a man in a hurry

After Péter Magyar’s dramatic victory, Hungary enters a period of rapid political reordering as he prepares for a transfer of power and tries to turn an electoral landslide into durable control.

Péter Magyar has moved quickly to turn his dramatic victory into a workable plan for government, as Hungary begins to reckon with the prospect of an orderly transfer of power after years under Viktor Orbán. The speed is striking: rather than waiting for the political dust to settle, Magyar’s Tisza party is already preparing for what comes next.

That urgency reflects both opportunity and risk. Landslide victories can create momentum, but they can also fade if a new force cannot translate public anger into durable institutions, disciplined staffing and a coherent agenda.

Hungary now enters a period of political reordering in which Magyar will need to prove that he can govern, not just oppose. The task is made harder by the scale of the system he would inherit, including a state apparatus shaped over many years by Orbán’s allies.

The bigger question is whether Magyar can convert a decisive electoral moment into lasting control before his opponents regroup. In that sense, the pace itself is part of the strategy.

For voters, the promise is a break with the old order; for Hungary’s political class, the challenge is to see whether the next chapter can be written as quickly as the first one changed.
