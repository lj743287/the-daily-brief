Ukraine and Russia trade accusations of ceasefire violations

Kyiv and Moscow each allege hundreds of breaches, underscoring the fragility of a brief holiday truce. President Zelensky warned of a symmetrical response to attacks, while residents braced for renewed strikes once the pause expires.

Kyiv and Moscow accused each other of hundreds of breaches during a brief holiday truce, underscoring how tenuous pauses have become in a war entering another grinding phase. President Volodymyr Zelensky warned of a symmetrical response to attacks, signalling no appetite to absorb strikes without reply.

The ceasefire window was intended to reduce hostilities around religious observances, but shelling, drones and small‑arms fire were reported along multiple sections of the front. Neither side signalled durable shifts in positions, and both appeared to use the lull for limited rotations and repair work.

Independent verification is difficult. With no standing international monitoring mission along most of the line, claims and counter‑claims function as information warfare as much as battlefield accounting. Each side tends to tally breaches differently and spotlight incidents that fit its narrative.

For civilians, the respite offered little certainty. Local authorities prepared shelters, cautioned against travel near front lines and warned that strikes could resume at scale once the pause expired. Repair crews continued hardening critical infrastructure in anticipation of renewed attacks.

Diplomatically, the exchange of accusations complicates efforts to extend or replicate the truce. Mediators often try to pair temporary pauses with prisoner exchanges or humanitarian corridors, but the lack of trust and fears of tactical disadvantage have repeatedly derailed such plans.

Attention now turns to whether the violence reverts to recent patterns or intensifies. Observers are watching for renewed pressure on energy infrastructure, long‑range strikes across borders and any mobilisation or resupply moves that could shape the next phase of fighting.
