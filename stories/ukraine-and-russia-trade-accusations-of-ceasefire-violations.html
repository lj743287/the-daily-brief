Ukraine and Russia trade accusations of ceasefire violations

Kyiv and Moscow each blame the other for hundreds of breaches, underscoring the fragility of the truce. President Volodymyr Zelensky said Ukraine would respond “symmetrically”, raising fears of renewed escalation after a muted Easter respite.

Ukraine and Russia each accused the other of committing hundreds of breaches of a fragile ceasefire, underscoring the limits of a truce that had briefly calmed parts of the front during the Easter period, according to the BBC.

President Volodymyr Zelensky said Ukrainian forces would respond in kind to attacks, while Moscow alleged intensified Ukrainian shelling in several sectors. Battlefield claims from either side could not be independently verified.

Reports pointed to artillery, drone and small-arms fire along multiple axes, with military spokespeople amplifying allegations through social media and state outlets. The absence of neutral monitoring has made it difficult to assess the scale of violations.

Diplomatic channels remain strained, and mediators have urged restraint to prevent a relapse into broader fighting. With both militaries reinforcing positions, the incentives to test the limits of the truce appear to be growing.

Civilians near the line of contact continue to face intermittent outages, disrupted services and constrained access to humanitarian aid. Repair crews have struggled to reach damaged infrastructure amid security risks.

Analysts warned that a tit-for-tat cycle could quickly unravel remaining de-escalation efforts, especially if longer‑range strikes resume. Both governments have signalled they will not tolerate what they deem persistent or asymmetric violations.
