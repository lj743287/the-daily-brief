Inside the prediction markets betting millions on war

A Guardian investigation into Polymarket finds traders can shape narratives by wagering on global conflicts. The platform’s rulings on ‘truth’ and the ethics of monetising violence are drawing scrutiny from policymakers.

A Guardian investigation into Polymarket details how traders are wagering millions on the course of global conflicts, shaping narratives as markets update in real time. The platform’s markets can influence attention and framing well beyond its user base.

Because contracts hinge on specific outcomes, the platform’s rulings effectively arbitrate ‘truth’ for settlement purposes. Those decisions can feed back into media and social media discussion, raising stakes around sourcing and adjudication.

The ethics of monetising violence and geopolitics are under scrutiny. Critics argue that financial incentives can distort information flows, reward incendiary claims and normalise speculation on human suffering.

Policy interest is intensifying. The BBC reported that White House staff have been told not to place bets on prediction markets, underscoring concerns about conflicts of interest and the optics of wagering on public events.

Supporters say prediction markets aggregate dispersed information and improve forecasting accuracy. Yet questions persist over governance, criteria for sources, and what subjects should be off‑limits even in a decentralised setting.

With geopolitical risks high, regulators are likely to revisit rules on market design, disclosure, KYC and permissible topics. How these platforms evolve could shape the information ecosystem that investors and the public rely upon.
