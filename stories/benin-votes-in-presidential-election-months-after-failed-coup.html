Benin votes in presidential election months after failed coup

Voters headed to the polls to choose a successor to Patrice Talon after two terms, amid concerns over stability and democratic space. The finance minister is seen as the frontrunner in an election watched closely across West Africa.

Benin went to the polls to choose a successor to President Patrice Talon, who steps down after two terms, only months after authorities said they had foiled a coup attempt. The vote is seen as a test of the country’s stability and democratic credentials in a region unsettled by military takeovers.

The finance minister is viewed as the frontrunner, with supporters touting policy continuity and macroeconomic management. Critics say the political landscape has narrowed in recent years, raising concerns about the openness of competition and the space for dissent.

Security was visibly tighter around polling stations, with additional forces deployed to deter intimidation and safeguard ballot materials. Regional bloc ECOWAS and international partners are watching closely, given Benin’s role as a trade hub and its proximity to conflict‑affected neighbours.

Economically, Benin has benefited from investments in logistics and the Port of Cotonou, but faces familiar pressures from cost‑of‑living concerns and regional insecurity. Continuity in fiscal and trade policy would likely reassure markets, though social demands remain high.

For West Africa, a peaceful, credible transfer would be a rare boost. A disputed outcome or unrest could reverberate beyond Benin’s borders, complicating efforts to coordinate security and migration policies across the sub‑region.

The electoral commission is expected to release results in the coming days in line with legal timelines. Candidates have been urged to channel any disputes through the courts, and international observers will assess the conduct and fairness of the process.
