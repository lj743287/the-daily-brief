Argentina clears mining in glacier and permafrost zones, angering environmentalists

A new law eases extraction in sensitive Andean regions to unlock copper, lithium and silver. The shift could boost investment but intensifies ESG concerns and legal challenges.

Argentina has approved legal changes to allow mining in glacier and permafrost zones of the Andes to unlock copper, lithium and silver, drawing sharp criticism from environmental groups, according to reporting by the BBC and the Guardian.

Backers of the amendments argue the country needs investment and export revenues from critical minerals central to the energy transition, and say modern techniques can manage environmental risks. The legislation modifies the so‑called “glacier law”, a longstanding constraint on activity in sensitive high‑altitude areas.

Opponents warn of potential harm to freshwater reserves, ecosystems and tourism, and have pledged protests and legal action. ESG‑focused investors are likely to reassess exposure, with reputational and compliance risks climbing alongside prospective returns.

The global supply chain context is pivotal: demand for battery metals remains robust, prompting miners to seek new jurisdictions as permitting tightens elsewhere. Argentina’s shift could divert capital from regional rivals, but the perceived legal and social licence to operate will be decisive.

Legal uncertainty may persist as courts interpret the amended protections and balance federal with provincial powers over natural resources. That could slow any project pipeline despite headline policy support.

For Andean communities, the move spotlights trade‑offs between jobs, royalties and environmental stewardship. International lenders and offtakers may press for tighter safeguards and independent monitoring before funding or signing long‑term purchase agreements.
