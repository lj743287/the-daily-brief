Faisal Islam: Why the government is relaxed about Chinese car imports

The government appears willing to accept cheaper Chinese vehicles as part of a wider calculation about consumers, competition and industrial policy.

The government's apparent calm about Chinese car imports reflects a wider calculation about consumers, competition and industrial policy. Cheaper vehicles can help keep prices down at a time when households are under pressure, even if they also unsettle domestic manufacturers.

Ministers seem to accept that the rise of China's car industry cannot simply be wished away. Instead, they appear to be betting that competition will force innovation and make the market more affordable, while Britain tries to protect the parts of its industrial base it still considers strategically important.

That approach carries risks. Greater imports can ease short-term pressures for drivers, but they may also deepen anxiety about jobs, supply chains and state support for British industry. The government's stance suggests it is prioritising flexibility over outright protection.
