Four arrested over murder of Scottish businessman in Kenya

Kenyan police detained suspects over the killing of Campbell Scott, whose body was found last year. The case has drawn cross-border attention to the safety of visiting executives and conference-goers.

Kenyan police have arrested four people over the killing of Scottish businessman Campbell Scott, in a case that has focused attention on the safety of visiting executives and conference-goers. The detentions come more than a year after Mr Scott’s body was discovered following a business trip to Kenya.

Mr Scott had been in Nairobi for a conference in February last year. His body was later found inside a pineapple sack about 60 miles from the capital, according to local reports at the time. Police say the four suspects have been detained as part of an ongoing murder investigation.

Authorities have not publicly released further details about the suspects or a possible motive. Investigators are expected to seek court approvals for continued detention while evidence is gathered, a routine step in serious criminal cases in Kenya. Prosecutors would then decide whether to bring formal charges.

The case has resonated in both countries because of the cross-border nature of modern business travel. Nairobi is a major regional hub for corporate events, and the killing has underscored long-standing guidance for visitors to exercise heightened vigilance, particularly when travelling beyond established urban areas or outside organised transport and accommodation arrangements.

For Kenya, which markets itself as a gateway for investment and conferences, the investigation’s progress will be closely watched by the events industry and international firms that rely on predictable security and clear legal processes. For UK businesses, the killing has revived questions about corporate travel risk management, from route planning and local partnerships to after-hours movements and communications protocols.

The next phase is likely to focus on assembling forensic evidence and clarifying the timeline of Mr Scott’s final movements. Any court proceedings will draw scrutiny in Scotland and Kenya alike, both for what they reveal about responsibility for the killing and for their implications for future cooperation on consular support and justice in cross-border cases.
