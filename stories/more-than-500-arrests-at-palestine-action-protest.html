More than 500 arrests at Palestine Action protest

Police detained hundreds at a large-scale demonstration after the group’s ban. Organisers framed the event as resistance to proscription; authorities cited public-order and extremism concerns.

Police detained more than 500 people during a large-scale demonstration linked to the recently banned activist network Palestine Action, in one of the biggest single-day protest policing operations this year. Officers said the action was taken to maintain public order and enforce the new restrictions arising from the group’s proscription.

Organisers framed the mobilisation as resistance to the ban, vowing to continue their campaign against UK-based firms they accuse of facilitating Israel’s military operations. The protest drew a substantial turnout and was intended to signal that support for the cause remains organised despite the government’s move to outlaw the network.

Ministers banned Palestine Action citing concerns about extremism and the risk of serious criminality. Proscription in UK law makes it a criminal offence to belong to or support a banned organisation, including organising activity on its behalf or publicly displaying its symbols, and can carry substantial prison terms.

Police indicated those held were arrested on suspicion of a range of public-order and related offences, with inquiries under way to determine whether terrorism legislation had been breached. Civil liberties advocates argue the ban blurs the line between disruptive protest and extremism and could chill legitimate dissent; the government contends it equips authorities to pre-empt serious disorder.

The confrontation underscores tensions seen across Europe and North America since the Gaza war, as officials tighten controls on disruptive demonstrations while activist networks shift towards decentralised actions against defence and technology supply chains. The UK’s sizeable defence sector and export links have made sites associated with it recurring flashpoints.

Further updates on arrests and charging decisions are expected in the coming days, with mass processing, bail conditions and initial court appearances likely to follow. Legal challenges to the proscription are anticipated, and more demonstrations are probable, setting up an early test of how forcefully the law will be applied and how the balance between public safety and the right to protest will be drawn.
