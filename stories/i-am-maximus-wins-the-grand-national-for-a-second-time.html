I Am Maximus wins the Grand National for a second time

Willie Mullins’s chaser completed a rare repeat at Aintree, echoing the feats of the sport’s greats. The victory underscores Mullins’s dominance and the horse’s stamina and class.

I Am Maximus delivered a rare repeat in the Grand National, prevailing again at Aintree to confirm both his stamina and class under Willie Mullins’s stewardship. The result underlined the trainer’s grip on the staying chase division and the horse’s capacity to handle the race’s unique demands.

Multiple Grand National wins are exceptional, placing I Am Maximus alongside a select group that includes Red Rum and Tiger Roll. In a modern era shaped by enhanced safety measures and deeper fields, backing up a previous success is an achievement that resonates beyond the sport.

The performance was marked by efficient jumping and measured positioning, traits that matter more at Aintree than raw speed alone. Kept out of trouble and asked for his effort late, I Am Maximus sustained a telling run from the final fences to settle the contest decisively.

For Mullins, it adds another significant laurel in a career spanning dominant campaigns on both sides of the Irish Sea. His training operation has translated festival form to Aintree repeatedly, and this victory bolsters his claims in the cross‑channel trainer standings as the season deepens.

The horse’s connections will now weigh the balance between protecting his mark and targeting the few staying prizes that fit his profile. Handicappers are likely to respond to a second National, and future campaigns may require selective entries to preserve longevity.

The Grand National’s ongoing evolution—adjustments to fences, field size and protocols—hasn’t diminished its global reach or jeopardy. That a seasoned chaser has adapted twice to its complexity is a reminder that experience, temperament and placement still decide the sport’s grandest handicap.
