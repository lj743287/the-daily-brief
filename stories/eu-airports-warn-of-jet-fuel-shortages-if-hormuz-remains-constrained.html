EU airports warn of jet-fuel shortages if Hormuz remains constrained

ACI Europe said members were increasingly concerned over jet-fuel availability, highlighting airlines’ exposure to Gulf supply routes. The sector is preparing contingency plans as energy logistics tighten.

European airports have warned of potential jet‑fuel shortages if constraints in the Strait of Hormuz persist, highlighting the sector’s exposure to Gulf supply routes. Airports Council International (ACI) Europe said members were increasingly concerned about availability as logistics tighten.

Operators are preparing contingency measures, including fuel tankering by airlines, schedule adjustments and coordination with suppliers to manage stocks. Such steps can buy time but add costs and complexity to operations that are finely tuned to seasonal travel patterns.

Stock levels vary by hub, reflecting storage capacity, access to pipelines and proximity to alternative marine deliveries. Where inventories are thinner, airports may need to prioritise certain services or work with carriers to rebalance uplift patterns across their networks.

Any sustained squeeze would likely raise operating costs, pressuring fares as hedging buffers roll off. Past fuel shocks have tended to pass through to ticket prices with a lag, though competitive dynamics and capacity constraints shape how quickly and by how much.

Europe has historically relied on refined product imports from the Gulf, including aviation kerosene, supplemented by domestic refining. Tightness at Hormuz narrows options in the near term, and alternative barrels from other regions come with higher freight and lead times, especially when insurers are cautious.

The outlook depends on whether maritime flows resume and insurers restore cover on workable terms. Industry and governments are discussing the use of strategic stocks and facilitating logistics, but the sector’s near‑term comfort ultimately rests on the reliability of shipping through the Gulf.
