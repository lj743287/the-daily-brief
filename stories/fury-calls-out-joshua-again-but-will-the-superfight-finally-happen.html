Fury calls out Joshua again — but will the superfight finally happen?

After comfortably outpointing Arslanbek Makhmudov, Tyson Fury said it is ‘him or I’m out’ as he pushed for a long‑mooted Joshua bout. A decade of false starts and broadcast, venue and purse hurdles still stand in the way.

After comfortably outpointing Arslanbek Makhmudov over 12 rounds at Tottenham Hotspur Stadium, Tyson Fury renewed calls for a long‑mooted bout with Anthony Joshua, casting it as the only fight he wants next. A decade of near agreements and abrupt collapses suggests caution.

Even with public declarations aligned, the familiar obstacles remain. Broadcast and streaming rights need harmonising, rematch clauses require careful drafting, and purse structure must satisfy both sides. Each element has derailed talks before.

Venue and timing are equally complex. A UK stadium date carries clear demand, while international options can offer outsized guarantees but tighter control over logistics and undercard. Balancing prestige, availability and revenue is a delicate trade‑off.

Sanctioning body calendars and mandatory challengers add another layer. Titles confer leverage but also deadlines, and aligning those with two separate camps’ preferred timelines is rarely straightforward.

Public signalling has grown louder in recent weeks, with both fighters and their teams indicating intent. Yet, as past cycles have shown, momentum can evaporate quickly once negotiations enter the weeds of exclusivity and distribution.

A deal this year remains plausible, but far from assured. Until a venue is booked, contracts are signed and a date is public, the prudent view is scepticism shaped by history rather than pessimism about demand.
