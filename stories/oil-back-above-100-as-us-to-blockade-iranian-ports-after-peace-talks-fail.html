Oil back above $100 as US to blockade Iranian ports after peace talks fail

Oil has moved back above $100 a barrel after the failure of peace talks, with the US preparing to blockade Iranian ports and worries growing about a deeper global energy crisis.

Oil has moved back above $100 a barrel after peace talks between the US and Iran failed, adding to fears of a deeper global energy shock. Markets are reacting not just to the collapse of diplomacy but to the prospect of a US blockade of Iranian ports, which would threaten shipping through one of the world's most critical energy corridors.

The increase matters because higher crude prices feed quickly into fuel, transport and inflation. That has knock-on effects for households, companies and central banks already trying to judge how much strain the conflict will put on the world economy.

The failure of negotiations at the weekend has left tankers stranded in the Gulf and has not produced any clear path back to de-escalation. If shipping remains disrupted, traders expect further pressure on oil and gas prices, with borrowing costs also likely to move higher as inflation risks build.

For governments, the immediate challenge is to prevent the crisis from becoming a broader economic shock. For consumers, the warning is simpler: the cost of the Middle East conflict may now be moving from the headlines into everyday bills.
