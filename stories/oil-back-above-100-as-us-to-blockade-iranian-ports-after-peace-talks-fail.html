Oil back above $100 as US to blockade Iranian ports after peace talks fail

The failure of negotiations and the threat of a blockade have pushed oil back above $100, intensifying fears that the global energy shock will deepen and spread further through prices.

Oil prices have climbed back above $100 after the collapse of peace talks and Donald Trump’s threat to blockade Iranian ports intensified fears of a deeper energy shock. The market reaction reflects not only the risk of lost supply, but the possibility that shipping routes in and around the Gulf could become much harder to use safely.

The Strait of Hormuz is central to global energy flows, so any move to restrict access immediately affects expectations for crude, refined products and freight. Even the prospect of disruption is enough to push prices higher, because traders are trying to price in not just lost barrels but delays, insurance costs and the risk of retaliation.

For consumers and central banks, the danger is that the shock spreads beyond oil. Higher energy costs can feed into transport, food and manufacturing prices, while also putting pressure on inflation and interest-rate expectations in economies that had only recently started to stabilise.

The main uncertainty is how far this confrontation goes. If the blockade threat is acted on, or if Iran responds by targeting shipping, the price spike could persist and widen. If diplomacy somehow resumes, markets may ease, but the episode has already shown how quickly geopolitics can reach the pump and the balance sheet.
