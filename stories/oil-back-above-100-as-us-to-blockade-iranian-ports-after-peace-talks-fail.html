Oil back above $100 as US to blockade Iranian ports after peace talks fail

Oil has moved back above $100 a barrel as the US prepares to blockade Iranian ports after weekend peace talks failed, deepening fears of a broader energy crisis and further inflation.

Oil has climbed back above $100 a barrel as the United States prepares to blockade Iranian ports after peace talks collapsed at the weekend. The move points to a deepening energy shock, with traders now pricing in the possibility that the conflict around Iran will last longer and disrupt supplies further.

The market reaction reflects a simple fear: if shipping through and around the Gulf is obstructed, the consequences will reach well beyond crude prices. Higher oil costs feed into transport, food and manufacturing, raising the risk that an already fraught geopolitical crisis becomes a broader inflation problem.

The blockade threat is also a reminder of how closely diplomacy, military pressure and market behaviour are now linked. Once negotiations fail, the first signs are often seen not in official statements but in shipping routes, insurance premiums and the price of fuel.

What happens next will depend on whether the US follows through and how Iran responds. But even the threat has already changed the mood in energy markets, where the assumption is now that disruption may be measured in weeks, not days.
