Collapse of US-Iran talks heightens fears of prolonged energy shock

With tanker traffic constrained and investors braced for more disruption, markets are pricing in a longer period of higher oil, gas and transport costs.

The collapse of US-Iran talks has deepened fears that the energy shock caused by conflict in and around the Gulf may last longer than markets initially hoped. With tanker traffic still constrained, traders are increasingly assuming that disruption could persist rather than ease quickly.

That matters because oil, gas and shipping costs are already responding to the risk premium attached to the Strait of Hormuz. Even when cargoes continue moving, hesitation by shipowners, insurers and buyers can tighten supply and keep prices elevated.

The wider economy is exposed as well. Higher transport and fuel costs feed into food, aviation and manufacturing, while governments that had hoped for a quick de-escalation now face the prospect of a drawn-out period of instability in a critical energy corridor.

For investors, the key uncertainty is whether the disruption is temporary brinkmanship or the start of a more entrenched regional shock. The answer will determine how long energy and freight markets remain under pressure.
