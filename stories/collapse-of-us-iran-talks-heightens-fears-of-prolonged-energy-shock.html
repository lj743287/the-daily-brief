Collapse of US-Iran talks heightens fears of prolonged energy shock

The failure of diplomacy has intensified fears of higher oil prices, costlier borrowing and renewed inflation pressure across the world economy.

The collapse of direct talks between the US and Iran has deepened fears that the shock to global energy markets will last longer than many hoped. With diplomacy stalled, traders are left to price in the risk of higher oil costs, possible disruption to shipping and a broader hit to confidence.

That matters because energy is feeding quickly into other parts of the economy. Higher fuel prices can lift transport bills, raise production costs and complicate central banks’ efforts to bring inflation down. The effects are rarely confined to one country; they tend to move through supply chains and consumer prices across regions.

Europe, Asia and the US are each exposed in different ways, but all face the same basic problem: uncertainty. Airlines worry about jet fuel, manufacturers about freight and households about what they pay at the pump. If the crisis persists, those costs may become harder to reverse even if fighting does not spread further.

For policymakers, the test is whether to treat the disruption as temporary or prepare for a more prolonged squeeze. A ceasefire would ease some pressure, but unless it is followed by a workable diplomatic reset, markets are likely to remain volatile and the inflationary impact could linger.
