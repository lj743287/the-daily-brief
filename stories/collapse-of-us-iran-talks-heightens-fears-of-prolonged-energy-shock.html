Collapse of US-Iran talks heightens fears of prolonged energy shock

With tanker traffic still constrained and the ceasefire uncertain, markets are braced for another leg of pressure on oil, gas and transport costs.

The collapse of US-Iran talks has left energy markets braced for a longer period of disruption rather than a swift return to normal. With tanker traffic still constrained and the ceasefire still uncertain, traders are now focused on how long shipments through the region remain impaired.

The Strait of Hormuz is central to global oil and gas flows, so even partial disruption can ripple through transport, refining and power costs. Higher freight rates and insurance premiums can feed into prices well beyond the Gulf, adding pressure to industries already facing tighter margins.

Consumers are likely to feel the effects more slowly, but they are unlikely to be spared if shipping remains subdued for long. Fuel costs can work through supply chains in stages, lifting the price of imported goods, food and travel before broader inflation measures fully reflect the shock.

What happens next depends on whether diplomacy resumes or the standoff hardens into a semi-permanent disruption. Markets are trying to judge not only the risk of another spike, but also whether this crisis resets a higher cost base for energy and transport over the months ahead.
