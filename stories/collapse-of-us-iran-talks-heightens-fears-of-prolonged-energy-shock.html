Collapse of US-Iran talks heightens fears of prolonged energy shock

The failure of talks has left markets braced for a longer period of elevated oil and gas prices, with tankers still stranded in the Gulf. Inflation, transport costs and borrowing expectations are all vulnerable if the Strait of Hormuz remains constrained.

The collapse of US-Iran talks has left energy markets braced for another period of instability, with tanker traffic in the Gulf still constrained and traders unsure how quickly conditions will normalise.

The Strait of Hormuz is central to the global oil trade, and even limited disruption can push up crude and gas prices. That filters quickly into transport, food and industrial costs, which is why the impact is being felt far beyond the region itself.

The bigger worry for markets is that a ceasefire or diplomatic pause would not immediately undo the damage already done. Businesses are still dealing with higher freight costs, tighter margins and the risk that borrowing costs stay elevated if energy prices remain volatile.

For governments and central banks, the problem is timing as much as scale. Even if the fighting eases, the economic shock can linger in supply chains and inflation data for weeks or months. That makes the next move in Washington and Tehran important not just for geopolitics, but for the wider outlook for growth and prices.
