Collapse of US-Iran talks heightens fears of prolonged energy shock

With tankers stranded and oil prices exposed to geopolitical risk, investors are weighing whether the disruption turns into a broader hit to growth and inflation.

The collapse of US-Iran talks has left markets facing a longer period of energy risk rather than a clean diplomatic reset. With tanker traffic constrained and the Strait of Hormuz exposed to further disruption, investors are having to price in the possibility of a sustained shock.

That is important because oil and gas remain central to inflation expectations, shipping costs and consumer confidence. Even when prices ease from immediate peaks, the effects of a geopolitical disruption can take months to work through the system.

The bigger worry for markets is that the disruption becomes structural rather than temporary. If shipping companies avoid the strait, insurers raise premiums and governments struggle to restore confidence, the impact could spread beyond energy into growth forecasts and corporate earnings.

For now, traders are trying to separate a temporary spike from a broader macro hit. The problem is that the politics remain unsettled, which means the market damage could outlast any single flare-up in the conflict.
