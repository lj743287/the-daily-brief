Collapse of US-Iran talks heightens fears of prolonged energy shock

The failure of US-Iran talks has put oil and gas markets on alert, with tankers still stranded in the Gulf and analysts warning of further damage to growth and prices.

The failure of US-Iran talks has put oil and gas markets on alert for a prolonged energy shock. With tankers still stranded in the Gulf, traders are now bracing for a period of higher prices, tighter supply and more expensive borrowing for energy-heavy businesses.

The collapse matters because diplomacy had offered at least the possibility of a managed outcome. Instead, the market is left with a more open-ended confrontation in which neither side appears ready to make the concessions the other demands.

That raises the chance that the conflict will be felt in ways that go well beyond the Gulf. If energy prices rise for long enough, the damage can spread to inflation, consumer demand and growth, especially in economies already under pressure.

For now, the picture is one of uncertainty rather than immediate breakdown. But as long as tankers remain stranded and talks are stalled, the assumption in markets is that the shock is not over — and may yet deepen.
