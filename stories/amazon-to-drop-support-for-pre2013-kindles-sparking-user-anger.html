Amazon to drop support for pre‑2013 Kindles, sparking user anger

Older devices will no longer download new e‑books, prompting complaints about planned obsolescence. Amazon says it will maintain access to existing libraries.

Amazon will end support for Kindle e‑readers released before 2013, preventing those devices from downloading new e‑books, the BBC reports. Reading of existing titles already on the devices will continue.

Users can still access their libraries via newer hardware and Kindle apps, and may sideload compatible files where possible. The change reflects the company’s stance on maintaining older, networked devices.

Owners have criticised the decision as planned obsolescence that creates e‑waste and undermines the value of purchased content. Amazon has previously cited security, connectivity and maintenance costs in similar deprecations.

Consumer advocates argue that closed ecosystems and digital rights management reduce longevity and portability, and are pressing for clearer minimum support periods and interoperability to extend device life.

Libraries, schools and accessibility users reliant on familiar e‑ink interfaces could face replacement costs and disruption. Older devices remain popular for their eye‑strain advantages and battery life.

The move revives debate about what it means to ‘own’ digital books tied to a vendor’s ecosystem, and whether portability mandates or right‑to‑repair rules should cover formats and services as well as hardware.
