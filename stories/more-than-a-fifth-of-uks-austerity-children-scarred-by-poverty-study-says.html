More than a fifth of UK’s ‘austerity children’ scarred by poverty, study says

A new study says child poverty has left a lasting mark on a large share of children born after 2013, linking the harm directly to years of welfare cuts.

A new study says more than a fifth of children born after 2013 have been left with lasting scars from poverty, linking the damage to years of welfare cuts. The findings add fresh weight to the argument that austerity has had consequences that extend well beyond the immediate squeeze on household budgets.

Child poverty affects health, school readiness, emotional development and later life chances, so the impact can persist long after the original policy choices have been made. That is why the study is likely to resonate in the broader debate over whether Britain has done enough to shield children from economic hardship.

The political significance is clear: this is not only a question of past policy, but of what current governments are willing to do about it. Unless incomes rise and support improves, the cohort now growing up in poverty may carry those effects into adulthood.
