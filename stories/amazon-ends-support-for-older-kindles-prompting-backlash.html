Amazon ends support for older Kindles, prompting backlash

Devices released before 2013 will no longer download new e-books. The decision highlights e-waste and the limits of long-term software support in consumer tech.

Amazon will end support for older Kindle e-readers, with devices released before 2013 no longer able to download new e-books. The move has prompted a backlash from owners who say the hardware remains usable, and from campaigners who argue the change undermines confidence in digital purchases.

The decision affects early e-ink models that have remained popular for their durability and long battery life. Users say the loss of store connectivity and cloud delivery will sharply limit the readers’ usefulness, even if the screens and batteries still function, and have voiced frustration that long-held libraries will be harder to access on ageing devices.

The episode highlights the environmental and consumer stakes in software support. E-readers are designed to last for years, yet service switches can shorten their lifespans, creating e-waste and nudging customers towards upgrades. The impact is global: in many markets, older devices circulate through families, schools and libraries, extending access to digital books where budgets are tight.

It also fits a broader pattern across consumer technology, where firms retire legacy systems as security, network standards and content delivery evolve. Industry arguments typically stress the costs and risks of maintaining outdated platforms, while consumer advocates call for minimum support periods and clearer end‑of‑life policies. Policymakers in Europe and the UK are pushing right‑to‑repair and longer software support, signalling rising expectations on manufacturers.

The Kindle shift revives a long‑running debate about digital ownership. Because many e-books are locked to online accounts and protected by digital rights management, device support decisions can have immediate consequences for access. Some users may be able to transfer files manually from a computer, but such workarounds do not replace integrated store features or ease of use.

Attention will now turn to how Amazon mitigates the fallout, including whether it expands recycling options or offers upgrade incentives, and how clearly it communicates the change and any alternatives. Consumer groups are likely to press for stronger guarantees on software longevity, while public institutions that rely on e-readers could reassess procurement policies. The controversy underlines a wider reckoning for connected devices: hardware may endure, but support lifecycles increasingly define what owners can actually do.
