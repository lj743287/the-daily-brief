The Dutch village at risk of being demolished

A local case of infrastructure-led displacement in the Netherlands raises broader questions about the costs of Europe’s energy transition and grid expansion.

A village in the Netherlands is facing removal to make way for a vast electricity substation, in a striking example of how the energy transition can impose costs on communities even as it promises wider benefits.

The case of Moerdijk highlights a familiar European problem: the infrastructure needed for cleaner power, stronger grids and industrial electrification often requires land, planning consent and difficult trade-offs. Local residents can end up carrying a disproportionate share of the burden.

That makes the story more than a narrow planning dispute. As governments race to expand networks and connect new sources of electricity, they will increasingly have to decide who is compensated, who is displaced and how far local opposition can slow projects deemed nationally necessary.
