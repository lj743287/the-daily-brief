Starmer urges US and Iran to avoid escalation after talks end without deal

The UK prime minister has called on Washington and Tehran to ‘find a way through’ following the collapse of intensive talks. He warned against steps that could further destabilise the region, after negotiations ended without agreement. London’s position aligns with European calls for restraint and the protection of commercial traffic. Britain’s leverage will be tested as tensions linger around the Strait of Hormuz.

Prime Minister Keir Starmer urged the United States and Iran to keep lines of communication open after protracted talks failed to produce an agreement. He cautioned both against actions that could deepen the crisis.

\n

The UK message mirrors concerns from European partners focused on safeguarding maritime transit and limiting economic spillovers. British officials emphasise that even partial disruption in the Gulf carries global costs.

\n

Downing Street sees value in coordinated messaging with allies to reinforce de-escalation incentives and support practical steps to reduce risks to commercial traffic.

\n

London’s role is constrained but not negligible. The UK retains diplomatic channels in the region and a stake in maritime security through its naval presence and shipping interests.

\n

Business groups have sought clarity on contingency planning should the closure of the Strait of Hormuz persist, including potential stock management for critical fuels and materials.

\n

Officials acknowledge the risk that inflation pressures, already visible in some economies, could feed through to UK prices if energy markets remain unsettled.

\n

Parliament is expected to scrutinise the government’s approach to balancing support for allies, maritime security, and domestic economic resilience.

\n

For now, the emphasis is on preventing incidents at sea, supporting dialogue and keeping commercial lanes as open and predictable as possible under the circumstances.
