Djibouti’s president wins sixth term with nearly 98% of the vote

Ismail Omar Guelleh extended his 27-year rule in an election boycotted by main opposition candidates. Rights groups and observers have long criticised the country’s political environment as uncompetitive.

Djibouti’s president, Ismail Omar Guelleh, secured a sixth term with nearly 98% of the vote in an election boycotted by main opposition candidates, extending his 27‑year rule, the BBC reported.

Rights groups and some observers have long criticised the country’s political environment as uncompetitive, while the government argues that continuity provides stability in a volatile region.

The small Horn of Africa nation occupies a strategic position at the mouth of the Red Sea, hosting key maritime routes and several foreign military facilities that underpin its security partnerships.

Economic challenges remain, including a high cost of living, debt burdens linked to infrastructure projects and the need to diversify beyond port and logistics revenues.

With the vote settled, international partners are expected to maintain pragmatic ties focused on security and trade, even as they continue to encourage governance and human rights reforms.

Domestic opponents are likely to renew calls for broader political space and institutional safeguards, though the opposition’s boycott limits immediate leverage over policy direction.
