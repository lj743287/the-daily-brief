Greece to ban social media for under‑15s

Athens plans to restrict access for younger teens, following similar debates across Europe. Supporters cite harms mitigation; critics warn of enforcement and rights concerns.

Greece plans to prohibit under‑15s from accessing social‑media platforms from next year, the BBC reports, positioning the country among European governments testing stricter age limits online.

Officials present the move as harm reduction aimed at mental health, bullying and exposure to abuse, while critics warn of enforcement difficulties and potential overreach into young people’s rights to information and expression.

Implementation is expected to rely on app‑store and platform‑level age verification, potentially involving identity checks or third‑party age‑assurance services. That raises privacy and data‑minimisation questions that regulators will need to address.

The proposal lands alongside EU‑wide rules under the Digital Services Act, which already restrict targeted advertising to minors and require risk assessments for large platforms. France and Spain have debated adjacent measures, reflecting a broader regional shift.

Schools, parents and guardians are likely to face practical challenges around device use, homework platforms and exceptions. Workarounds such as borrowed accounts or VPNs are foreseeable, complicating compliance and undermining intended protections.

Civil‑society groups are expected to test the proportionality of any implementing rules in court. Authorities will need to show necessity, precision and safeguards under EU law, including GDPR constraints on processing children’s data for verification.
