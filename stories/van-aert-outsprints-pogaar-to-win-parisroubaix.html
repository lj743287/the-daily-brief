Van Aert outsprints Pogačar to win Paris–Roubaix

The Belgian prevailed in a thrilling velodrome finish to capture cycling’s ‘Hell of the North’. The result intensifies a multi-discipline rivalry that is lifting the sport.

Wout van Aert claimed Paris–Roubaix in a tense velodrome sprint, outsprinting Tadej Pogačar after a bruising day on the cobbles that distilled the Monument’s brutal theatre into a final lap of precision and nerve. The Belgian timed his effort to perfection in Roubaix’s old concrete bowl, sealing victory at cycling’s ‘Hell of the North’ and denying the four-time Tour de France winner in the most exacting of one-day tests.

The race fragmented repeatedly across the infamous pavé, with the lead selection forming over a sequence of sectors that punished positioning as much as power. After attacks ebbed and flowed, a small group reached the velodrome, where calculation replaced chaos and track craft decided what the cobbles could not. Van Aert, long a master of the northern classics, found the extra turn of speed that Pogačar could not answer.

This matters because Paris–Roubaix remains one of sport’s great elemental contests: a global showcase of resilience, luck and tactical clarity, where reputations forged elsewhere are re-examined. Van Aert’s triumph adds heft to a career built across disciplines, while Pogačar’s runner-up finish underlines how a Grand Tour dominator can translate form to the pavé—broadening the appeal of a race once seen as the preserve of specialists.

The outcome also intensifies a rivalry that is redefining men’s road cycling. Van Aert’s cyclo-cross pedigree and classics acumen meeting Pogačar’s stage-race supremacy is emblematic of a generation unwilling to be siloed. Their repeated collisions across terrain and formats are lifting standards, compressing the calendar’s hierarchies and drawing in audiences from Europe to the Americas and Asia who increasingly follow riders rather than race types.

Interpretations will differ. For some, the sprint confirms that Roubaix still rewards hard-earned nuance—positioning, equipment choices and team depth—as much as raw wattage. Others will see Pogačar’s performance as proof that the sport’s best can now contend everywhere. Either way, the balance of power will be tested again in the months ahead, with the summer’s marquee races and an Olympic year inviting further crossovers and fresh fault lines.

Beyond the podium, the spectacle reinforces cycling’s direction of travel: more star-to-star matchups, fewer sacred boundaries between classics and stage racing, and a premium on adaptability. It also renews debates on rider safety over cobbles and the fine margins imposed by weather and surface. For now, though, Roubaix’s verdict is clear—van Aert has the upper hand, and the sport is stronger for the breadth and intensity of this rivalry.
