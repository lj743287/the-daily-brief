Wales Explainer

What’s your Senedd constituency under the new map?

Boundary changes alter the political geography of Wales. Knowing your new seat helps voters assess local contests and candidate priorities.

Wales’ electoral map for the Senedd has been redrawn, with the number of constituencies cut and each seat made larger. Every voter will now fall into a new constituency boundary, part of a wider reform aimed at making representation more even and the parliament better able to scrutinise government. For voters, knowing the new seat name and shape matters: it determines which contest you take part in, which candidates are competing for your support, and who will be accountable for local priorities after polling day.

What has changed is not just the lines on the map but the way the Senedd is elected. The old model of local constituency members plus regional top‑ups is being replaced by fewer, larger constituencies that elect multiple members using a list system designed to be more proportional. This is separate from Westminster boundaries and UK general elections. In practice, you will be voting within a bigger patch than before, and the result should better reflect the balance of votes cast across that wider area, a feature common to proportional systems used in many democracies.

To find your new Senedd constituency, use an official postcode checker from election authorities or your local council. Many seat names combine familiar places, reflecting how several communities are now grouped together. If you live near a county line or on the edge of a city, check carefully: some neighbourhoods have moved to a different Senedd seat than before. These changes affect devolved elections only—local council services and UK Parliament constituencies may have different boundaries and names.

The political implications will be felt quickly. Incumbent Members of the Senedd may now compete alongside colleagues in larger multi‑member seats, and parties are recalibrating where they think they can win. Local priorities—transport links that cross council borders, access to GPs, support for high streets, coastal management—may be reframed for a wider patch. Supporters argue this produces teams of representatives with broader reach; critics worry some communities could feel less directly tied to a single, clearly defined local champion.

With boundaries finalised for the next Senedd election, parties are selecting and ranking candidates, publishing maps and guidance, and refining their ground campaigns. Voters can expect more information from election authorities on the rules, accessibility provisions and deadlines for registration, postal and proxy voting. If you already receive casework support from an MS, it is worth checking which candidates will seek to represent your newly defined area, and how they plan to divide responsibilities within larger constituencies.

When weighing your local race, bear in mind that past results are not a straight guide because the map and the system have changed. Look at how parties have performed across the communities now bundled into your seat, the mix of local and national priorities in each manifesto, and how candidate teams propose to cover the geography—from urban centres to rural hinterlands. Understanding your new constituency will help you judge which slate is best placed to deliver for your area in the next Senedd.
