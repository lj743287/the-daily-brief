What is a naval blockade and how would it work in Strait of Hormuz?

As Donald Trump threatens a blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, this explainer sets out what a naval blockade means in practice, and why it would matter for oil, shipping and war risk.

Donald Trump says the US is blockading the Strait of Hormuz, a claim that has put renewed focus on what a naval blockade actually means in practice.

In simple terms, a blockade is an attempt to stop ships entering or leaving an area by force or threat of force. In the Strait of Hormuz, one of the world’s most important oil chokepoints, that would have immediate consequences for global shipping and energy prices.

The practical questions are just as important as the legal ones: who enforces the blockade, how tightly it is policed, and how other states or shipping companies respond. Even the prospect of a blockade can push up insurance costs and unsettle markets.

The issue matters because the Strait carries huge volumes of oil and fuel. If access is restricted for long, the economic effect could spread far beyond the Gulf.

What happens next depends on whether the threat remains rhetoric or becomes an operational reality.
