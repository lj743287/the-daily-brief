What is a naval blockade and how would it work in Strait of Hormuz?

As the US threatens action in the Strait of Hormuz, the practical and legal meaning of a naval blockade matters for shipping, energy prices and allied military commitments.

Donald Trump’s threat to blockade the Strait of Hormuz has put a rarely used and legally contentious weapon back into the centre of debate. In simple terms, a naval blockade is an attempt to stop ships entering or leaving a port or coastline, usually enforced by military power and backed by international law only in limited circumstances.

The Strait of Hormuz matters because it is one of the world’s most important shipping lanes, carrying a large share of global oil and gas traffic. Any obstruction there would quickly affect energy prices, freight costs and the willingness of allied navies to commit assets to the region.

In practice, a blockade would not be a single event but a sustained operation requiring ships, surveillance, rules of engagement and political backing. It could also raise immediate questions about whether it is lawful, whether it is enforceable and how other states would respond if their commercial vessels were affected.

That is why the issue matters far beyond the Gulf. Even the threat of a blockade can unsettle markets and diplomatic partners, because it signals that the crisis is no longer being managed only through sanctions and strikes, but through control of sea lanes.
