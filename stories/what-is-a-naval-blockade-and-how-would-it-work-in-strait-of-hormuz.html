What is a naval blockade and how would it work in Strait of Hormuz?

A practical guide to how a blockade might be attempted, what it could and could not achieve, and why the legal and military risks are so high.

A naval blockade is an attempt to stop ships entering or leaving an area by using naval forces, surveillance and the threat of interception. In the Strait of Hormuz, that would mean trying to control one of the world’s most sensitive choke points, where even brief disruption can affect global trade.

In practice, a blockade would be difficult to enforce. Warships would need to identify vessels, issue warnings, stop or search them and deal with any challenge from Iran or from other states whose ships are caught up in the operation. That raises the risk of rapid escalation.

The legal position is also contested, especially if no broader international mandate exists. Even if Washington argued the move was necessary for security, allies, tanker operators and coastal states would need to decide whether to treat it as legitimate or as an unlawful act of coercion.
