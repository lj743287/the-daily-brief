What is a naval blockade and how would it work in Strait of Hormuz?

A practical guide to what a blockade would mean, how it might be enforced and why the Strait of Hormuz matters so much to global trade and energy flows.

A naval blockade is an attempt to stop ships entering or leaving a defined area, usually by using warships, aircraft and surveillance to enforce restrictions at sea. In practice, that means identifying, warning, inspecting or turning back vessels, and being ready to use force against those that do not comply.

In the Strait of Hormuz, that would be especially serious because the waterway is one of the world’s most important energy chokepoints. Large volumes of oil and liquefied gas move through it every day, so even a partial interruption can quickly affect prices, insurance costs and supply chains far beyond the Gulf.

Whether a blockade could be sustained would depend on naval capacity, international law, and whether other powers treated it as legitimate. Even short of a formal blockade, the mere threat can deter shipping, slow traffic and create the kind of uncertainty that markets and governments are already trying to avoid.
