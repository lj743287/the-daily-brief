What is a naval blockade and how would it work in Strait of Hormuz?

An explainer on the mechanics and limits of blockading one of the world’s most strategically sensitive waterways.

A naval blockade is an attempt to stop ships entering or leaving a designated area, usually by deploying warships, patrol aircraft and surveillance systems to intercept or warn vessels away. In the Strait of Hormuz, that would mean policing a narrow and heavily trafficked passage through which much of the Gulf's oil and gas moves.

In practice, a blockade can take several forms. A navy may seek to halt traffic completely, selectively inspect vessels, or create such a level of risk that shipping companies avoid the route altogether. The latter can be almost as disruptive as a formal closure, even if no declaration of war is made.

The strait is difficult to control because it is shared by multiple states, watched by international forces and used by commercial shipping from across the world. Any attempt to stop traffic would raise immediate legal, military and diplomatic questions, including who has authority to enforce it and how neutral vessels would be treated.

That is why talk of a blockade matters even before a single ship is stopped. The economic effect can begin with uncertainty: insurers raise premiums, companies reroute cargoes and energy markets price in disruption. The result is that a blockade can create damage long before it becomes a fully functioning military reality.
