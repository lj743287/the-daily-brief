Deep-fried food banned in new plans for school dinners

Ministers are proposing tighter school food standards, including limits on sugary puddings and deep-fried items. The changes are aimed at improving nutrition, but will draw scrutiny from schools and parents over cost and practicality.

Ministers are proposing tighter standards for school meals, including restrictions on deep-fried foods and sugary puddings. The plans are part of a broader push to improve nutrition in England’s schools and to reduce children’s exposure to food that is high in fat, salt and sugar.

The argument for the changes is straightforward: better school food can help children eat more healthily at a time when concerns over childhood obesity remain high. Supporters will say schools should be offering meals that set a benchmark for balanced diets, not simply reproducing cheaper convenience food.

But the practical questions are just as important. Schools will need to absorb any extra costs, adapt menus and ensure pupils will actually eat what is served. Parents and caterers are likely to ask whether the new rules are realistic in under-pressure school kitchens, and whether the policy will be backed by enough funding to make it work.
