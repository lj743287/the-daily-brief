Deep-fried food banned in new plans for school dinners

England’s school dinner rules are being tightened, with deep-fried food banned and more vegetables and whole grains required as ministers try to curb childhood obesity.

England’s school dinner rules are being tightened, with deep-fried food to be banned and schools told to provide more vegetables, whole grains and less sugar.

The changes are aimed at tackling childhood obesity and improving diet quality in classrooms. They form part of a wider effort to make school meals healthier and more consistent across the country.

What children eat at school matters because it can shape habits, nutrition and concentration, especially for pupils who rely heavily on school meals. The policy also signals a stronger state role in setting nutritional standards.

Some schools may welcome the clearer guidance, while others will worry about cost, kitchen capacity and whether pupils will accept the changes.

The question now is whether the reforms improve health in practice, not just on paper.
