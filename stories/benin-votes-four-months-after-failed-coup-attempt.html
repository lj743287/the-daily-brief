Benin votes four months after failed coup attempt

Benin holds a presidential election viewed as a test of stability after a foiled coup. With the outgoing president stepping down after two terms, the finance minister is widely seen as the frontrunner, amid opposition warnings over media freedom and fairness.

Benin held a presidential election four months after a foiled coup, a test of stability as the outgoing president steps down after two terms. The finance minister is widely seen as the frontrunner, positioning the contest as one of continuity versus change.

Security was tightened around key sites and electoral logistics were bolstered to reassure voters. Authorities said contingency plans were in place to address potential disruptions and to safeguard the count.

Opposition parties and activists have warned about media access and fairness, alleging advantages for the ruling camp. Domestic observers and civil society groups monitored polling stations to document any irregularities and support transparency.

Economic stewardship featured prominently in the campaign, with inflation, jobs and public finances at the forefront of voter concerns. The frontrunner’s record at the finance ministry drew both praise for stability and criticism over living costs.

Turnout will be closely watched as a barometer of public confidence in institutions following the failed coup. Regional bloc ECOWAS has urged respect for constitutional norms in a region unsettled by repeated military takeovers.

Preliminary results are expected within days. If no candidate secures an absolute majority, the constitution provides for a second‑round runoff between the top two contenders.
