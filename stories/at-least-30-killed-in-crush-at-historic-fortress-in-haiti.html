At least 30 killed in crush at historic fortress in Haiti

A crowd crush at the mountaintop Citadelle Laferrière left dozens dead and many injured, officials said. The incident at the UNESCO-listed site highlights the country’s severe security, governance and emergency-response challenges amid deepening instability.

At least 30 people have been killed in a crowd crush at the Citadelle Laferrière in northern Haiti, with dozens more injured, officials said. The death toll is expected to change as authorities account for those taken to clinics and hospitals and continue to review the scene.

The crush occurred during an event that drew large numbers of visitors to the mountaintop fortress, which sits above Cap-Haïtien and is one of Haiti’s best-known landmarks. The prime minister said the gathering had many young attendees. The precise chain of events remains unclear, and officials have not yet confirmed what triggered the fatal surge.

Authorities said an investigation is under way and that casualty figures could rise. The site’s elevation and narrow access routes complicated emergency access and evacuation, underscoring how geography and limited infrastructure can magnify risk during mass gatherings.

The disaster comes amid Haiti’s wider security and governance crisis, which has strained policing, healthcare and basic public services. Years of instability and gang-related violence have hampered movement around the country and weakened institutions, factors that can slow emergency response and hinder coordination when lives depend on speed.

The Citadelle Laferrière is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, built in the early 19th Century after Haitian independence and regarded as a powerful symbol of the world’s first Black republic. Its global cultural value attracts visitors when conditions allow, and Saturday’s tragedy will raise urgent questions about crowd management, site stewardship and the safety of public events at heritage locations.

Officials are expected to publish casualty lists, support families and review permits and safety plans for major gatherings. External partners may offer technical assistance, and UNESCO is likely to seek information from the authorities. With details still emerging, early claims about the cause should be treated cautiously; a fuller picture will depend on a formal inquiry and the testimonies of those who were there.
