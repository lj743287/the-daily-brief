Ukraine and Russia accuse each other of hundreds of ceasefire violations

Kyiv and Moscow traded blame for widespread breaches of a holiday truce, with President Volodymyr Zelensky vowing a “symmetric” response. The flare-ups, focused in the east and around Kharkiv, spotlight the fragility of frontline freezes and the limits of external pressure to pause hostilities.

Ukraine and Russia have accused each other of hundreds of breaches of a holiday ceasefire, underscoring how tenuous any pause in fighting remains more than two years into the war. Kyiv said Russian forces struck repeatedly despite the truce period, while President Volodymyr Zelensky signalled Ukraine would respond in kind. Moscow, for its part, blamed Ukrainian attacks for shattering the lull. The fiercest exchanges were reported along the eastern front and around the city of Kharkiv.

Both militaries issued statements cataloguing artillery and drone strikes, with Ukraine highlighting sustained pressure on positions in Donbas and the Kharkiv region, and Russia alleging shelling and sabotage attempts along the line of contact and in border areas. The claims could not be independently verified, and battlefield conditions make timely confirmation difficult. Each side accused the other of exploiting the truce to reposition forces and gather intelligence.

The flare-ups brought little respite to civilians. In Kharkiv, where air-raid sirens and intermittent bombardment have become routine, local authorities reported fresh damage to residential areas and infrastructure, further fraying public patience with short-lived pauses. Residents described subdued holiday observances and expressed scepticism that any truce would hold, reflecting the corrosive effect of repeated failed ceasefires on public trust.

International actors had urged restraint over the holiday period, hoping for a brief humanitarian calm to allow aid deliveries and evacuations. But the episode highlights the fragility of frontline freezes when neither party sees advantage in standing down and when there is no credible enforcement mechanism. In modern conflicts, religious or symbolic truces are often overtaken by battlefield calculations, and this war has proved no exception.

Mr Zelensky’s pledge of a ‘symmetric’ response suggests Ukraine will mirror the tempo and nature of Russian strikes rather than seek unilateral de-escalation, a stance likely aimed at deterrence as much as domestic reassurance. For both sides, drones, artillery and electronic warfare shape the rhythm of fighting, and short pauses can be used to rotate units, replenish munitions and probe for weak points. That logic favours rapid reversion to hostilities once any declared truce starts to fray.

Attention now turns to whether the clashes presage a broader intensification as the truce window closes, particularly in the Kharkiv sector and eastern approaches where pressure remains acute. The stakes are wider than the battlefield: ceasefire failures complicate aid access, heighten risks to civilians and dim prospects for even limited confidence-building measures. With neither camp inclined to step back, outside pressure appears to have limited leverage, and the coming days will test whether heightened exchanges harden into another sustained escalation.
