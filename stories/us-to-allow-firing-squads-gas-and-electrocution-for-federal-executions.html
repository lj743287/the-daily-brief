US to allow firing squads, gas and electrocution for federal executions

The US Department of Justice says it will permit firing squads, gas and electrocution for federal executions, a move it argues would strengthen the death penalty and deter crime. The policy marks a hardening of US punishment debates under Donald Trump.

The US Department of Justice says it will allow firing squads, gas and electrocution as methods for federal executions, marking a further hardening of the Trump administration's stance on capital punishment.

In a memo, the department said the move would strengthen the death penalty and deter crime. The policy change matters because it broadens the range of execution methods available to federal authorities at a time when the death penalty remains deeply contested in the US.

Supporters argue the shift gives states and federal prisons more flexibility, especially where lethal injection drugs are harder to obtain or have faced legal challenge. Critics are likely to see it as a retreat from efforts to limit state-sanctioned killing.

The move also fits a broader political pattern under Donald Trump, who has repeatedly sought to emphasise punitive criminal justice policy.

For campaigners against capital punishment, the practical effect may be to revive methods long associated with the past and with stark constitutional disputes. Any challenge to the policy would probably focus on legality, cruelty and whether the federal government is moving against the direction of international norms.

The debate is likely to intensify as the administration tries to frame the change as a deterrent and opponents present it as a moral and legal step backwards.
