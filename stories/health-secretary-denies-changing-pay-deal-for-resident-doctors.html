Health secretary denies changing pay deal for resident doctors

As a six-day strike in England ends, the government rejects claims of moving the goalposts. Prolonged industrial action has strained services and sharpened arguments over workforce retention and NHS funding.

Health Secretary Wes Streeting has denied changing the terms of a proposed pay deal for resident doctors, as a six-day strike across England ends at 06:59 on Monday. The government rejected claims it had moved the goalposts during negotiations, saying it remains committed to ending the dispute.

Doctors’ representatives have argued that ministers altered expectations as talks progressed, undermining trust and progress. The Department of Health maintains its stance has been consistent and that it wants a negotiated settlement, but within what it sees as responsible limits for the public finances. Neither side has indicated a breakthrough.

The latest walkout forced widespread disruption to routine care, with hospitals postponing planned treatments and reworking rotas to shore up urgent and emergency services. NHS leaders warn the cumulative impact of repeated stoppages has deepened backlogs and complicated recovery plans, even as urgent and critical care has continued.

At the heart of the dispute are pay, workload and retention. Resident doctors—known in the UK as junior doctors—say real-terms pay erosion and rota pressures are driving colleagues overseas and out of the profession, threatening patient care in the long term. Ministers counter that settlement levels must be affordable and balanced against wider NHS funding pressures.

The row is a test of the government’s approach to public sector pay and the health service’s workforce plan. With international competition for medical staff intensifying, the UK faces a strategic question: how to retain and motivate clinicians while keeping to tight fiscal plans and restoring momentum on NHS reform and productivity.

As the strike concludes, attention will turn to whether formal talks resume quickly and on what basis. Unions are weighing next steps, including the possibility of further action if they judge progress insufficient, while hospital leaders focus on rescheduling care and stabilising services. Any movement on a new offer, the role of independent pay advice, and signals from both sides in the coming days will shape whether the dispute cools or escalates.
