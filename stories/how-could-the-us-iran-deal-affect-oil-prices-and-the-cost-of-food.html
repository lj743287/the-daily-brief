How could the US-Iran deal affect oil prices and the cost of food?

The US-Iran deal has already pushed energy markets lower, but experts say the ripple effects could continue to affect oil prices, shipping and food costs for months, depending on whether the Strait of Hormuz fully reopens.

The US-Iran deal has already pushed energy markets lower, but the effects could continue to ripple through oil prices, shipping and food costs for months. Much will depend on whether the Strait of Hormuz fully reopens and whether tanker traffic returns to normal.

When oil falls, the impact does not stop at the petrol station. Transport, fertiliser and packaging costs all feed into the price of food, which means any sustained change in energy markets can affect household budgets more widely than people often expect.

Experts caution, however, that the relief may be partial and uneven. If buyers rush to rebuild stockpiles or if the deal frays, prices could stay volatile even after the headline risk has eased.

For consumers, the key question is whether the current fall in energy prices becomes a lasting trend. For now, the answer is not yet clear.
