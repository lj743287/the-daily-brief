Reports of mass civilian deaths after Nigerian air strike hits market

Amnesty International and local media say more than 100 civilians may have been killed when a Nigerian Air Force strike hit a market in Yobe state. Officials acknowledged a misfire targeting jihadists but have not provided full details. The incident will intensify scrutiny of counter-insurgency tactics and civilian protections.

Amnesty International and local media reported that more than 100 civilians may have been killed after a Nigerian Air Force strike hit a market in Yobe state. Officials acknowledged a misfire during an operation targeting jihadists but have not provided full details of the incident or casualties.

The air force has yet to release a comprehensive account of what happened, and casualty figures could not be independently verified. Humanitarian groups said access to the area is constrained by insecurity, complicating efforts to confirm the scale of the loss and to evacuate the wounded.

Witness accounts gathered by rights organisations suggest the strike occurred during a busy trading period, with the blast and shrapnel causing extensive casualties among vendors and shoppers. Local health facilities were reported to be overwhelmed, with some victims transferred to hospitals in neighbouring districts.

The episode adds to longstanding concerns over civilian protection in Nigeria’s counter-insurgency campaign against Boko Haram and Islamic State West Africa Province. The armed forces have previously faced criticism over air operations that struck civilian areas, underscoring the risks of targeting militants embedded among local populations.

Pressure is mounting for a transparent investigation, clear rules of engagement and improved targeting procedures. The reports follow a series of mass trials that have led to hundreds of sentences for alleged links to militant Islamists, reflecting the government’s wider security drive but also raising questions over due process and accountability.

Aid agencies warned that such incidents can undermine community trust, complicate intelligence-gathering and hinder humanitarian operations in the north-east. International partners are likely to press Abuja for credible findings and remedial measures to reduce the likelihood of further civilian harm.
