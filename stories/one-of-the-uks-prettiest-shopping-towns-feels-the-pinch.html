One of the UK’s ‘prettiest’ shopping towns feels the pinch

Cardigan’s retailers report rising costs and weak footfall, a microcosm of high-street challenges across Wales. Rates, energy bills and online competition continue to squeeze margins.

Cardigan, often cited as one of the UK’s prettiest shopping towns, is wrestling with the less picturesque realities of a fragile high street. Independent retailers and hospitality businesses say rising bills and subdued footfall are tightening margins, turning a place known for distinctive shopfronts and lively weekends into a test case for how Welsh town centres adapt to harsher economics.

Owners of shops, cafés and restaurants report that energy costs, business rates and wage pressures have climbed at the same time as customers have become more price‑sensitive. Many point to online retail and changing habits since the pandemic as steady drains on casual browsing and impulse purchases. The effect, traders say, is a thinner buffer against shocks and less room to invest in new lines, refurbishments or extra staff hours.

Why it matters goes beyond one town’s fortunes. Cardigan’s blend of independent boutiques, galleries and eateries has helped draw visitors to west Wales and sustain local jobs. If a destination with those advantages is struggling, communities with fewer attractions may face deeper challenges. The risk is a feedback loop of empty units, shorter trading weeks and weaker confidence, a pattern seen in high streets across the UK and far beyond as e‑commerce and inflation reshape spending.

There are competing views on what would help most. Some businesses argue that reliefs and grants available in Wales ease immediate pressures but do not address the structure of business rates or high fixed costs for small units. Others look to local measures—parking, public transport, events and street improvements—to stimulate dwell time and repeat visits. Policymakers, for their part, balance support for town centres with fiscal constraints, emphasising regeneration programmes and the need for retailers to build stronger digital offers.

Adaptation is under way. Traders are experimenting with shared spaces, pop‑ups, maker markets and coordinated promotions to create reasons to visit beyond routine shopping. More are investing in click‑and‑collect or local delivery to knit online discovery into in‑person sales. Hospitality venues are rethinking how long customers occupy tables, reflecting the tight margins in daytime trade, while interest in thrifting and repair culture is pulling new visitors onto the high street—even if the spend is spread differently.

The next few months will show whether seasonal tourism can offset structural headwinds. Energy prices, wage floors and the future of rates relief will shape decisions on hiring and opening hours, while councils and business groups consider how to sustain momentum past the summer. Cardigan’s experience suggests the path forward will be a mix of targeted policy, landlord flexibility and retailer ingenuity—turning pretty streets into productive ones without losing the character that drew people there in the first place.
