‘Irresponsible failure’: Google, Meta, Snap and Microsoft slam EU over child sexual abuse law lapse

The expiry of a key scanning exemption has reopened a hard contest between privacy rights and the detection of child abuse material online.

Google, Meta, Snap and Microsoft have attacked the EU after a key exemption that allowed some scanning of child sexual abuse material expired, reopening a bitter debate over privacy, safety and platform responsibility.

The companies say the lapse is a failure that risks weakening efforts to detect abuse material online. Privacy advocates, by contrast, have long argued that broad scanning powers can create dangerous precedents and expand surveillance beyond their original purpose.

The stakes are high because the internet’s biggest platforms sit at the centre of both enforcement and rights concerns. Regulators now face pressure to produce a system that protects children without handing firms or governments blanket powers over private communications.

What happens next will depend on whether Brussels can agree a durable legal framework before the issue becomes another prolonged fight between safety campaigners and civil liberties groups.
