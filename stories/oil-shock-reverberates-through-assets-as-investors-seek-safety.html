Oil shock reverberates through assets as investors seek safety

Energy disruption around the Strait of Hormuz and a fresh jump in US inflation to 3.3% have reset risk appetite, lifting oil and the dollar while pressuring equities. Airlines and energy-intensive sectors are under strain as input costs rise, and bond markets are reassessing the path of rates. Currency markets favour perceived havens amid a fragile geopolitical backdrop.

Crude benchmarks extended gains as supply uncertainty persisted, with traders factoring in longer and costlier routes for tankers and the risk of further interruptions. Higher oil prices have revived concerns that the disinflation trend could stall, complicating central bank calculus.

Equity markets reflected the squeeze, with travel and transport facing headwinds from rising fuel costs and potential operational disruption. By contrast, energy producers and some commodity-linked names found support from firmer prices, tempering broader index declines.

The latest US inflation print, driven by higher pump prices, nudged rate expectations, pushing up short-dated yields and reviving debate over how quickly monetary easing can proceed. European bond markets tracked the move, though growth concerns lent some support to longer maturities.

In currencies, the dollar outperformed as investors sought liquid havens, while sterling and the euro were mixed, balancing energy exposure against relatively resilient domestic data. Safe-haven flows also supported the yen and Swiss franc, although policy divergences capped gains.

Credit markets saw spreads widen modestly, reflecting a cautious tone rather than outright stress. Issuers with heavy energy exposure or discretionary consumer sensitivity underperformed, while investment-grade defensives held firmer amid a rotation toward quality.

Commodities beyond oil were mixed. Industrial metals reacted to the growth implications of higher energy costs, while gold benefited from risk aversion and its role as a store of value in periods of geopolitical strain.

Derivatives positioning showed increased demand for downside protection in equities and for call options in energy contracts, signalling a desire to hedge tail risks as the news flow remains volatile.

Market direction will hinge on the duration of energy route disruption and any sign of renewed diplomacy. Clearer timelines on central bank policy and evidence that second-round inflation effects are contained would be needed to stabilise risk sentiment more broadly.
