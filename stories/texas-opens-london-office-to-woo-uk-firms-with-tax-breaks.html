Texas opens London office to woo UK firms with tax breaks

The low‑tax US state is stepping up efforts to attract British corporate investment with subsidies and incentives. The move highlights post‑Brexit competition among jurisdictions to secure capital and jobs.

Texas has opened a London office to court British companies with tax incentives and subsidies, intensifying competition to attract foreign investment. The move underscores how US states are marketing themselves directly to overseas firms.

The pitch combines comparatively low taxes, streamlined regulation and sector‑specific sweeteners in areas such as energy, technology and manufacturing. State‑level incentives can complement federal programmes, shaping the calculus for capital‑intensive projects.

For the UK, the initiative lands in a crowded field. Post‑Brexit, regions within Britain, EU member states and US locales are all vying for mobile investment, offering sites, grants and workforce training to anchor jobs and supply chains.

The implications are mixed. Fresh commitments can bring high‑value employment and export capacity, but subsidy races risk shifting activity rather than raising overall investment. Policymakers will scrutinise how deals measure up on productivity and local spillovers.

Companies weighing a move must navigate exchange‑rate swings, access to talent, immigration rules and legal environments that differ between US states. Infrastructure, energy costs and proximity to customers are proving as decisive as headline tax rates.

Success will be judged by concrete announcements, timelines and job creation rather than ribbon‑cuttings alone. As jurisdictions compete, transparency over incentive packages and accountability for outcomes are likely to feature prominently in the debate.
