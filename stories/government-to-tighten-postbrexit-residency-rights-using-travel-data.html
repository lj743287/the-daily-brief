Government to tighten post‑Brexit residency rights using travel data

EU citizens who are no longer ‘continuously’ resident face loss of status under the Withdrawal Agreement. The plan has prompted concerns after errors in border data recently led to wrongful child‑benefit removals.

The government plans to tighten enforcement of post‑Brexit residency rights by using travel data to assess whether EU citizens remain “continuously” resident in the UK, with those deemed to have broken the rules at risk of losing their status under the Withdrawal Agreement.

The approach would involve matching border records with Home Office files to flag prolonged absences for caseworker review. People with pre‑settled or settled status could be asked to evidence residence or face curtailment, depending on their circumstances.

The plan has prompted concerns after errors in border data recently led to wrongful removal of child‑benefit payments in thousands of cases. Advocacy groups warn that any automated checks on residence risk misidentifying lawful absences and creating anxiety for long‑term residents.

While the Withdrawal Agreement allows for verification of residence and sets conditions for permitted absences, it also requires proportionate decision‑making and effective redress. The Independent Monitoring Authority and the courts may be engaged if systemic problems emerge, and the European Commission is likely to scrutinise compliance.

Those with irregular travel patterns, including students, carers and cross‑border workers, could be particularly exposed to data gaps or misunderstandings. Campaigners are urging clear guidance, advance notice and an opportunity to provide alternative evidence such as tenancy records, utility bills or employment documentation.

Ministers are expected to outline timelines, safeguards and appeal routes when the policy is confirmed. MPs have signalled they will seek assurances on data accuracy, the handling of edge cases and protections for families who have built their lives in the UK since before Brexit.
