Orbán era swept away by Péter Magyar's Hungary election landslide

Viktor Orbán’s 16-year rule has ended after a decisive defeat by Péter Magyar, an ex-party insider who persuaded a majority of voters to turn the page. The result is a major shift for Hungary and for Europe, with implications for the rule of law, relations with Brussels and the wider nationalist right.

Viktor Orbán’s long hold on power has been broken after Péter Magyar led the opposition to a landslide that swept away the governing order built over more than a decade and a half. The scale of the defeat leaves Orbán with little room to argue that this was a narrow protest vote; it was a clear instruction from a weary electorate to move on.

Magyar’s rise has been remarkable because he is not a conventional opposition veteran. An ex-insider from Orbán’s political orbit, he managed to present himself as both credible and disruptive, turning disillusionment with corruption allegations, state capture and democratic backsliding into a broad coalition for change.

The result matters well beyond Budapest. Orbán has been one of Europe’s most stubborn nationalist leaders, repeatedly clashing with Brussels over the rule of law, judicial independence and migration policy, while also offering a model for other populist right-wing movements across the continent.

For the European Union, the change could be significant. A new government in Hungary may be more open to compromise on funding, institutional checks and relations with the bloc, though the extent of any reset will depend on how quickly Magyar can translate election momentum into governing authority and whether the old networks resist change.

Hungary’s political system was remade under Orbán through control of institutions, media influence and a hard-edged culture-war politics. Undoing that will not be simple, even with a clear electoral mandate, because entrenched interests and a polarised electorate can outlast a single election.

The next test is whether Magyar can turn a symbolic victory into stable government. He inherits high expectations at home, strained relations with allies and a country whose politics have been shaped for years by confrontation with Brussels and by Orbán’s appeal to nationalist voters.
