Orbán era swept away by Péter Magyar's Hungary election landslide

Péter Magyar has defeated Viktor Orbán in a historic Hungarian election landslide, ending Orbán’s 16-year rule and opening a new phase for a country long at the centre of Europe’s political and security anxieties. The result will reverberate in Brussels and Moscow alike, and tests whether a formerly dominant nationalist machine can be displaced at the ballot box.

Péter Magyar has ended Viktor Orbán’s long dominance of Hungarian politics, in a result that marks the most significant change of government in Budapest in 16 years. The 45-year-old former insider persuaded a majority of voters to turn away from a leader who had built one of Europe’s most durable nationalist machines.

The scale of the defeat matters well beyond Hungary. Orbán has been a regular irritant in Brussels and one of the European Union’s most awkward members on issues from rule of law to sanctions on Russia. His removal will be read in European capitals as a political correction, while in Moscow it is likely to be viewed with far less enthusiasm.

For many Hungarians, the vote appears to have been driven by frustration with the pace of change at home and by exhaustion with a politics dominated by one figure for so long. Magyar’s rise also suggests that even entrenched incumbents can be displaced if an opposition figure can unite discontent behind a credible alternative.

The result is still likely to raise questions about what kind of government Magyar will try to build, and how quickly he can turn an anti-Orbán mandate into a workable programme. He inherits a country whose domestic politics, media landscape and institutions have all been shaped by Orbán’s rule since 2010.

Hungary’s position will also matter for Europe’s security debate, not least because the country sits at the junction of several current anxieties: Russia’s war on Ukraine, energy supply and the EU’s own internal cohesion. A change of leadership will not resolve those tensions on its own, but it does alter the balance in a bloc that has struggled to contain Orbán’s veto power.

For now, the scale of the landslide is the story. A political order that once looked entrenched has been broken at the ballot box, and the consequences will be felt in Budapest, Brussels and Moscow alike.
