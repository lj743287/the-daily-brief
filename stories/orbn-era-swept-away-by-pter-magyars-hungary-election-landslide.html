Orbán era swept away by Péter Magyar's Hungary election landslide

Péter Magyar has ended Viktor Orbán’s 16-year rule in Hungary, with a landslide that overturns one of Europe’s most durable nationalist governments and could reshape the EU’s response to Moscow, rule-of-law disputes and enlargement politics.

Péter Magyar has ended Viktor Orbán’s 16-year grip on power in Hungary, overturning one of Europe’s most durable nationalist governments and setting up a major political reset in Budapest.

The result is likely to reverberate well beyond Hungary’s borders. Orbán has long been one of the European Union’s most defiant leaders, clashing with Brussels over rule-of-law standards, immigration and support for Ukraine, while keeping a pragmatic line to Moscow.

Magyar, a 45-year-old former insider turned insurgent, persuaded a majority of voters that change was worth the risk. His win suggests a sharp public appetite for a less polarising style of government after years of bitter political division.

For the EU, the shift could alter debates on sanctions, enlargement and funding, especially if Hungary’s next government is more willing to align with its partners. It also raises questions about how quickly Orbán’s political machine can adapt after such a heavy defeat.

The immediate test will be whether Magyar can turn a protest vote into a workable governing majority and whether he can hold together the broad coalition that brought him victory.

If he succeeds, Hungary could move into a new phase in its relations with both Brussels and Moscow; if he fails, Orbán’s influence may still shape the country from opposition.
