Mass trial for 486 alleged gang members begins in El Salvador

A court in El Salvador has begun the mass trial of 486 alleged gang members, a case that highlights President Nayib Bukele’s hard-line approach and renewed human-rights concerns over due process.

A court in El Salvador has begun a mass trial of 486 alleged gang members, in one of the clearest examples yet of President Nayib Bukele’s hard-line campaign against gang violence.

The case has drawn renewed concern from human-rights groups because collective proceedings on this scale raise questions about due process, access to legal representation and the ability of defendants to answer individual allegations properly.

Footage released by the attorney general’s office shows large groups of men in prison attending the hearing by video link, underlining the extraordinary scale of the prosecution.

Supporters of Mr Bukele argue that his emergency powers have restored public order in a country once terrorised by gangs; critics say the crackdown has come at the expense of legal safeguards and fair trials.

What happens next will be watched closely both in El Salvador and abroad, because the outcome may shape how far governments elsewhere are willing to go in the name of security.
