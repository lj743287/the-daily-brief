Energy shock risk rises as US–Iran talks fail and tankers idle

The collapse of negotiations raises the prospect of a prolonged oil and gas squeeze, with tankers stranded in the Gulf and insurers wary. Traders expect further price volatility and higher borrowing costs if flows through Hormuz remain constrained.

The collapse of US–Iran negotiations has kept oil and gas markets on edge, with growing signs of tankers idling or diverting as charterers weigh legal and safety risks in the Gulf.

War‑risk insurance premiums and sanctions exposure are complicating fixture activity, while some shipowners and cargo interests wait for clearer assurances before attempting the Strait of Hormuz.

Energy traders report sharper intraday swings across crude, products and liquefied natural gas, with financing conditions tightening as margin requirements rise and lenders price in higher volatility.

While spare capacity and strategic stockpiles could provide partial relief, quality mismatches, limited pipeline alternatives and refinery maintenance schedules constrain how quickly supply can be re‑routed.

The squeeze is transmitting through financial channels: a stronger dollar and pricier trade finance are pressuring importers and frontier borrowers, while high‑yield energy credits and freight exposures are being repriced.

Absent a credible maritime security framework and insurance cover, risk premia are likely to persist, even if diplomatic contacts resume, as shippers and buyers demand firmer guarantees before normalising flows.
