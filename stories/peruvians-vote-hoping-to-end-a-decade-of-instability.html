Peruvians vote hoping to end a decade of instability

Peru heads to the polls amid record fragmentation, surging crime and deep distrust of institutions. With 35 presidential candidates and a new congress to select, the outcome will shape economic policy and the country’s capacity to govern after years of turmoil.

Peruvians are voting in presidential and congressional elections that many hope will draw a line under a decade of political upheaval. A record-fragmented field of 35 presidential candidates and dozens of congressional lists has left polls volatile and expectations of a run-off high. The vote follows years of rolling crises that have eroded trust in institutions and coincides with mounting fears over crime and economic drift.

The past decade has seen repeated clashes between the executive and a combative Congress, frequent impeachment attempts and rapid presidential turnover. Anti-corruption investigations have touched successive leaders while deadly protests, states of emergency and allegations of abuses have deepened polarisation. Pollsters report historically low confidence in the political class, a backdrop that favours outsiders pledging to upend the status quo but complicates any effort to govern.

Under Peru’s two-round system, the lack of a clear frontrunner means a second ballot is widely expected. The new Congress, elected by proportional representation, is likely to be splintered among numerous small parties, raising the risk of legislative gridlock from day one. Campaigns have centred on law-and-order pledges, promises of constitutional changes and competing narratives over how to repair a frayed social contract.

The stakes extend well beyond Lima. As one of the world’s top copper producers, Peru’s policy direction on mining, taxation and community consultation will ripple through global supply chains and the energy transition. Investors are watching for clarity on permits, royalties and protest management, while voters weigh proposals ranging from targeted stimulus and formalisation of labour to tighter controls on illegal mining and improved delivery of basic services. The central bank’s credibility remains a relative anchor, but growth has been hit by social unrest and recent climate shocks.

Public security dominates the campaign, with candidates vowing tougher sentences, expanded police powers and border controls in response to rising extortion, contract killings and trafficking. Supporters see decisive action as overdue; critics warn of due-process risks and the potential for heavy-handed measures to inflame tensions. The electoral authority has deployed extra security at polling stations and urged caution over misinformation, while international observers are monitoring the process.

Attention will now turn to preliminary results, potential legal challenges and the bargaining needed to assemble a working majority in Congress. A fragmented outcome could prolong uncertainty, with early priorities likely to include the budget, a security package, stalled infrastructure projects and efforts to defuse mining-related conflicts. For Peru’s neighbours and trading partners, the result will signal whether one of Latin America’s most open economies can re-establish predictability after years of turmoil—or whether institutional deadlock will endure into a fraught run-off.
