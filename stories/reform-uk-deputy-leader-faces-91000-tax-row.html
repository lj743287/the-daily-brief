Reform UK deputy leader faces £91,000 tax row

A company owned by Richard Tice allegedly failed to pay dividend taxes, described by the party as a minor administrative error. The case feeds wider scrutiny of political figures’ business affairs and compliance.

Reform UK’s deputy leader Richard Tice is facing questions over a reported £91,000 shortfall in dividend tax linked to a property company he owns, in a case his party has characterised as a minor administrative error. The claims, first set out in weekend reporting, have thrust the businessman-turned-politician’s private affairs into the spotlight amid heightened scrutiny of political figures’ financial conduct.

According to those reports, the liability relates to tax due on dividends associated with the company, which was not paid in full. Reform UK has said the issue is administrative in nature. There has been no public indication of any formal enforcement action, and neither Mr Tice nor the party has provided detailed documentation of the transactions at issue.

Dividend income in the UK is typically taxed at individual rates through the self-assessment system, though record-keeping, timing and declaration requirements can become complex where closely held companies are involved. When payments are missed or delayed, HM Revenue & Customs can apply interest and, in some circumstances, penalties, in addition to any outstanding sums.

For a political party that has campaigned on tax, regulation and accountability, the episode carries reputational risk. Opponents are likely to question governance standards and personal compliance, while supporters may look for swift clarification to preserve credibility around Reform UK’s economic messaging and stewardship claims.

The row also speaks to a broader trend: voters and markets are demanding greater transparency over the business interests of elected and would-be elected officials. Recent reforms to corporate registrations and ongoing efforts to tighten oversight aim to bolster trust, but controversies involving dividends, loans and related-party dealings continue to test confidence in the UK’s governance ecosystem.

It is not yet clear whether HMRC will open a formal inquiry into the matter. Any resolution would be expected to involve confirmation of liabilities, potential interest and, where applicable, penalties, alongside amended filings. With political and business scrutiny intensifying, pressure will grow on Mr Tice and Reform UK to set out the timeline, remedial steps and assurances designed to prevent a recurrence.
