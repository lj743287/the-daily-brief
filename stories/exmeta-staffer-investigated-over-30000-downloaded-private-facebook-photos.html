Ex‑Meta staffer investigated over 30,000 downloaded private Facebook photos

Meta said a former employee was fired amid suspicions they exfiltrated tens of thousands of user images. The case highlights insider‑risk controls and the sensitivity of personal data even within trusted teams.

Meta says a former employee is under investigation over suspicions they downloaded about 30,000 private Facebook photos, and that the individual has been dismissed. The case, first reported by the BBC, centres on alleged exfiltration of user images and has renewed scrutiny of insider risks at large platforms.

While high‑profile breaches often involve external attackers, the incident underlines the sensitivity of personal data within trusted teams and the difficulty of calibrating access for staff who need it to do their jobs. Photos and associated metadata are among the most intimate categories of user information and carry elevated risks if mishandled.

Meta has not disclosed detailed findings from its internal probe. Typically, investigations of this kind examine access logs, approval workflows and data transfer patterns, and assess whether controls such as data loss prevention, role‑based access and anomaly detection were effective or need strengthening.

Regulatory obligations may follow. Under European data protection law, companies must assess whether an incident is likely to pose a risk to individuals’ rights and freedoms, notify authorities where required and, in some cases, inform affected users. Meta’s lead EU regulator is Ireland’s Data Protection Commission, though other national authorities can engage.

The episode comes amid wider industry efforts to curb insider threats with stricter least‑privilege access, just‑in‑time permissions, stronger segregation of data sets and continuous monitoring, especially for teams handling production systems. Large platforms increasingly pair technical controls with staff training, deterrence and post‑employment restrictions.

Meta has said the matter is being investigated and that the employee has been fired. Whether further disclosures, user notifications or regulatory actions follow will turn on what the probe concludes about the scope of access, any onward sharing and the adequacy of safeguards in place at the time.
