China leads in some AI fronts, the US in others

A BBC assessment finds each side gaining ground in different layers of the AI stack and applications. The race remains fluid, with policy, chips and capital likely to reshape the balance again.

A BBC assessment finds leadership split across layers of the AI stack: chips and compute capacity, model development, enterprise adoption and sector‑specific applications. The balance remains fluid, shaped by policy, capital costs and access to advanced hardware.

China’s strengths include rapid commercial deployment, integration of AI into manufacturing and services, and large‑scale adoption in retail, logistics and governance. A dense ecosystem of domestic model providers and integrators supports diffusion, and industrial policy channels funding to applied projects and data infrastructure.

The US retains an edge at the frontier of model capabilities, fuelled by leading chip designers, hyperscale cloud platforms, research universities and deep pools of venture capital. Open‑source communities and partnerships with industry continue to accelerate benchmarks and tools, even as access rules tighten.

Hardware remains a bottleneck. Export controls limit China’s access to the most advanced accelerators and high‑bandwidth memory, prompting workarounds such as clustering, optimisation for inference and custom accelerators. The US, for its part, faces vulnerabilities around talent immigration, permitting for new datacentres and grid capacity.

Policy and capital flows are pivotal. Subsidies and tax credits influence fab construction and packaging capacity; standards for transparency and safety shape compliance costs. Both sides are courting the global south with cloud services and developer ecosystems, while allies debate how to align on compute governance and model testing.

Given these cross‑currents, advantage may swing with each generation of chips, shifts in financing conditions and the stringency of safety rules. Scientific collaboration endures in some areas despite political tensions, but a deeper decoupling would raise costs and slow diffusion. Watch export‑control updates, memory supply chains and energy availability as leading indicators.
