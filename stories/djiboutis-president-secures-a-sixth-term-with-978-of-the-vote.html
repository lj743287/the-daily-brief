Djibouti’s president secures a sixth term with 97.8% of the vote

Ismail Omar Guelleh extended his 27‑year rule in a vote boycotted by the main opposition. The result underscores continuity in a strategically located state that hosts foreign bases at the mouth of the Red Sea.

Djibouti’s long‑serving president, Ismail Omar Guelleh, secured a sixth term with an official tally of 97.8%, extending more than a quarter‑century in power. The main opposition boycotted the poll, leaving the result in little doubt and signalling continuity in the Horn of Africa state.

The election underscored a political order marked by strong executive control and limited competition. With state institutions firmly aligned behind the presidency, policy direction is expected to remain stable in the near term.

Djibouti’s outsized strategic importance stems from its location at the mouth of the Red Sea, near the Bab el‑Mandeb strait. It hosts multiple foreign military facilities and serves as a logistics hub for operations in the Gulf of Aden and beyond.

The economy relies heavily on port services, transshipment and infrastructure financed in part by external partners, including large Chinese‑backed projects. Supporters argue that stability has underpinned growth; critics warn about debt and the concentration of power.

Rights groups have long raised concerns about constraints on media, civil society and opposition activity. The boycott will reinforce questions about political openness and the scope for peaceful alternation of power.

International partners are likely to prioritise pragmatic engagement on security and trade. Domestically, attention will turn to cabinet appointments, port concessions and regional diplomacy as Djibouti positions itself amid shifting Red Sea dynamics.
