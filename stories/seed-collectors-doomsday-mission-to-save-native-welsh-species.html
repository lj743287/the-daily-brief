Seed collectors’ ‘doomsday’ mission to save native Welsh species

Two conservationists are banking seeds to preserve biodiversity as climate and land‑use pressures bite. Their work aims to safeguard habitats and future restoration efforts.

Two Welsh conservationists are methodically gathering seeds from the country’s native flora, banking them against the twin threats of climate change and habitat loss. Their work feeds into the global push to secure genetic diversity now so that future restoration has a living library to draw upon.

The pair target a mix of coastal, lowland and upland species—plants of dune systems, hay meadows and Atlantic oakwoods among them—prioritising those with fragmented ranges or declining habitats. Collections are carefully timed to catch viable seed, avoiding over‑harvesting and recording provenance to preserve ecological integrity.

Seeds are cleaned, dried and stored at low temperatures so they can remain viable for decades. For species whose seeds do not tolerate conventional drying, small research batches may be cryopreserved or maintained in living collections, often in partnership with specialist gardens and research institutes.

The Welsh effort is closely linked to the Millennium Seed Bank partnership and to local botanic gardens and wildlife trusts. That collaboration brings shared protocols, quality control and a route for Welsh samples to sit alongside collections from across the world, improving the chances that rare traits are not lost.

Changing weather patterns are already altering flowering and fruiting times, complicating collection schedules. Land‑use shifts add pressure, with grasslands still vulnerable to nutrient enrichment and scrub encroachment, and coastal species facing erosion and sea‑level rise. Securing material now, collectors say, buys time while habitats are restored.

Banked seeds are not an end in themselves. The goal is to underpin practical recovery: supplying plants for dune stabilisation, meadow creation, riverbank work and climate‑ready trials. By keeping Welsh genetics in Welsh projects wherever possible, the team hopes to retain local character while giving threatened habitats a chance to rebound.
