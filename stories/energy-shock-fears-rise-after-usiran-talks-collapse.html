Energy shock fears rise after US–Iran talks collapse

With large numbers of tankers stranded and the Strait of Hormuz only partially moving, oil and gas prices are poised for renewed pressure. Markets are bracing for higher inflation, tighter financial conditions and spillovers to aviation, agriculture and freight.

The breakdown of US–Iran talks has reignited worries over a fresh energy shock, with many tankers still waiting for clearance and the Strait of Hormuz operating only partially. The immediate effect has been to re‑embed a risk premium across oil and gas benchmarks as traders reassess the likelihood of sustained supply interruptions and higher transport costs.

The bottlenecks are simultaneously physical and financial. Vessel queues and selective transits are constraining loadings and deliveries, while war‑risk insurance premia and day rates have climbed. Liquefied natural gas cargos, which depend on reliable scheduling, are especially exposed to disruption, and refiners face a tougher task juggling crude slates and maintenance timetables.

Macro balances are shifting with the shock. Headline inflation has accelerated in the US to its fastest pace in almost two years, according to the BBC, complicating the case for early interest‑rate cuts. Higher fuel bills squeeze real incomes and can dent discretionary spending, while emerging markets dependent on imported energy contend with currency and balance‑of‑payments pressures.

Sectoral spillovers are already visible. Airports Council International Europe has warned of potential jet‑fuel tightness if Hormuz remains constrained, forcing airlines to plan schedule adjustments and tankering strategies. Agriculture is bracing for sustained fertiliser and transport costs, and freight operators face complex legal and insurance choices, not least amid advisories telling tankers not to pay any Iranian levies.

There are cushions, but none is a panacea. Some Gulf producers can redirect limited volumes via pipelines that bypass Hormuz, and strategic reserves in the US and Asia provide a buffer against acute shortages. Yet inventories were already being drawn earlier in the year, OPEC+ policy is calibrated to a different set of assumptions, and refinery outages can amplify local shortages of diesel and jet fuel.

For investors, the path ahead hinges on whether secure maritime corridors are established, how sanctions are enforced, and what OPEC+ signals in the coming weeks. Energy price volatility typically steepens funding costs for cyclical sectors and benefits producers and energy‑linked credit, while extended disruption could keep yield curves elevated and complicate central banks’ disinflation plans.
