Could a digital twin make you into a 'superworker'?

The spread of digital twins in workplaces is being sold as a productivity leap, but the idea raises legal and ethical questions about surveillance, labour rights and control.

Digital twins are being promoted as a way to make workers more productive, but the idea also raises uncomfortable questions about surveillance and control.

In principle, a digital copy of a worker’s patterns and behaviour could help organisations plan tasks more efficiently or automate repetitive decisions; in practice, it may also give employers a deeper view of how staff behave.

That matters because workplace technology often arrives as a productivity tool first and a labour issue second, leaving questions about consent, ownership of data and employee rights to be settled later.

If firms want to use such systems at scale, they will have to confront the possibility that they are creating not just a new tool, but a new legal minefield.
