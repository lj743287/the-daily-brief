'Every drop of water counts': Fear for the future of Argentina's glaciers

Glaciers in the Andes act as natural reservoirs, releasing meltwater that underpins river systems and sustains communities during dry months. As temperatures rise and precipitation patterns shift, researchers warn that these ice stores are retreating, sharpening worries that every drop of water now counts.

The implications are broad. Reduced glacial mass threatens the reliability of supplies for households and farms, increases stress on urban networks during droughts, and complicates flood management when extreme weather hits. Hydropower, a key component of Argentina’s energy mix, also becomes more vulnerable as seasonal flows grow less predictable.

Tourism and local livelihoods add to the stakes. Iconic landscapes draw visitors from around the world, yet shrinking ice and safety concerns can dampen demand and disrupt jobs. Conservationists caution that short‑term development pressures risk undermining long‑term environmental and economic resilience.

Policymakers face hard choices over funding for monitoring, adaptation and protection, alongside broader efforts to cut emissions. International cooperation and access to climate finance will be critical, but so too will local measures to manage water more efficiently and protect high‑mountain ecosystems.
