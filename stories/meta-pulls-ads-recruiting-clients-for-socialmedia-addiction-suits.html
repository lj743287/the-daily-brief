Meta pulls ads recruiting clients for social‑media addiction suits

Facebook’s owner removed adverts seeking litigants after a landmark California trial. The move comes amid intensifying scrutiny of platforms’ impacts on mental health.

Meta has removed Facebook adverts that recruited potential claimants for lawsuits alleging social‑media addiction harms, following a landmark trial in California, according to the BBC.

The adverts targeted users who said heavy platform use affected their wellbeing. Their removal was presented as an enforcement of advertising policies in media reports. Meta has rejected claims that its services are designed to harm, even as it defends multiple cases in US courts.

Plaintiffs argue that engagement‑maximising features contribute to anxiety, depression and sleep disruption, particularly among teenagers. Researchers and clinicians continue to debate causality and confounding factors, and regulators are scrutinising youth‑safety measures across major platforms.

Taking down the adverts may limit law firms’ ability to recruit new claimants via Meta’s own services, but existing actions continue. Solicitors are likely to pivot to search advertising, television spots and third‑party platforms to reach potential litigants.

The move comes amid wider changes: platforms have introduced teen‑specific defaults, added parental controls and adjusted recommendation systems, while lawmakers in the US and Europe weigh age‑assurance mandates and design‑code obligations.

The litigation landscape ranges from consumer‑protection and product‑liability theories to claims about deceptive design. Outcomes in early cases will influence disclosure standards, risk warnings and how platforms balance engagement with duty‑of‑care expectations.
