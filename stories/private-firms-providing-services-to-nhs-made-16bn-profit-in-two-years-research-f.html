Private firms providing services to NHS made £1.6bn profit in two years, research finds

Research on £12bn of NHS contracts says private providers made £1.6bn in profit over two years, renewing pressure on ministers to curb profiteering in health outsourcing.

Private companies providing services to the NHS made £1.6bn in profit over two years on contracts worth £12bn, according to research that is likely to intensify pressure on ministers over outsourcing. The findings have revived long-running arguments about how much public money should flow to firms delivering healthcare and related services.

The report also says a significant share of contracts went to firms with overseas ownership, including businesses linked to tax havens. That will sharpen concerns among critics that the health service is not getting full value for money, particularly when hospitals and patients are already under strain.

The political issue is not simply whether private providers should be involved, but under what terms. Supporters of outsourcing argue it can add capacity and expertise; opponents say the profit motive can distort priorities and drain resources away from care.

The government will now face renewed pressure to set clearer limits on returns and tougher rules on transparency. The challenge is to answer the profit question without disrupting services that still rely on outside providers.
