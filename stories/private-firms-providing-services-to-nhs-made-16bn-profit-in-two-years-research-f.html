Private firms providing services to NHS made £1.6bn profit in two years, research finds

New research revives the long-running argument over outsourcing in the NHS, with MPs calling the level of profit extracted from public contracts “scandalous” and demanding tighter limits.

Private firms providing services to the NHS made £1.6bn in profit over two years, according to new research that has reignited the argument over outsourcing in the health service. The figure is likely to sharpen scrutiny of how public contracts are awarded and what the taxpayer receives in return.

Supporters of outsourcing say private providers can help the NHS manage demand and fill gaps in capacity. Critics argue that excessive profits drain money from frontline care and leave the health service dependent on commercial arrangements that are not always transparent or good value.

The political pressure is now on ministers to justify where the line is drawn between partnership and profit extraction. If MPs succeed in pushing for tighter limits, the debate could reshape how much of the NHS is delivered by outside firms and under what terms.
