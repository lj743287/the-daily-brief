Golden eagles’ return to England secures government backing

A £1m plan could see reintroductions begin next year, subject to habitat and stakeholder work. Supporters cite ecosystem benefits, while farmers and landowners seek assurances on livestock impacts.

Golden eagles could be reintroduced to England as early as next year after the government confirmed £1m to develop a programme of releases, subject to habitat checks, licensing and local agreement. Officials say the funding is intended to accelerate preparatory work so that a first phase can proceed if ecological and community conditions are met.

The species has been largely absent from England for decades and is now mostly confined to Scotland within the UK. Conservationists argue that returning a top predator to suitable upland landscapes would help restore natural processes and signal progress on nature recovery commitments, while critics warn that the practicalities are complex and long term.

Supporters point to potential ecosystem benefits, from scavenging that removes carrion to the control of abundant prey such as rabbits, as well as the prospect of wildlife tourism in rural areas. They also note that golden eagles are sensitive to disturbance and require extensive, connected habitats, making any reintroduction a test of England’s wider land management ambitions.

Farmers’ groups and landowners are seeking assurances on risks to lambs and gamebirds, and on how conflicts would be handled. Evidence from previous projects suggests predation on healthy livestock is limited, but not impossible, particularly in harsh weather or where alternative food is scarce. Mitigation measures typically discussed include carcass management, seasonal guidance during lambing and clearly defined routes for reporting concerns.

If the plan advances, the next steps would include selecting release areas in upland England, securing birds from sustainable sources, fitting satellite tags for monitoring and agreeing protocols with estates and local authorities. The effort would sit alongside other raptor recoveries in the UK and across Europe, where targeted protections and reintroductions have driven gradual returns of once-rare birds of prey.

Key tests over the coming months will be the outcome of habitat assessments, the strength of stakeholder agreements and the robustness of measures to deter illegal persecution. A green light later this year could see trial releases in 2025; delays would likely reflect the need for further consent-building. Either way, the decision will be read as a marker of how far the government is prepared to go to reconcile rural livelihoods with large-scale nature restoration.
