Meta pulls Facebook ads recruiting for social-media addiction lawsuits

The move follows a high-profile legal defeat in California. It underscores intensifying legal and reputational risks over platform design and youth harm.

Meta has pulled Facebook adverts that sought to recruit people to join lawsuits over alleged social‑media addiction, days after a high‑profile legal defeat in California. The decision removes a prominent channel used by law firms to find potential claimants on the very platform whose design and impact are at issue.

The withdrawn adverts typically promoted class‑action or mass‑claim efforts focused on youth harm and compulsive use, a growing area of contention for the tech sector. Their removal highlights the difficult balance for a platform that profits from advertising while hosting promotions that could materially increase its legal exposure.

The move follows a landmark trial loss in California centred on claims that platform features contribute to addictive behaviour and mental‑health harms among young users. That ruling is part of a wave of litigation in the United States, where parents, school districts and state officials have accused large platforms of prioritising engagement over safety. Meta disputes allegations that its products are defective or unlawfully harmful, and has pointed to parental controls and safety tools it says are designed to protect teens.

Why it matters extends beyond one company’s ad decisions. By curbing legal‑recruitment campaigns, Meta will please some advertisers and users fatigued by claims marketing, but critics are likely to argue it impedes access to justice by limiting how potential claimants learn about their rights. The change could invite questions from regulators about how ad‑policy enforcement intersects with competition, consumer protection and transparency obligations.

Scrutiny of youth protection online is intensifying globally. European policymakers have tightened rules on risk management and ad transparency for very large platforms, while the UK’s Online Safety Act places specific duties around child safety. Separately, Greece plans to ban social media use for under‑15s from next year, following similar moves announced in France and Spain, reflecting a broad political shift towards stricter controls on minors’ digital environments.

What happens next will likely hinge on appeals in the California case and the pace of related litigation now moving through US courts. Law firms can redirect recruitment to search, television and rival social platforms, reducing the practical impact but not the legal pressure. For Meta, the immediate test is whether its ad‑policy stance is applied consistently and transparently; the longer‑term challenge is evidencing that product design and safeguards reduce risks for young people in ways that satisfy courts and regulators.
