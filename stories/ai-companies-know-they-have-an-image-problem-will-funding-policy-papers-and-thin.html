AI companies know they have an image problem. Will funding policy papers and thinktanks dig them out?

The AI industry is investing heavily in public-policy influence as scrutiny grows over safety, labour impacts and market power.

AI companies are investing heavily in thinktanks, policy papers and public debate as scrutiny grows over safety, labour displacement and market power. The industry clearly understands that technical progress alone will not settle public concern about what frontier AI is becoming.

That matters because the argument over AI is no longer just about product performance. It is about who gets to shape the rules, who funds the expertise behind them and whether public policy is being informed by independent analysis or by companies seeking legitimacy.

The use of policy influence is not unusual in itself, but the scale and speed of AI investment have made it more visible. As the technology becomes more powerful, the fight over reputation, regulation and narrative is likely to become as important as the race for capability.

What remains uncertain is whether this strategy can rebuild trust. Funding papers may improve access and credibility in policy circles, but it will not by itself answer the harder questions about control, accountability and the social effects of increasingly capable systems.
