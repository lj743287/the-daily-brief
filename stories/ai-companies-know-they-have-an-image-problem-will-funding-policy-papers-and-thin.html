AI companies know they have an image problem. Will funding policy papers and thinktanks dig them out?

OpenAI and its rivals are trying to shape the public debate on AI through policy papers and thinktanks, but growing concern over jobs, cyber risks and trust may be harder to manage.

OpenAI and other major AI companies are trying to reshape the public debate around artificial intelligence through policy papers, thinktanks and Washington outreach. The effort is a sign that the industry recognises it has an image problem as concern grows over jobs, cyber risks and trust.

The timing is telling. Rather than focusing only on product launches or new data centres, some of the biggest firms are now trying to influence the policy conversation itself. OpenAI’s recent paper on an “industrial policy for the intelligence age” is one example of a broader attempt to present the technology as socially beneficial and politically manageable.

But a communications campaign can only go so far when public anxiety is rising. Polling and wider scepticism suggest that worries about job displacement, misinformation and security are not going away simply because companies fund sympathetic research or open more policy-friendly offices.

The larger question is whether these efforts are about genuine engagement or reputation management. In practice, they are likely to be both. The industry wants rules it can live with, but it also wants a better story to tell about why its power should be trusted.
