AI companies know they have an image problem. Will funding policy papers and thinktanks dig them out?

The major AI firms are trying to manage a public backlash by reframing their technology as a social and economic good, even as concern over its risks continues to rise.

Major AI companies are spending heavily on policy work as public unease about the technology grows.

The push is not just about one report or one thinktank. It is about trying to shape the debate around jobs, safety, competition and regulation at a moment when AI firms are becoming more powerful and more visible in politics.

That in itself is a sign of the problem. Companies that once marketed themselves as neutral builders of useful tools now face suspicion over whether they will automate work too quickly, concentrate too much power or move faster than safeguards can keep up.

Funding policy papers may help these firms sound engaged and responsible, but reputational repair is harder than messaging. The deeper question is whether they can persuade governments and the public that their economic ambitions are compatible with tighter oversight and slower, safer deployment.
