AI companies know they have an image problem. Will funding policy papers and thinktanks dig them out?

As public scepticism rises, the biggest AI firms are leaning harder on policy papers, lobbying and institutional influence.

The biggest AI companies increasingly know they have an image problem. As public scepticism rises, they are leaning harder on policy papers, thinktanks and institutional influence in an attempt to shape how governments and the public think about the technology.

That strategy reflects a recognition that technical progress alone does not guarantee trust. AI firms are now trying to present themselves as responsible actors with answers on jobs, safety and regulation, while also pushing for a policy environment that is favourable to their business models.

The concern is that this looks less like a public conversation and more like a contest over agenda-setting. If AI companies fund the debates that are meant to scrutinise them, the result may be more influence, but not necessarily more credibility.

What happens next will depend on whether policymakers treat these interventions as useful expertise or as reputational management. As AI becomes more powerful, the struggle over who gets to define the risks may matter almost as much as the technology itself.
