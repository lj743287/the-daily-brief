Germany’s AfD adopts more radical platform ahead of key eastern polls

The far-right Alternative for Germany approved a platform described as more radical as it climbs in polls in Saxony-Anhalt. The shift will sharpen political fault lines ahead of state elections and test centrist parties’ containment strategies.

Germany’s far‑right Alternative for Germany approved a platform described by observers as more radical, as the party climbs in polls in Saxony‑Anhalt ahead of state elections. The shift is expected to sharpen political fault lines and intensify debate over how centrist parties respond.

The new programme is reported to harden positions on immigration, the European Union and climate policy, aiming to mobilise core supporters. Detailed texts have not been fully scrutinised by independent analysts, and the party’s exact policy contours will be tested on the campaign trail.

Mainstream parties face renewed pressure to maintain their refusal to cooperate with the AfD at regional and national levels. Leaders of the conservative and centre‑left blocs have reiterated red lines in principle, though post‑election arithmetic in eastern states can be complex.

Parts of the AfD have been monitored by domestic intelligence services in several Länder over suspected extremist tendencies, a point of legal contention that the party disputes. This scrutiny shapes media coverage and can influence public funding and access during campaigns.

Polling indicates the AfD retains particular strength in eastern regions, where economic grievances, demographic shifts and energy costs have featured prominently in political discourse. Whether a more strident platform broadens the party’s appeal beyond its base remains uncertain.

The coming vote in Saxony‑Anhalt will test centrist containment strategies and the AfD’s capacity to convert polling into durable power. Coalition building after the election will be closely watched for signs of shifting red lines or new tactical alignments.
