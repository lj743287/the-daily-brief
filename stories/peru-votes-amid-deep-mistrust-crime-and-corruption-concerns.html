Peru votes amid deep mistrust, crime and corruption concerns

Peruvians go to the polls after a decade of churn that produced nine presidents, rampant corruption scandals and rising violent crime. A crowded field of candidates and a vast ballot underline public disillusionment with the political system.

Peruvians went to the polls amid deep mistrust after a decade of political churn that produced nine presidents, with corruption scandals and violent crime dominating the campaign. The vote is widely seen as a test of whether the country can move beyond instability.

A record field of 35 presidential candidates underscored voter disillusionment and party fragmentation. The sprawling ballot also included races for a bicameral congress, adding to the complexity of the choice facing electors.

Under electoral rules, a candidate must secure an absolute majority to win outright, otherwise a second‑round runoff will be held between the top two finishers. Given the fractured field, analysts consider a runoff highly likely.

Public security has risen sharply up the agenda as homicide and extortion rates have increased. Candidates have proposed a mix of policing measures and institutional reforms aimed at tackling organised crime and rebuilding public trust.

Corruption investigations have touched many recent leaders and legislators, fuelling scepticism about the capacity of the political class to deliver change. Observers warn that executive–legislative tensions could persist without a stronger mandate for reform.

Electoral authorities deployed security and logistical resources nationwide to reduce disruptions and speed the count. Official results could take days to finalise, with markets and regional partners watching for signals on fiscal policy and governance priorities.
