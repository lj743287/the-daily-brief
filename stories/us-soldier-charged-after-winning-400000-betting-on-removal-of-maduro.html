US soldier charged after winning $400,000 betting on removal of Maduro

A US soldier, Gannon Ken Van Dyke, has been charged after allegedly winning $400,000 on Polymarket by trading on classified information about Nicolás Maduro’s removal. The case raises fresh questions about prediction markets, national security and market abuse.

A US soldier, Gannon Ken Van Dyke, has been charged after allegedly winning $400,000 on Polymarket by trading on classified information about Nicolás Maduro's removal.

According to the justice department, the case involves the misuse of sensitive information to place bets on a prediction market. It raises questions about market abuse, national security and how lightly regulated online betting platforms should be treated.

The allegations also matter because prediction markets have been gaining visibility as a way to trade on political and geopolitical outcomes, even though they are often presented as speculative rather than financial instruments.

If prosecutors can prove the trades were based on classified material, the case could become a warning for others tempted to use insider information in new forms of digital gambling.

It may also add pressure on regulators to decide whether markets like Polymarket need tighter oversight when their contracts are linked to real-world political events.

The broader issue is simple: as betting markets expand, the line between speculation, intelligence and abuse can become dangerously thin.
