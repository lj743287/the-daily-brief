Argentina eases glacier protections to allow mining, sparking protests

Congress approved amendments to the ‘glacier law’ to facilitate extraction of metals in sensitive Andean regions. Environmentalists warn of risks to water security and ecosystems as the government pursues investment.

Argentina’s Congress has approved amendments to the country’s ‘glacier law’ to facilitate mining in sensitive Andean regions, The Guardian reported. The decision sparked protests from environmental groups concerned about the impact on fragile ecosystems.

The government argues the changes will unlock investment and jobs, enabling extraction of metals such as copper, lithium and silver seen as vital to the energy transition. Officials say the reforms will help revitalise the economy.

Environmentalists warn the shift threatens water security for downstream communities and could damage habitats in areas of glaciers and permafrost. They contend that robust safeguards are essential and currently insufficient.

The previous framework had sharply limited industrial activity on or near ice masses. Easing the rules may open high‑altitude projects that were previously off‑limits, though practical constraints remain.

Global miners and EV supply chains are watching closely as competition for critical minerals intensifies. Argentina is already a key player in lithium, and broader access could change regional project pipelines.

Legal challenges, provincial permitting and community consent will shape how quickly any new projects move. Investor appetite will also depend on clarity of enforcement and environmental oversight.
