US inflation re-accelerates as fuel costs surge

Headline inflation rose to 3.3%, driven by higher pump prices linked to the Iran war shock. The rebound complicates hopes for early rate cuts, with bond markets reassessing the path of monetary easing and households facing renewed pressure on real incomes.

US inflation has re-accelerated to 3.3% as higher fuel costs feed through to consumer prices, complicating hopes of early interest-rate cuts. The jump, driven by a resurgence in pump prices following the Iran war shock, has prompted investors to reassess how quickly monetary easing can begin and has revived concerns about renewed pressure on households’ real incomes.

The move owes much to energy. Oil’s risk premium has widened as traders weigh disruption risks and shipping reroutes, while seasonal refinery maintenance and tighter product markets have amplified the rise at the pump. That energy impulse is now rippling into transport and goods distribution costs, offsetting earlier disinflation from goods and keeping overall price momentum firmer than policymakers would like. Base effects from last year’s declines are also turning less helpful, making monthly prints look stickier.

For the Federal Reserve, the composition of inflation matters as much as the headline. A fuel-led rebound is typically less persistent than domestically driven services inflation, but the danger is that repeated energy shocks seep into wages and service prices if expectations drift. With progress on underlying measures uneven and growth still resilient, the central bank has little incentive to rush. A longer hold at restrictive settings looks more likely, with officials keeping their data-dependent stance while emphasising the need for clearer evidence that inflation is returning sustainably to target.

Bond markets have moved quickly to reprice the path of policy. Yields have risen and the curve has shifted, reflecting fewer or later cuts being discounted, while a firmer dollar tightens global financial conditions. Equity investors are rotating towards energy and cash-generative defensives, and credit markets are testing risk appetite after a strong start to the year. For emerging markets, the mix of higher oil and a stronger dollar is a challenging combination, raising funding costs and complicating inflation management.

The squeeze on real incomes is most acute for lower-income households, which spend a larger share on fuel and transport. Consumers face a familiar trade-off: resilience in employment and wages versus the erosion of purchasing power at the forecourt and supermarket. Sectors with heavy logistics exposure, from retailers to manufacturers, are bracing for higher freight and input bills, while refiners and some energy producers enjoy stronger margins. The longer prices at the pump remain elevated, the greater the risk that firms try to rebuild margins through additional price rises, entrenching stickier services inflation.

What happens next hinges on energy markets and transmission. Key signposts include the security of supply routes, any changes in OPEC+ production strategy, the speed of a US shale response, and the state of US refining capacity into the summer driving season. Domestically, the breadth of inflation across services and shelter will determine whether the shock remains contained or broadens. Globally, Europe and parts of Asia are exposed to the same fuel dynamics, raising the risk of a synchronised growth slowdown if energy costs stay high. For now, the balance of risks argues for patience from central banks and for investors and households to prepare for a slower, more uneven disinflation path.
