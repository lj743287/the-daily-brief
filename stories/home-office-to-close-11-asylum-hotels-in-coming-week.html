Home Office to close 11 asylum hotels in coming week

The closures form part of a pledge to end hotel use this parliament. Ministers face pressure to expand alternatives while avoiding renewed local tensions and ensuring support services for asylum seekers.

The Home Office is set to close 11 hotels currently used to accommodate asylum seekers in the coming week, as ministers press a pledge to end hotel use by the end of this parliament. The step signals a shift away from emergency provision that expanded during the pandemic and amid record backlogs in processing claims.

Hotels became a costly stopgap as arrivals rose and permanent accommodation lagged. They also became flashpoints in a number of towns, with local objections and misinformation fuelling protests. Ending their use has therefore become both a fiscal and political priority, with ministers under pressure to demonstrate progress without displacing people into unsuitable or unsafe conditions.

Officials say the plan is to expand alternatives, including more long‑term dispersed housing, repurposed sites and partnerships with local authorities and housing providers. That will require capacity for on‑site support services, legal advice and safeguarding, alongside coordination with community groups to avoid renewed tensions around new placements.

The immediate operational challenge is to move residents out of hotels in an orderly way while meeting statutory duties on accommodation and subsistence. Children’s schooling, access to healthcare and continuity of legal representation are central considerations. Charities have previously warned that abrupt closures risk retraumatising people and disrupting asylum claims if not carefully managed.

The closures sit within a broader push to reduce the case backlog, tighten returns for those refused and expand integration routes for those granted protection. Capacity constraints in social housing and the private rented sector remain a bottleneck, and any spike in arrivals could quickly test the system. Devolved and local governments will seek clarity on funding and notice periods as sites are decommissioned.

Ministers will face calls to publish where and when hotels are being stood down, the costs saved, and the outcomes for those moved. With the policy under close scrutiny from both campaigners and critics, the next test will be whether alternative housing can be brought online at pace without triggering renewed local disputes or degrading support for vulnerable people.
