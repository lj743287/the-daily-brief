Mortgage rates show signs of falling after Iran war peak

Major lenders are trimming mortgage rates as markets take some comfort from the ceasefire signal in the Iran war. The question now is whether the relief proves durable enough to ease borrowing costs more broadly.

Major lenders are beginning to trim mortgage rates as markets take some comfort from the signal of a possible truce in the Iran war. The change offers a small but welcome sign that borrowing costs could ease if the geopolitical shock continues to unwind.

The fall is linked to expectations that pressure on oil and wider financial markets may be less severe than feared. When energy prices and bond yields stabilise, lenders often have room to adjust mortgage offers downward.

For households, the question is whether this is a temporary reprieve or the start of something more durable. Mortgage rates have been volatile for years, and another flare-up in the conflict could quickly reverse the improvement.

The broader issue is that housing costs remain highly sensitive to developments far beyond the property market. A ceasefire or peace signal can ease pressure, but the transmission to family finances is indirect and uncertain.

Lenders are moving cautiously, which suggests they see the same risk as borrowers: relief may be real, but it is not yet secure.
