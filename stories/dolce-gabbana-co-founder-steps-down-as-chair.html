Dolce & Gabbana co-founder steps down as chair

The luxury house is wrestling with weak demand and rising debt as the sector cools. Leadership change comes as brands recalibrate inventories, pricing and marketing to slower global growth.

Dolce & Gabbana said a co‑founder has stepped down as chair, a symbolic shift for one of Italy’s best‑known luxury houses as it contends with softer global demand and a rising debt burden. The move lands at a delicate moment for the sector, which has cooled after two years of post‑pandemic exuberance, and underscores how founder‑led brands are rethinking governance to navigate a slower cycle.

The change is confined to the chair role, with day‑to‑day operations continuing under the established executive team. Founder transitions are closely watched in luxury, where creative vision and corporate control often sit together; separating oversight from daily management can be read either as routine succession planning or a bid to sharpen focus on execution and financing.

Luxury demand has moderated across key markets. Spending by aspirational shoppers in the US has softened, China’s recovery has been uneven, and wholesale partners have been clearing stock after aggressive orders in 2021‑23. Brands have responded by recalibrating price increases, trimming inventories and leaning more on clienteling and events rather than broad‑based marketing splurges.

Against this backdrop, higher borrowing costs have made debt more onerous, particularly for privately held groups that rely on bank financing. With pricing power ebbing and full‑price sell‑through harder to sustain, cash generation has become a priority. Companies across the sector are tightening buy plans, moderating store expansion and scrutinising capex, while trying to protect brand equity by limiting discounting.

Strategically, luxury groups are pivoting to a steadier mix: deepening ties with core, high‑spending clients; refining assortments; and building out categories that are capital‑lighter or less seasonal. That includes selective pushes in beauty, accessories and homeware, as well as digital services that lift margins without swelling inventories. Any reset, however, takes time to flow through balance sheets.

Next steps to watch include the appointment of a successor as chair and any updates on financing, investment plans and the pace of new store openings. Sector‑wide, the health of Chinese demand, US holiday spending and tourism flows will set the tone for 2025. With geopolitics, energy prices and currency swings still in play, investors and creditors will look for improved cash discipline and clearer signals on growth priorities.
