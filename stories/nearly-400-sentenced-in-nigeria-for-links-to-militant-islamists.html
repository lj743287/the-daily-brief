Nearly 400 sentenced in Nigeria for links to militant Islamists

Mass trials in Nigeria concluded with sentences ranging from five years to life for alleged ties to Islamist groups. Rights advocates have raised due-process questions, while authorities argue the prosecutions are vital to restoring security in the north-east.

Nearly 400 people have been sentenced in Nigeria after mass trials for alleged ties to militant Islamist groups, with judges imposing punishments ranging from five years to life imprisonment. The proceedings mark one of the largest terror-related case conclusions in the country in recent years.

Authorities say the prosecutions are a critical step in restoring security in the north-east, where Boko Haram and the Islamic State West Africa Province have waged a long insurgency. Officials argue the cases demonstrate that suspects will be pursued through the courts rather than through extrajudicial means.

Rights advocates have raised due-process concerns, pointing to opaque proceedings, questions over access to defence lawyers, and reliance on confessions or intelligence that cannot be independently tested. They say the sheer scale of the trials risks miscarriages of justice, especially after years of detention without charge faced by some suspects.

The conflict in Nigeria’s north-east has devastated communities and spilled across borders in the Lake Chad basin, disrupting livelihoods, education and basic services. While front lines have shifted, sporadic attacks, abductions and forced recruitment persist, leaving civilians caught between militant violence and heavy-handed security responses.

The sentences underline the difficult balance between delivering justice at scale and upholding fair-trial standards in an overburdened judicial system. Beyond the courtroom, Nigeria still faces the task of separating hardline fighters from low-level associates, expanding deradicalisation programmes, and preparing communities for the complex work of reintegration and reconciliation.

Further cases are expected, and appeals are likely, keeping international attention on how Nigeria manages accountability and safeguards rights. The next tests will include greater transparency around judgments, independent monitoring of prisons, and sustained support for victims—steps that could determine whether the crackdown enhances long-term stability or fuels further grievances.
