How many ships are crossing the Strait of Hormuz?

BBC Verify analysis shows only a trickle of vessels transiting since the US–Iran ceasefire announcement. The pace of normalisation will drive near-term moves in crude benchmarks, tanker rates, and insurance premia across key shipping lanes.

The short answer is: far fewer than normal. In the days since the announcement of a US–Iran ceasefire, only a trickle of vessels has been seen transiting the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow waterway that connects Gulf exporters to world markets. For energy and shipping, this is the chokepoint that matters most. The speed at which traffic returns will shape near‑term moves in crude benchmarks, tanker earnings and the price of war‑risk cover.

Independent vessel‑tracking feeds and satellite imagery point to a marked slowdown in confirmed passages. Tankers and gas carriers are loitering at anchorages off Fujairah and inside the Gulf, while some masters are edging along coastal routes or waiting for instructions to join escorted transits. Port agent circulars suggest operators are proceeding with heightened caution. These snapshots are not perfect—Automatic Identification System signals can be spoofed or switched off, and cloud cover can obscure satellites—but taken together they show a system still in pause mode rather than flowing freely.

The hesitation reflects a web of operational and financial frictions that a political announcement alone does not dissolve. War‑risk insurance remains elevated and subject to short‑notice revision. P&I clubs, flag states and classification societies are maintaining strict guidance, and charterers’ approvals are tied to evolving risk assessments. Naval escort protocols and mine‑countermeasure assurances are still being clarified. Legal teams are parsing force majeure clauses, while banks and traders keep sanctions compliance checks on a short leash.

Not all cargoes behave the same. National oil companies are prioritising flagship crude and refined‑product liftings, but many private owners are unwilling to commit hulls without clearer protections. LNG carriers, given their safety profiles and boil‑off dynamics, are among the most cautious. Some Gulf exporters have limited capacity to bypass Hormuz via existing pipelines to the Gulf of Oman or the Red Sea, reducing pressure at the margins, but key flows—especially Qatari LNG—cannot avoid the strait. Importers are meanwhile re‑optimising slates, leaning on Atlantic Basin barrels and drawing down inventories rather than risking delays.

Markets are reading this as a timing story. If traffic normalises swiftly, crude benchmarks are likely to ease from risk‑premia highs, tanker spot rates could retrace, and insurance premia may drift lower. A more protracted lull would tighten physical availability, steepen freight and sustain elevated cover costs. Refiners in Asia and Europe are already balancing runs against on‑hand stocks and delivery uncertainty. Some brokers argue that once insurers publish clearer guidance, transits could rebound quickly; others warn that crews, lenders and compliance officers will keep a de facto brake on flows even if navies signal green lights.

What to watch next: visible convoys and regularised transit windows; a steady rise in ballast tonnage heading into the Gulf; the resumption of large‑class LNG carriers from Ras Laffan; and formal downgrades of risk by underwriters. Export programme updates from major terminals, plus any statement on supply policy from producers, will set the tone. Absent those signals, expect choppy price action across crude spreads, tanker rates and specialty insurance. The ceasefire may have lowered the temperature, but the world’s most sensitive shipping lane is not yet back to business as usual.
