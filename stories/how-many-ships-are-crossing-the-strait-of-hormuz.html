How many ships are crossing the Strait of Hormuz?

BBC Verify finds only a handful of vessels have transited since the ceasefire announcement, underscoring operator caution. Maritime insurers, shippers and charterers are weighing risks, toll demands and security before resuming normal flows.

Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has thinned markedly, with BBC Verify reporting that only a handful of vessels have transited since a ceasefire announcement. The numbers underscore how wary operators remain, even as diplomats seek to lower tensions.

Caution reflects a mix of commercial and legal risks. War‑risk insurance is costlier and harder to obtain, charterers are reassessing liabilities, and sanctions exposure is a live concern. Without clear security assurances, many owners prefer to wait rather than test the route.

Transit counts depend on definitions and data sources. Automatic Identification System (AIS) signals can be delayed or switched off, and not all passages are easily attributed to specific cargoes in real time. Analysts often triangulate AIS, port line‑ups and satellite imagery to build a picture.

Different vessel classes face different trade‑offs. Crude and product tankers assess not just safety but the economics of demurrage and deviation, while LNG carriers weigh boil‑off and scheduling constraints. Charter parties may include force majeure clauses that complicate incentives to proceed.

Costs mount quickly when ships divert around Africa or idle at anchor. Longer voyages tie up tonnage, widen regional price spreads and strain just‑in‑time supply chains. Crew safety protocols, security escorts and routing restrictions add to the operational burden.

Early signs of normalisation would include a steady rise in both ballast and laden traffic lining up for passage, a softening of war‑risk premiums and more permissive guidance from insurers and flag states. Until then, the muted flow remains a barometer of perceived risk in the Gulf.
