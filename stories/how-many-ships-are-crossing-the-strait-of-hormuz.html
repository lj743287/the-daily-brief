How many ships are crossing the Strait of Hormuz?

BBC Verify analysis suggests traffic through the strait remains severely limited, a sign of how fragile the route has become.

BBC Verify analysis suggests that traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remains severely limited, with only a small number of vessels crossing since the ceasefire deal. That is significant because the strait is normally a vital corridor for global oil and gas shipments.

The low level of traffic shows how quickly a political crisis can affect commercial behaviour even without a formal closure. Shipowners, insurers and crews tend to act cautiously when they believe a route may become dangerous, which can reduce traffic before any official blockade is in place.

The open question is whether this is a temporary slowdown or the start of a longer rerouting of trade. If vessels stay away, the economic consequences could deepen regardless of whether the strait is legally open, because markets respond to perceived risk as well as physical obstruction.
