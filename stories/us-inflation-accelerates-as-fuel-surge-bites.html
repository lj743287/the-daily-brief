US inflation accelerates as fuel surge bites

US consumer prices rose at the fastest monthly pace in nearly two years, driven by pump prices linked to the Iran conflict. The data complicate the outlook for interest-rate cuts and underscores the pass‑through from geopolitics to household budgets.

US consumer prices rose at the fastest monthly pace in nearly two years, with higher petrol costs linked to the Iran confrontation driving the headline increase, according to data cited by the BBC.

Energy‑related pass‑through is visible beyond the forecourt, with airfares, delivery and logistics costs nudging up and core measures proving stickier than policymakers had hoped.

Bond markets reacted by pushing yields higher and paring back expectations for near‑term interest‑rate cuts, while the dollar firmed and equity performance bifurcated between energy winners and rate‑sensitive laggards.

Federal Reserve officials have signalled patience, and the latest print strengthens the case for holding policy steady until disinflation clearly resumes across a broader set of categories.

For households, the fuel spike is lifting transport and shopping basket costs unevenly by region, and may weigh on discretionary spending if wage growth fails to keep pace.

Globally, the reacceleration complicates decisions for other central banks and raises financing pressures for emerging markets with dollar‑denominated debt, particularly energy importers facing a terms‑of‑trade hit.
