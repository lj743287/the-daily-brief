Airlines warn of jet-fuel shortages if Hormuz disruption persists

Europe’s airports body said members have growing concerns over jet-fuel availability as flows through the Gulf remain constrained. Carriers are exploring alternative sourcing and contingency routings, but sustained disruption could pressure fares and schedules.

Airlines have warned that jet‑fuel supplies could tighten if disruption around the Strait of Hormuz continues, with Europe’s airports body saying members report growing concern about availability. Carriers are lining up alternative sourcing and contingency routings to keep schedules running, but a prolonged squeeze risks pushing up fares and forcing timetable adjustments.

The Strait of Hormuz is a critical conduit for oil and refined products, and recent tensions in the Gulf have constrained flows. With diplomatic efforts to ease frictions with Iran faltering, some shipments have faced delays or diversions, adding uncertainty to supply chains that feed major European hubs as well as airports in the Middle East, Africa and Asia.

In response, airlines are increasing ‘tankering’ — uplifting more fuel at airports with stronger supply — and seeking additional volumes from European refineries and storage centres in the North Sea and Mediterranean. Contingency air routings that skirt higher‑risk airspace can extend flight times and fuel burn, while any need to detour marine cargoes around longer sea lanes would further stretch logistics and raise costs.

Jet‑fuel markets have already been volatile, and airport operators are reassessing on‑site inventories and delivery timetables. Larger hubs typically have more flexibility through storage and diversified pipelines, but smaller and regional airports could feel pinch points sooner. Network carriers may juggle aircraft rotations and fuel stops on long‑haul routes, while low‑cost operators with tight turnarounds face limited room to absorb disruption without affecting frequency or pricing.

For travellers and shippers, tighter fuel supply tends to ripple through via higher operating costs, potential surcharges and selective capacity cuts. Any squeeze would arrive as demand for leisure and business travel remains resilient, and as logistics networks depend on airfreight belly space. Separately, recent fuel unrest in parts of Europe has highlighted the broader sensitivity of consumers and businesses to energy‑supply shocks.

Attention in the coming days will focus on shipping conditions and insurance in the Gulf, refinery output in Europe and Asia, and whether governments move to ease bottlenecks, including through strategic stock releases or temporary rule waivers. A rapid de‑escalation would likely calm supply chains; a drawn‑out disruption would increase the odds of schedule changes, localised rationing at some airports and upward pressure on fares into the summer season.
