Oil jumps back above $100 after US-Iran peace talks fail

Oil prices have risen back above $100 a barrel after the collapse of US-Iran talks, intensifying the risk of a broader energy shock for markets, shipping and consumers.

Oil prices have climbed back above $100 a barrel after US-Iran talks collapsed at the weekend, raising the prospect of a broader energy shock for markets and consumers.

The failure of the negotiations has revived fears that supply routes and tanker traffic could be disrupted further if tensions keep rising in the Gulf. That risk matters well beyond energy traders, because higher crude prices can feed through to transport, food and inflation.

For shipping firms, airlines and refiners, the immediate concern is whether the Strait of Hormuz remains open enough to keep crude moving. For households, the danger is renewed pressure on petrol, heating and food bills.

The move above $100 underlines how quickly geopolitical shocks can spill into the wider economy. The next question is whether the price spike proves temporary or becomes the start of a prolonged squeeze.
