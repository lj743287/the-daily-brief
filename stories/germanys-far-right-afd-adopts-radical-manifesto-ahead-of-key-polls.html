Germany's far-right AfD adopts 'radical' manifesto ahead of key polls

The AfD approved a sharpened platform as it seeks gains in eastern state elections, testing Germany’s political centre. Mainstream parties warn of the implications for EU policy, migration and the rule of law if support consolidates around harder-line positions.

Germany’s far-right Alternative for Germany (AfD) has adopted a sharpened manifesto ahead of a new round of regional elections in the east, seeking to lock in gains where it has built a loyal base. Party strategists are pitching the platform as a decisive break with the political mainstream, underscoring a campaign designed to turn strong polling into institutional power.

The document advances tougher positions on migration, challenges elements of European Union authority and pledges a more muscular approach to law and order. Mainstream parties contend the programme marks a further radicalisation and warn of implications for EU cohesion, asylum policy and the rule of law if support consolidates around harder-line positions. The AfD rejects that characterisation, saying it is offering clear alternatives to what it sees as failed policies in Berlin and Brussels.

The stakes are clearest in the eastern states, where the party’s message has resonated amid longstanding economic and demographic disparities. Opinion surveys indicate the AfD is riding high in Saxony-Anhalt and competitive elsewhere in the region. Yet it remains cordoned off from coalition deals at federal and most state levels, a stance that could yield fragmented parliaments and complex government formation even if the party tops the vote.

Any advance would reverberate beyond state borders. While foreign and EU policy are led from Berlin, state governments influence national legislation via the Bundesrat, potentially complicating federal moves on migration, climate and energy. Gains for an overtly eurosceptic force in Europe’s largest economy would also add to pressure on EU policymaking as member states grapple with border controls, fiscal rules and the bloc’s longer-term strategic direction.

The AfD’s trajectory continues amid institutional friction. Courts and domestic intelligence bodies have scrutinised parts of the party and affiliated networks, citing concerns over extremism; opponents say elements of its platform risk colliding with constitutional safeguards. The party portrays such measures as political interference and argues that heightened oversight validates its anti-establishment appeal. These competing narratives are now set to collide on the campaign trail.

The contests will likely turn on cost-of-living pressures, migration management, public safety and the uneven impact of the energy transition between east and west. Watch for whether the centre-right maintains its refusal to collaborate with the AfD, for turnout dynamics, and for civil society mobilisation in the final weeks. European partners and markets will be reading the results as a barometer of the resilience of Germany’s political centre in a period of rising polarisation across the continent.
