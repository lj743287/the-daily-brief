Texas opens London office to woo UK firms with low taxes and subsidies

The US state expands its international footprint to attract finance and corporate HQs from Britain. The pitch highlights looser regulation and incentives as rivals vie for post‑Brexit relocations.

Texas has opened a London office to court British companies with the promise of low taxes, looser regulation and financial incentives, expanding the US state’s international footprint, the Guardian reports.

The pitch targets finance, technology and corporate headquarters as states compete to attract post‑Brexit relocations and expansions. Dallas’s “Y’all Street” push to rival New York’s financial heft underscores the ambition to pull more white‑collar jobs south.

Incentive menus can include grants, tax abatements and workforce programmes. Supporters say such packages catalyse investment and speed permitting, while critics argue subsidy competitions risk poor value for taxpayers and uneven regional development.

For UK firms eyeing the US, considerations include access to customers, energy costs, labour markets and local regulation. Texas offers abundant energy and a large workforce, alongside a different landscape on unions and environmental standards compared with many European locations.

The move comes as UK regions also vie for capital, pitching specialised clusters and R&D incentives. Some policymakers worry about a gradual shift of headquarters functions abroad, though others frame it as part of a two‑way investment flow.

Boardrooms will weigh talent pipelines, time zones and capital markets when deciding on footprints. London’s listings ecosystem remains under scrutiny as global firms seek higher valuations and deeper liquidity in competing financial centres.
