Trump's threat raises risks and leaves predicaments unchanged

Donald Trump’s escalation against Iran is turning the conflict into a test of endurance between Iran’s capacity to absorb strikes and Trump’s tolerance for the war’s costs. The strategic dilemma remains unchanged, but the danger of wider regional spillover is growing.

Donald Trump’s escalation against Iran has turned the conflict into a test of endurance, but it has not changed the basic strategic dilemma. Iran is trying to show it can absorb punishment and keep operating; Trump is trying to prove he can impose costs without being drawn into a wider war.

That is why the latest threats are more dangerous than decisive. They increase the risk of miscalculation, especially around the Strait of Hormuz and other choke points that matter to shipping, oil and allied military deployments. Yet they do little to solve the underlying problem of how either side can force the other to bend.

The conflict now carries a built-in imbalance: each new strike or warning can be presented as resolve, but each also raises the chance of spillover into the wider region. Gulf states, energy markets and Western governments all have reason to fear that a confrontation framed as controlled pressure could drift into something larger and harder to contain.

What happens next depends less on rhetoric than on whether both sides see an exit. If Iran digs in and Trump decides that escalation is politically affordable, the conflict could settle into a damaging war of attrition with no clear off-ramp. If either side blinks, it may do so only after the costs have already risen.
