Trump's threat raises risks and leaves predicaments unchanged

The confrontation with Iran has become a test of wills, with Tehran’s capacity to absorb strikes now being measured against Donald Trump’s tolerance for the costs of a widening war.

Donald Trump’s threat to escalate pressure on Iran has sharpened the risks in a confrontation that is now being measured as much by political endurance as by military capability. The immediate question is no longer whether both sides can inflict damage, but how much each is prepared to absorb before widening the conflict further.

Iran can still absorb considerable punishment and has shown in the past that it can respond asymmetrically, through regional proxies, shipping disruption and missile or drone attacks. That makes the military picture dangerous even when neither side appears to want a full-scale war.

For Trump, the calculation is different but no less fraught. Escalation may appeal as a show of resolve, yet any prolonged conflict would expose the United States to higher energy prices, retaliation against partners and the risk of being drawn into another Middle Eastern war with no clear exit.

The result is a familiar impasse. Pressure may change the tone of the standoff, but it does not solve the underlying dispute over Iran’s nuclear programme, regional influence or Washington’s demand for concessions. That is why this remains less a breakthrough than a test of nerve, with very little evidence that either side has found a way to step back.
