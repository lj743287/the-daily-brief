Trump's threat raises risks and leaves predicaments unchanged

The Iran conflict is now a test of wills between Donald Trump and Tehran, with the immediate stakes centred on Iran’s ability to absorb strikes and Mr Trump’s tolerance for the war’s economic and political costs.

Donald Trump's threat over the Iran conflict has sharpened the confrontation, but it has not altered the underlying strategic problem. The contest is now a test of wills between the US president and Tehran, with each side trying to show it can absorb more pain than the other.

Iran's immediate question is whether it can withstand further strikes on its military and nuclear infrastructure without being forced into concessions. For Mr Trump, the risk is political as much as military: any prolonged escalation would bring pressure from voters, markets and allies over the costs of a wider war.

The danger is that an attempt to restore deterrence could instead deepen the crisis. The region has already seen a cycle of attacks, counterattacks and failed diplomacy, and there is little sign that either side is ready to retreat on the core issues.

That means the outlook remains unstable even if the rhetoric intensifies. A stronger threat may buy time, but it does not resolve the larger question of how to stop escalation without appearing weak.
