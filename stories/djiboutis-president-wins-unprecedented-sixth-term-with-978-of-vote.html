Djibouti's president wins unprecedented sixth term with 97.8% of vote

Ismail Omar Guelleh extended his 27-year rule in an election boycotted by main opposition figures. The result cements continuity in a maritime chokepoint state critical to global logistics and to the military basing strategies of major powers.

Djibouti’s president, Ismail Omar Guelleh, has secured an unprecedented sixth term with 97.8% of the vote, extending his 27-year rule in the strategically placed Horn of Africa state. The 78-year-old leader prevailed in an election boycotted by the main opposition figures, ensuring a largely uncontested outcome.

The boycott by principal rivals left voters with limited choice, with Guelleh presenting the poll as a mandate for continuity and stability. Opposition figures have long argued that the political playing field is uneven; the authorities insist elections are conducted lawfully and emphasise order and security as national priorities.

Djibouti’s significance far outweighs its size. Sitting astride the Bab el-Mandeb strait, the gateway between the Red Sea and the Gulf of Aden, it is a maritime chokepoint critical to global trade flows between Asia, Europe and the Middle East. The country hosts multiple foreign military bases, including from the United States, China and France, underscoring its role in major powers’ basing strategies.

Regional instability has heightened the stakes. Disruptions to shipping in the Red Sea have focused attention on Djibouti’s ports and facilities as navies seek to protect key routes and monitor threats. For external partners, Guelleh’s renewed tenure signals policy continuity on security cooperation and basing agreements.

At home, supporters credit Guelleh with anchoring stability and investing in port and logistics infrastructure, while critics say the concentration of power has come at the expense of political openness. Rights organisations have for years raised concerns about restrictions on dissent; officials have rejected those characterisations, arguing that economic development and security needs take precedence.

Economically, Djibouti remains a hub for Ethiopia’s seaborne trade and aims to deepen its role in transshipment and logistics. The government relies on foreign investment—including from Asian and Gulf partners—to finance infrastructure, leaving it exposed to external shocks and shipping volatility. In the coming months, investors and allies will watch for signs of cabinet continuity, fiscal steadiness and how the presidency balances great-power ties amid a more contested Red Sea.
