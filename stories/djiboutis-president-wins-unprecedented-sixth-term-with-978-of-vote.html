Djibouti's president wins unprecedented sixth term with 97.8% of vote

Ismail Omar Guelleh has extended his long rule after an election boycotted by the main opposition candidates. The result underlines the entrenchment of power in a small but strategically located state on the Horn of Africa.

Djibouti’s President Ismail Omar Guelleh has secured an unprecedented sixth term after winning 97.8% of the vote in an election boycotted by the main opposition candidates. The result extends his 27-year rule in a small state whose location at the mouth of the Red Sea gives it outsized strategic importance.

Mr Guelleh, 78, has long presided over a political system in which opposition activity has been tightly constrained and little real electoral competition has been available. The near-total victory, while not surprising, underlines how firmly power remains concentrated around the presidency.

Djibouti hosts foreign military bases and sits on a vital shipping route linking Europe and Asia to the Indian Ocean, making stability there a matter of wider concern. But the absence of meaningful contest also raises familiar questions about succession, political openness and how long the current governing arrangement can endure.
