UK could adopt EU single market rules under new legislation

Sir Keir Starmer is planning a law that would let the government adopt EU single market rules without them being voted on in Parliament, signalling a major shift in post-Brexit economic policy.

Sir Keir Starmer is planning legislation that would allow the government to adopt EU single market rules without taking them through Parliament in the usual way. That would mark a notable shift in post-Brexit economic policy, even if ministers present it as a practical rather than ideological change.

The appeal for government is clear: closer regulatory alignment could reduce friction for exporters and make it easier to remove barriers in selected sectors. But the constitutional significance is equally obvious, because handing ministers greater latitude to follow Brussels rules will revive arguments about scrutiny, sovereignty and democratic oversight.

The debate will also be political, not just procedural. Supporters will argue that the UK needs flexibility to improve trade and competitiveness; critics will say it amounts to rule-taking by another name. What happens next will depend on how wide the legislation is drawn and how much Parliament is prepared to resist it.
