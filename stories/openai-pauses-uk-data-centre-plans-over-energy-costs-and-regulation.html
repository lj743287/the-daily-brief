OpenAI pauses UK data centre plans over energy costs and regulation

The AI firm has put a flagship UK infrastructure project on hold, citing power prices and policy uncertainty. The delay raises questions over Britain’s bid to be an AI ‘superpower’ and the grid capacity needed for data‑intensive growth.

OpenAI has put a flagship UK data centre plan on hold, citing high energy costs and policy uncertainty. The pause, first reported by the BBC, halts a project that had been promoted as part of Britain’s push to lead in artificial intelligence.

The move raises questions over the government’s ‘AI superpower’ ambition and whether the UK can provide the affordable, reliable electricity and regulatory clarity hyperscale computing now demands. Industry groups say the speed of AI adoption is exposing bottlenecks across power networks.

Volatile wholesale power prices and grid capacity constraints have become central considerations for cloud and AI operators. Data centres are highly energy‑intensive, and developers increasingly locate new capacity where electricity is abundant, low‑carbon and priced predictably.

Regulatory uncertainty is another factor, with the UK still shaping rules for AI safety, data governance and critical infrastructure. Planning and grid connection timelines remain a concern for large‑scale digital projects, even as ministers court investment.

Developers and utilities have called for faster transmission upgrades and clearer incentives to bring new low‑carbon generation online. The government has said it wants to streamline approvals and expand grid capacity, though timelines for delivery remain in focus.

Globally, competition for AI infrastructure is intensifying, from the Nordics’ low‑cost renewable power to US states offering swift permitting. The UK’s ability to attract the next wave of AI build‑out may hinge on bringing cheaper power and predictable rules to bear at pace.
