Energy shock risk grows as US–Iran talks collapse

The breakdown of negotiations and ongoing constraints in the Strait of Hormuz have markets braced for further oil and gas price spikes. With tankers delayed and supply routes uncertain, borrowing costs could rise and broader inflation pressures persist.

Energy markets are braced for renewed turbulence after the collapse of US–Iran talks and continued constraints in the Strait of Hormuz, a key conduit for crude and liquefied gas. Tanker schedules have slipped, and shippers face higher premiums and tighter terms as the conflict risk remains unresolved.

The strait carries a significant share of global oil and LNG flows, and any hesitation by operators quickly ripples through prices and availability. BBC Verify reporting indicates only a handful of vessels have crossed since a ceasefire announcement, underscoring the caution among charterers, insurers and crews despite diplomatic signals.

Disruptions at Hormuz feed inflation through multiple channels: higher pump prices, elevated utility costs and dearer freight for energy‑intensive goods. With inventories cushioning shocks only temporarily, the risk is that a short supply squeeze becomes a longer‑lived price level shift for consumers and firms.

The macro‑financial transmission is already visible. Persistent energy‑led inflation can lift bond yields and widen corporate spreads as investors demand compensation for uncertainty, raising borrowing costs for households and businesses. Emerging markets are particularly exposed via a stronger dollar and costlier energy imports.

Sectoral strains are mounting. Airports and airlines in Europe have warned about jet‑fuel availability if Hormuz remains constrained, while chemicals and refining face feedstock tightness. Industry advisories telling tanker firms not to pay any Iranian levies for transits add legal and logistical obstacles to restoring normal flows.

Market balance now hinges on the pace of shipping normalisation, policy responses and spare capacity. Possible stabilisers include coordinated stock releases and flexible output from key producers, though neither can substitute for uninterrupted transit. Investors will watch ship transits, war‑risk insurance rates and refinery margins for early signs of relief or escalation.
