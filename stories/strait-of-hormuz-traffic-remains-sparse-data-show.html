Strait of Hormuz traffic remains sparse, data show

BBC Verify tracking indicates only limited passages since the ceasefire, reflecting risk premiums and legal uncertainty. Shipping insurers and charterers remain cautious as navies assess maritime security corridors.

Shipping movements through the Strait of Hormuz remain limited, according to BBC Verify analysis, despite a ceasefire holding elsewhere. AIS tracking indicates only a trickle of passages relative to normal patterns, reflecting caution among owners and charterers as they await clearer security guarantees.

Several factors are suppressing transits. Insurers have raised war‑risk premia and tightened conditions, and legal uncertainty persists over reported Iranian toll claims that industry advisories say should not be paid. Without unambiguous guidance from underwriters and flag states, many operators prefer to wait rather than risk cover being invalidated.

The strait carries a large share of the world’s seaborne oil and significant LNG volumes, making even partial disruption consequential. Alternative routes exist but are limited: Saudi Arabia’s east‑west pipeline can move some crude to the Red Sea, and the UAE’s link to Fujairah reduces reliance on Hormuz for specific grades. These channels help at the margin but cannot fully replace normal flows.

Operational complexity compounds the picture. Some vessels are slowing or drifting offshore to conserve fuel while they await instructions, and a minority may reduce broadcasting to avoid attention, complicating tracking. Navies are assessing whether controlled corridors or convoying can be offered, but rules of engagement and clearance protocols take time to agree.

The knock‑on effects are already spreading through supply chains. Demurrage costs are rising as ships idle, freight rates have firmed on alternative routes, and refiners are balancing runs against uncertain feedstock arrivals. European aviation fuel markets, which draw on Middle Eastern and Indian supply, look particularly sensitive to any further slippage in deliveries.

Key signals to watch include explicit green‑lights from major P&I clubs, announcements on maritime security corridors, and legal clarity on the status of any levies. Evidence of sustained pipeline rerouting and storage draws in consuming countries would indicate adaptation, while a build‑up of anchored tonnage would point to tighter conditions ahead.
