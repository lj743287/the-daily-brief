Djibouti’s president secures sixth term in boycotted election

Ismail Omar Guelleh extended his 27‑year rule with 97.8% of the vote in an election boycotted by main opposition candidates. The result underscores the dominance of the incumbent and heightens concerns over political openness in a strategic Horn of Africa state.

Djibouti’s president, Ismail Omar Guelleh, secured a sixth term with 97.8% of the vote in an election boycotted by main opposition candidates, extending his 27‑year rule. The result underscores the dominance of the incumbent and renews concerns over political openness.

Opposition figures cited restrictions and an uneven playing field in explaining their boycott, leaving voters with limited choice. International observation was minimal, and independent verification of turnout and procedures remains limited.

Djibouti’s location at the Bab el‑Mandeb strait gives it outsized strategic importance, hosting military facilities for the US, China, France and others. Stability and access to bases are priorities for foreign partners, even as they raise governance issues in bilateral dialogues.

The government points to infrastructure development, port expansion and relative security as achievements during Guelleh’s tenure. Critics argue that media controls and curbs on dissent have narrowed space for political competition and civic participation.

Regional dynamics add to the country’s significance, with Djibouti serving as a logistics hub amid tensions in the Red Sea and instability in the Horn of Africa. Its economy is closely tied to services for landlocked Ethiopia, making external shocks particularly consequential.

International partners are likely to calibrate their responses, balancing calls for reform with strategic interests. Few immediate policy shifts are expected, though pressure for incremental changes on rights and electoral standards may persist.
