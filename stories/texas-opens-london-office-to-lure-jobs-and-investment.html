Texas opens London office to lure jobs and investment

The low‑tax US state is targeting UK corporates with incentives as it builds a global footprint. The move intensifies inter‑state competition for finance and tech talent amid shifting regulatory and cost dynamics.

Texas has opened a London office to court UK corporates, offering incentives and a lower‑tax operating base as it extends its international footprint. The move underscores intensifying competition among US states to attract finance, tech and advanced manufacturing jobs from global firms weighing costs and regulation.

The state’s pitch centres on the absence of personal income tax, comparatively light regulation, abundant land and a large, growing workforce across hubs including Austin, Dallas–Fort Worth and Houston. High‑profile relocations and expansions in recent years have burnished its credentials, even as debates continue over infrastructure and power reliability.

For UK companies, the timing aligns with a reassessment of global footprints amid energy‑price volatility, shifting supply chains and divergence in regulatory regimes since Brexit. Generous federal incentives in the US for clean energy and semiconductors have tilted boardroom calculations, and Texas is positioning itself as a gateway to those programmes with additional state‑level sweeteners.

Financial services are a focal point. Dallas’s bid to build “Y’all Street” has drawn back‑ and middle‑office functions from coastal cities, with a new campus for one Wall Street bank emblematic of the trend. Time‑zone advantages, lower operating costs and growing local talent pools are part of the sales pitch to UK‑based firms considering US expansion.

There are caveats. The 2021 winter storm exposed vulnerabilities in the state’s power grid, prompting calls for reform, and some companies weigh cultural and policy considerations when relocating staff. Incentive packages face scrutiny over their long‑term value for taxpayers, pushing development agencies to demonstrate clear net benefits.

More US states are likely to establish outposts in London and other European capitals as the race for mobile investment accelerates. UK national and devolved authorities, in turn, are sharpening their own offers on planning, skills and clusters. For multinationals, a multi‑hub approach balancing tax, talent, infrastructure and regulatory certainty is becoming the default.
