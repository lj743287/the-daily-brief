EU entry/exit biometric rules take effect for UK travellers

The long‑delayed Entry/Exit System now requires fingerprints and photos for non‑EU arrivals, changing passenger flows to 29 countries. Airlines and border posts warn of teething problems as the system beds in.

The EU’s long‑delayed Entry/Exit System (EES) has come into force, requiring fingerprints and photographs from non‑EU arrivals, including UK travellers. The change alters border processes across 29 European countries, the BBC reported.

Airlines, ports and border posts have warned of initial teething problems as the system beds in. Carriers have advised passengers to allow extra time while staff and infrastructure adapt to new checks.

EES is designed to replace manual passport stamping, recording biometric and travel data on first entry and automating subsequent crossings. Officials say it will strengthen border management and help track overstays.

Travel providers have updated guidance and reconfigured passenger flows where possible. Training, signage and IT integration are expected to improve throughput as familiarity grows.

For travellers, the main impact is likely at the first enrolment, when biometric capture can take longer. Families, infrequent visitors and those with limited mobility may experience slower processing until systems and staffing settle.

In the longer term, EES is a precursor to ETIAS, the EU’s planned electronic travel authorisation. Combined with the UK’s own ETA scheme, the shift reflects a global move towards biometric, pre‑authorised border regimes.
