UK farmers warn food costs to stay elevated despite ceasefire

Producers say fertiliser, fuel and transport expenses remain high and will take time to ease even if tankers move again. Supermarkets and consumers face a delayed pass‑through from the energy shock.

UK farmers say food prices are likely to remain elevated despite a ceasefire, citing persistently high costs for fertiliser, fuel and transport. The industry expects a delayed pass‑through to supermarkets and consumers even if tankers begin moving more freely again.

Fertiliser prices are closely tied to global gas markets, and many producers locked in purchases earlier at higher levels to secure supply for key planting windows. That cost base will filter through harvests before any relief from lower input prices can show up on shelves.

Haulage and cold‑chain logistics are also energy‑intensive, leaving growers exposed to diesel and electricity bills that have yet to normalise. Renegotiating contracts with processors and retailers takes time, further slowing the adjustment even as wholesale benchmarks shift.

Supermarket pricing reflects forward contracts and category strategies. Private‑label lines can move sooner in some cases, but branded goods often carry longer lags. Competitive pressures may compress margins rather than immediately deliver broad‑based price cuts.

Weather risks and biosecurity concerns add uncertainty to the outlook, potentially compounding energy‑related costs. Imported inputs and packaging have also risen in price with freight and insurance, particularly on routes affected by security concerns.

Industry groups are urging measures that improve resilience, including support for energy efficiency and access to working capital. Ultimately, a durable easing in food inflation depends on sustained normalisation in energy markets and logistics, not just short‑term diplomatic progress.
