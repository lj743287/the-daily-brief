India refuses to criminalise marital rape. A TV series forces a reckoning

A hit drama shining a light on consent and misogyny has ignited debate about India’s refusal to criminalise marital rape. The cultural response underscores the gap between public discourse and legal reform, and the societal barriers victims face in seeking justice.

A hit Indian television drama has thrust the country’s refusal to criminalise marital rape back into the spotlight. Chiraiya, which has drawn an audience of millions, has sparked extensive online discussion about consent and misogyny, placing the private realities of marital sex and coercion at the centre of public debate in a way that court petitions and policy papers have struggled to do.

Under India’s penal law, non-consensual sex within marriage is excluded from the definition of rape, a stance that leaves survivors reliant on a patchwork of civil protections for domestic violence and other criminal provisions that do not directly address sexual assault by a spouse. Efforts to overturn the exception have stalled: a prominent high court delivered a split verdict in 2022, and petitions challenging the law remain before the Supreme Court. India’s position stands out as many countries have moved to explicitly recognise marital rape as a crime, aligning with international human rights standards.

Chiraiya’s narrative, by treating violation within marriage as both harm and injustice, has provided a vocabulary for audiences to discuss consent beyond the courtroom. Viewers have shared personal accounts on social media, reflecting how a primetime storyline can help translate abstract legal principles into lived experience. The programme has also reframed a broader conversation—rooted in India’s #MeToo moment—about how power, patriarchy and silence operate inside the home as much as in the workplace.

Yet the distance between cultural reckoning and legal reform can be wide. Survivors face stigma from families and communities, the prospect of social and economic abandonment, and inconsistent responses from police. Even where other offences can be invoked, evidentiary hurdles and the intimate nature of the violence deter reporting. Lawyers and counsellors say sexual coercion frequently overlaps with dowry harassment, custody disputes and financial control, exposing how fragmented remedies leave many women choosing survival over litigation.

Supporters of the marital exception argue that criminalisation could invite false complaints and destabilise the institution of marriage; women’s rights advocates counter that these claims obscure the scale of unaddressed abuse and the signalling function of the law. Political leaders have largely tread carefully, wary of polarising an electorate in which notions of marital privacy and tradition remain potent. With criminal law centralised at the federal level, reform ultimately requires national will; for now, governments have preferred incremental policy steps over statutory change.

What happens next will likely be decided as much in living rooms as in courtrooms. Pending litigation could eventually force a constitutional reading on consent within marriage, while practical measures—police training, survivor services, and clearer prosecutorial guidance for related offences—may offer near-term relief. International scrutiny and regional precedents are likely to sustain pressure. Chiraiya’s success suggests there is now a mass audience for uncomfortable truths; whether lawmakers follow that audience remains the unresolved question.
