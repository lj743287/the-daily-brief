Tanker firms urged not to pay Iranian levies for Hormuz passage

Advisories have told shippers to avoid any tolls amid legal and sanctions risks. Owners face a complex calculus over rerouting, insurance cover and contractual liabilities.

Tanker operators have been advised not to pay any Iranian tolls or levies for passage through the Strait of Hormuz amid legal and sanctions risks, according to BBC reporting. The guidance adds another layer of complexity to routing decisions as owners weigh safety, compliance and commercial obligations.

Shipowners face a difficult calculus. Rerouting or waiting for clearer security guarantees can mean lost time and higher costs, while proceeding exposes crews and cargoes to heightened risk. War‑risk insurance is pricier and more conditional, and any transfer of funds that could breach sanctions carries significant liabilities.

Charter‑party arrangements matter. On spot voyages, the allocation of costs and delays can be highly sensitive to contract terms, while on time charters owners and charterers must agree on safe employment clauses and acceptable routes. Force majeure claims are possible but contentious, and disputes can arise over who bears demurrage.

Insurance cover is pivotal. Protection and indemnity clubs and war‑risk underwriters set the parameters within which ships can sail, and cover may be jeopardised by dealings deemed to contravene sanctions or by entering declared high‑risk zones without proper notification. Letters of indemnity cannot substitute for invalid insurance.

Market effects are rippling outward. Freight rates on alternative routes have risen, refiners are juggling feedstock choices, and end‑users in aviation and trucking worry about product availability if deliveries stretch out. Even where physical supply is sufficient, logistics snarls can translate into localised shortages.

Next steps hinge on whether a credible maritime security corridor is established and on legal clarity from governments and insurers. For now, many owners are opting to wait, accepting near‑term costs in exchange for reduced legal and operational risk.
