US inflation jumps as fuel prices surge amid Middle East conflict

The consumer price index rose 3.3% year-on-year, with a sharp monthly gain driven by pump prices. The data complicate the policy outlook as the war and shipping disruption feed through to households.

US consumer price inflation rose to 3.3% year on year, with a sharp monthly increase driven by higher fuel costs, according to BBC reporting. The data complicate the policy outlook as the Middle East conflict and shipping disruption filter into household budgets.

Energy was the key driver, reflecting the pass‑through from crude and refined products to prices at the pump. Elevated transport and logistics costs tied to rerouted shipping and pricier insurance are also beginning to show up in goods and services.

Beyond energy, pressures in services remain a concern for policymakers, with elements of shelter and labour‑intensive categories proving slow to cool. While some goods prices are easing as supply chains normalise, they are no longer offsetting broader inflation as they did last year.

Financial markets reacted by reassessing the path of interest rates, with investors trimming expectations of near‑term cuts. Higher yields and a firmer dollar are typical in such episodes, tightening financial conditions both in the US and abroad.

The spillovers are global. A stronger dollar can raise import costs for other economies, and higher US rates tend to pressure emerging markets with dollar debt. Europe faces the added risk of energy‑linked price persistence if Gulf shipping remains constrained.

The trajectory from here depends on the energy path and the speed at which shipping lanes stabilise. The Federal Reserve has signalled it will remain data‑dependent, scrutinising wages, rents and core services to judge whether the latest move is a blip or a re‑acceleration.
