Greece to ban social media for under-15s: what the move means for families and platforms

Athens plans to bar under-15s from social media from next year, part of a European trend that includes measures in France and Spain. Supporters cite mental health and safety; critics question enforceability and civil liberties. The policy will test age-verification systems and cross-border compliance. Here is how the rules could work and the likely fallout.

Greece’s government says it will prohibit social media use for those under 15, positioning the country among European states tightening rules on children’s online activity.

\n

Proponents argue that younger teens are particularly vulnerable to harmful content and compulsive use patterns, and that bans can give families clearer guardrails.

\n

Opponents raise practical and legal concerns: verifying ages reliably without intrusive data collection is difficult, and outright bans may push activity to less-regulated platforms.

\n

France and Spain have advanced their own measures, signalling a regional shift towards stronger youth protections. The specifics differ, but the direction of travel is similar.

\n

Implementation hinges on age checks that balance privacy with effectiveness. Options include device-level settings, ID-based verification, or third-party attestations—each with trade-offs.

\n

Platforms face compliance and product redesign challenges, from changing default settings to strengthening parental controls and content moderation for older teens.

\n

Cross-border enforcement within the EU’s single market will matter. If rules diverge, platforms may need geofenced features or face forum-shopping by users and developers.

\n

Legal tests are likely as civil society groups probe proportionality and compatibility with existing digital rights frameworks. The outcomes will shape how far Europe can go in reshaping adolescents’ online lives.
