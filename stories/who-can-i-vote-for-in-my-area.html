Who can I vote for in my area?

A service guide to candidates and contests across Wales. With powers over health, education and transport, the stakes for daily life are tangible.

Elections take place across Wales in May, with decisions at stake that directly affect the NHS, schools, buses and trains. This guide explains how to find out exactly who is standing where you live and what has changed about the contests.

Voters are choosing representatives to the Senedd under a new system that replaces many smaller seats with fewer, larger multi-member constituencies and a more proportional method of allocating seats. Parties will publish ranked lists of candidates for each seat; independents can also stand. Supporters say the change should better match votes to seats, while critics worry about a weaker link to a single local member and more power for party headquarters over who appears on the list.

The definitive place to see who you can vote for is the official statement of persons nominated, published by your local returning officer once nominations close. You can find it on your local council’s elections page, via the Electoral Commission’s postcode lookup, or through the Senedd election pages. Local news outlets and party websites usually collate candidate lists after they are confirmed.

If your area name looks unfamiliar, it may be because boundaries have changed for this election. Use an online constituency finder to check which new Senedd seat covers your address and whether community ward or local authority boundaries are also shifting. Students and people who have recently moved should confirm the address where they are registered; you can be on more than one register but you must only vote once in the same election.

To take part you must be on the electoral register; apply as early as possible if you are not. Postal and proxy voting are available for those who need them, with deadlines before polling day. Photo ID rules differ by type of election and do not apply uniformly across the UK, so check your poll card and council website for what is required in Wales this time. Polling stations provide assistance, including support for disabled voters and bilingual materials.

Next, watch for the publication of confirmed candidate lists, local hustings and party manifestos. Results will be declared seat by seat and will determine who forms the next Welsh Government, potentially through a coalition under the more proportional system. Expect immediate debate on health backlogs, curriculum and qualifications, bus and rail reform, and how Welsh decisions interact with UK funding and cross-border services. However you vote, checking the official notices for your address is the simplest way to know exactly who is on your ballot.
