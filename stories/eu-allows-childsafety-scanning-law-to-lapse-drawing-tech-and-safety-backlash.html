EU allows child‑safety scanning law to lapse, drawing tech and safety backlash

The European Parliament did not extend a legal carve‑out that enabled platforms to scan for child sexual abuse material. Firms warn reports will drop sharply; privacy advocates say new frameworks must better balance safety with rights.

The European Parliament has allowed a legal carve‑out that permitted platforms to scan for child sexual abuse material to expire, creating a gap in the framework companies used to detect and report harmful content on their services. The temporary measure, linked to the EU’s ePrivacy rules, lapsed on 3 April after lawmakers declined to extend it.

Technology firms and child‑safety groups warned that reports of abuse could fall sharply as automated detection is curtailed on some services. Experts point to a previous legal gap in 2021, during which reports fell significantly, including an estimated 58% decline cited by advocates, as evidence that scanning regimes materially affect detection rates.

Privacy and civil liberties organisations counter that blanket scanning risks violating fundamental rights, particularly in end‑to‑end encrypted environments, and argue that any replacement must be targeted, necessary and proportionate. They have pressed for stronger due process, transparency and limitations on the scope of any mandated detection.

Practically, companies may now switch off or scale back certain detection tools for EU users, with effects varying across messaging apps, file‑sharing and public platforms. Some services will continue to use measures they deem compatible with the baseline ePrivacy regime, while others are expected to make conservative changes pending legal clarity.

The lapse throws fresh attention on the EU’s broader child sexual abuse regulation, a long‑running and contentious proposal that would establish a permanent regime. Divisions over client‑side scanning, treatment of encrypted services and safeguards for users have stalled progress, with national governments and the Commission weighing interim options.

The episode highlights a persistent policy dilemma: how to sustain effective reporting and disruption of child abuse online while upholding privacy and security guarantees for the general public. Even if temporary, the gap is likely to shape negotiations over technical standards, transparency obligations and the role of app stores and operating systems in any future framework.
