Greece to ban social media for under-15s from next year

Athens joins European moves to curb youth access online, citing mental-health and safety concerns. Platforms face fresh compliance hurdles and age-verification demands.

Greece will prohibit under-15s from using social media from next year, becoming the latest European country to tighten access for children amid mounting concerns about mental health, online safety and addictive design. The move will require platforms to build or adopt stronger age checks, setting up a new compliance test for global tech firms operating in the EU.

Athens has framed the policy as a child-protection measure that responds to pressures on families and schools, saying it wants to curb exposure to harmful content and reduce compulsive use among younger teens. Key details, including how age will be verified and how enforcement will work, are expected to be set out in secondary rules, but officials have signalled that platforms rather than parents will shoulder the primary responsibility for keeping under-15s off their services.

The decision places Greece within a wider European realignment. France has moved to require parental consent for users under 15, while Spain has signalled tougher national measures as regions clamp down on smartphones in schools. At EU level, the Digital Services Act obliges large platforms to mitigate risks to minors and avoid profiling them; national bans and age-gates are emerging as governments test how far those duties can be pushed in practice, alongside existing data-protection rules that already set strict conditions for processing children’s data.

For the industry, the shift raises practical and legal hurdles. Robust age-verification at scale remains contested, with options ranging from document checks to third‑party attestations and on‑device estimates, each entailing privacy trade‑offs and risks of exclusion for those without formal ID. Companies must also navigate divergent national standards inside the single market, where inconsistent thresholds and verification methods could fragment user onboarding and increase liability for non‑compliance.

Supporters of tighter access argue that curbs are warranted given evidence of cyberbullying, exposure to self‑harm material and worries about compulsive product features; critics counter that research on causal links between social media and adolescent mental health remains mixed and that blanket bans could drive young people to harder‑to‑supervise spaces. Rights groups also warn that intrusive age checks may erode privacy and chill expression, while recent controversies over data handling and legal battles about alleged ‘addictive’ design have intensified scrutiny of the sector’s safeguards.

The next steps will hinge on Athens publishing technical standards, designating an enforcer and spelling out penalties. Platforms are likely to seek clarity on acceptable age‑assurance methods and could test the law in court if obligations appear disproportionate. Schools and parents will look for guidance on digital literacy and alternatives for communication, while Brussels watches how national measures mesh with EU rules. Other countries weighing similar moves will study the Greek rollout for lessons on feasibility, circumvention and unintended consequences.
