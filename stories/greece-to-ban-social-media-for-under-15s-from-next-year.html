Greece to ban social media for under-15s from next year

Greece is moving to restrict social media use for children under 15, following similar steps elsewhere in Europe. The policy raises familiar questions about enforcement, child protection and the role of platforms in young people’s lives.

Greece is set to restrict social media use for children under 15 from next year, joining a growing European debate over how far governments should go in protecting young people online. The move follows similar efforts in countries including France and Spain, reflecting mounting concern about screen time, exposure to harmful content and the effect of platforms on mental health.

In principle, the aim is to make it harder for children to access services that are not designed for them. In practice, enforcement is the central challenge. Age checks can be bypassed, accounts can be shared and platforms are often better at collecting data than verifying who is using a device at any given moment.

The policy also raises broader questions about responsibility. Governments want action from tech firms, but parents, schools and app stores would all have roles in making such a ban workable. If Greece does proceed, it will become another test case for whether digital-age restrictions can be enforced without pushing children into less visible and potentially less safe corners of the internet.
