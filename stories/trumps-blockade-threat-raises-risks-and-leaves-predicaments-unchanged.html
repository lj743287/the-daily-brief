Trump's blockade threat raises risks and leaves predicaments unchanged

The Strait of Hormuz threat escalates the stakes without resolving the underlying conflict, leaving Iran, the US and allies to weigh deterrence, escalation and the cost of disruption to global energy flows.

Donald Trump’s threat to blockade the Strait of Hormuz has intensified an already volatile stand-off between Washington and Tehran, but it has not resolved the deeper problem that brought the two sides to this point. The strait is one of the world’s most important energy chokepoints, and even the prospect of disruption is enough to unsettle governments, insurers, shippers and markets far beyond the Gulf.

In practical terms, any attempt to block or impede traffic through the waterway would carry immediate risks of military escalation. Iran has long treated Hormuz as a pressure point, while the US and its allies have made freedom of navigation a red line. That leaves both sides with incentives to signal resolve without stumbling into a broader conflict they may struggle to control.

For Iran, the threat may be meant to show it can still impose costs despite air strikes and sanctions. For Mr Trump, it projects toughness and seeks to raise the price of defiance. But threats of this kind can also narrow the space for diplomacy, especially if either side feels compelled to answer force with force to avoid looking weak.

The immediate economic impact is already visible in higher energy prices and greater anxiety in shipping and aviation. Even short-lived disruption in Hormuz can ripple quickly through oil markets, transport costs and inflation expectations. That is why governments from Europe to Asia are watching the waterway so closely, even if they are not direct parties to the confrontation.

Yet the underlying strategic predicaments remain unchanged. The US still wants to deter Iran without being drawn into a longer war, while Iran wants sanctions relief and security guarantees without conceding too much under pressure. Allies are caught between supporting Washington, protecting trade routes and avoiding steps that could deepen the crisis.

That makes the blockade threat less a solution than a further escalation of the test of wills already under way. The danger now is not only whether Hormuz is physically closed, but whether enough actors start behaving as if it might be, with all the economic and military consequences that would follow.
