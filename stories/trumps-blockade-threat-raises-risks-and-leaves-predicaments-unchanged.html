Trump's blockade threat raises risks and leaves predicaments unchanged

Donald Trump’s threat to blockade the Strait of Hormuz has heightened the chance of escalation, but it has not solved the central problem: how far Iran can sustain pressure and how long Washington is willing to bear the economic and military costs of confrontation.

Donald Trump's threat to blockade the Strait of Hormuz has increased the chance of a wider confrontation, but it has not altered the basic arithmetic of the crisis. Iran is still trying to show it can absorb pressure, while Washington is weighing how long it can sustain military, diplomatic and economic costs without a clear route to a lasting settlement.

The strait is one of the world's most sensitive waterways because a large share of seaborne oil and gas passes through it. Even the prospect of a blockade or prolonged disruption is enough to unsettle shipping, rattle energy markets and prompt governments and companies to plan for higher costs and supply delays.

For Trump, the blockade threat is a way of signalling resolve after talks with Iran faltered. But threats of this kind also narrow the room for manoeuvre, because they can force both sides to demonstrate strength rather than compromise, making a miscalculation more likely.

Iran's leaders, meanwhile, can seek to exploit the pressure by showing that they can still impose pain on global trade. That may include disruption, delays or coercive measures short of an outright closure, any of which could keep markets nervous without resolving the underlying dispute.

The central problem is that neither side has yet found a durable off-ramp. Washington wants to stop Iran from using the crisis to gain leverage, while Tehran wants to avoid appearing to yield under fire. That leaves the conflict suspended between escalation and uneasy containment.

Unless there is a renewed diplomatic channel, the most likely outcome is continued tension: enough military risk to keep shipping and energy markets on edge, but not enough clarity to end the standoff. That is precisely what makes the situation so dangerous for the region and for economies far beyond it.
