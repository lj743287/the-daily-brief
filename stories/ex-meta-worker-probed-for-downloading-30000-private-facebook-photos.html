Ex-Meta worker probed for downloading 30,000 private Facebook photos

Meta said a former employee is under investigation over a mass data breach. The case renews questions about insider risk and governance at social platforms.

Meta has said a former employee is under investigation after being suspected of downloading 30,000 private Facebook photos, in a case that revives long‑running concerns about insider risk at major social platforms. The company confirmed the individual had been dismissed and that inquiries are ongoing.

Few details have been made public. Meta has not disclosed when the alleged activity occurred, how it was detected, or whether any of the images were distributed beyond the company’s systems. It is also unclear how many users may have been affected or whether those people have been notified.

The episode underscores a persistent vulnerability in large tech companies: employees with legitimate access to sensitive data can, if controls fail, remove it at scale. Private images are among the most delicate categories of user information, and any unauthorised copying raises the risk of privacy harms and potential downstream misuse.

Regulators in multiple jurisdictions closely scrutinise the handling of personal data, and companies are generally required to assess and report breaches when there is a risk to individuals’ rights. Depending on where the users are located, authorities in the UK, EU and US could seek further information on Meta’s safeguards, access controls and breach‑notification processes.

Insider incidents are not unique to Meta. Other large platforms have faced controversies over staff access to user data, prompting industry‑wide efforts to tighten ‘least‑privilege’ access, expand audit logging and adopt zero‑trust approaches. The development also lands as Meta confronts broader legal and policy pressures, including fresh scrutiny of how its services are governed and monitored.

Attention will now turn to the scope and independence of Meta’s internal review, whether affected users are informed, and if external investigators or regulators are engaged. Beyond immediate remediation, investors and policymakers are likely to press the firm—and the wider industry—on measurable progress in curbing insider risk, from stricter access segregation to real‑time anomaly detection and clearer accountability for privacy governance.
