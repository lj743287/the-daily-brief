Pump prices climb again as ceasefire doubts linger

Motoring groups warned drivers not to expect swift declines, citing continued volatility and supply uncertainty. Retailers face scrutiny over pass-through speeds as households grapple with elevated transport costs.

Petrol and diesel prices ticked higher again this week, as oil markets priced in a fresh risk premium amid fading hopes of durable ceasefires in key conflict zones. Motoring organisations cautioned drivers not to expect swift relief at the pump, pointing to a volatile backdrop in crude and refined fuel markets and continued uncertainty over supply routes.

Geopolitics has reasserted itself as a dominant driver. The latest setback in efforts to ease tensions involving Iran, and renewed accusations of ceasefire violations between Russia and Ukraine, have reinforced traders’ focus on disruption risks. While outright supply losses remain limited, heightened diplomatic strain often lifts insurance costs, lengthens delivery times and supports refining margins, keeping retail prices under pressure even when headline crude benchmarks move only modestly.

Attention is also returning to how quickly changes in wholesale costs filter through to forecourts. Economists describe a familiar ‘rocket and feather’ pattern, in which pump prices rise rapidly when oil jumps but tend to drift down more slowly when conditions ease. Retailers counter that the apparent lag reflects contracted supply, stock already in storage, and the need to balance thin margins against fixed costs. Competition authorities in several markets have previously scrutinised this pass‑through, and could intensify monitoring if margins widen again.

For households and businesses, dearer fuel risks complicating an already uneven disinflation path. Transport costs feed into delivery charges and airfares, with ripple effects on food and consumer goods. Even small, repeated increases can sap disposable incomes, particularly in rural areas where alternatives to car travel are limited, and keep pressure on governments weighing fuel duty choices against fiscal constraints.

Recent scenes of fuel protests and supply disruption in Ireland underline how sensitive consumers have become to price spikes and availability concerns. Beyond Europe, the resilience of global demand heading into the Northern Hemisphere summer driving season, refinery maintenance schedules, and any shifts in output policy from major producers will shape the next leg for pump prices. In this context, diplomatic signals carry outsize weight: any credible progress towards ceasefires could cool risk premia, while further setbacks would likely entrench current levels or push them higher.

In the coming weeks, analysts will watch wholesale petrol and diesel benchmarks, shipping and insurance costs, and headlines from conflict‑related talks for clues on direction. Absent a material easing in geopolitical tensions, forecourts are unlikely to mirror any day‑to‑day softness in crude swiftly. Consumers may find savings by shopping around and moderating discretionary journeys, while policymakers face familiar trade‑offs between targeted relief and longer‑term measures to diversify transport and energy demand.
