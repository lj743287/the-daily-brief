EU legal lapse on child‑abuse scanning sparks Big Tech backlash

The European parliament let a law permitting automated detection of child sexual abuse on platforms expire, citing privacy concerns. Firms warn reports could plunge, echoing a sharp drop during a similar 2021 gap.

The European parliament has allowed a temporary law permitting platforms to use automated tools to detect child sexual abuse material to expire, citing privacy concerns from some lawmakers. The measure, a carve‑out from the EU’s ePrivacy rules, ended on 3 April, leaving companies uncertain about the legality of scanning private communications for harmful content within the bloc.

The lapsed framework had enabled providers to deploy techniques such as hash‑matching of known illegal images, classifiers for suspected grooming and sextortion, and reporting to hotlines and law enforcement, even in services that include private messaging. It was conceived as a stopgap while a longer‑term child‑safety regulation was negotiated.

Technology firms and child‑protection advocates warn the change could sharply reduce detections. During a similar gap in 2021, experts recorded a steep fall in reports originating from EU services; some cite a 58% decline as platforms dialled back scanning to avoid legal exposure. Companies argue that with end‑to‑end encryption more widespread, automated detection is often the first alert.

Civil liberties groups counter that bulk scanning of private communications risks eroding confidentiality, generates false positives and may expand to other forms of content. Providers of encrypted messaging have long argued that client‑side or server‑side scanning undermines security guarantees, and data‑protection authorities have raised questions about proportionality and legal basis.

In the absence of the derogation, firms are expected to restrict automated detection for EU users, while continuing moderation of public posts, manual review triggered by user reports, and safety features such as age‑gating. Some may geofence detection capabilities, creating divergent safety baselines between EU and non‑EU users and complicating cross‑border investigations.

Negotiations on the EU’s proposed long‑term child‑safety regulation remain contentious, with institutions split over how far scanning should reach into private messaging. Member states face limited room for national fixes under EU law. In the meantime, expect legal challenges, product design changes to limit risk, and greater reliance by law enforcement on non‑content signals and international cooperation.
