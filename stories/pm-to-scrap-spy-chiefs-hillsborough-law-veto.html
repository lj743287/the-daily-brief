PM to scrap spy chiefs' Hillsborough Law veto

The prime minister is preparing to remove the veto that would have allowed spy chiefs to block intelligence evidence in future public inquiries.

The prime minister is preparing to remove a veto that would have allowed security chiefs to block intelligence evidence in future public inquiries. The move is a significant adjustment to the proposed Hillsborough Law, which is intended to improve transparency and accountability after state failures.

The issue matters because public inquiries exist precisely to establish facts that governments or institutions may prefer not to disclose. Allowing intelligence agencies to veto evidence would have risked undermining public confidence in any process meant to get to the truth.

By scrapping the veto, ministers are signalling that security concerns should not automatically override the public interest in disclosure. That is likely to be welcomed by campaigners who have long argued that the state should not be able to shield itself from scrutiny too easily.

There may still be friction with security officials, who will want reassurance that sensitive material can be protected where necessary. But the direction of travel is clear: the government wants future inquiries to be harder for the state to frustrate.
