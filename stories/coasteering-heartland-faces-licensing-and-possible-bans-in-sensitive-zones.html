‘Coasteering heartland’ faces licensing and possible bans in sensitive zones

Pembrokeshire National Park plans a licensing regime to protect fragile coastal environments. Operators warn of impacts on tourism businesses; conservationists cite safety and ecological pressures.

Pembrokeshire Coast National Park is preparing to introduce a licensing scheme for coasteering, with the possibility of bans in the most sensitive areas, in an effort to protect fragile cliff and intertidal habitats along one of Wales’ busiest adventure coastlines.

The plans would require commercial operators to hold permits and follow agreed conditions such as limits on group size, timing of sessions and access routes, alongside potential seasonal exclusions around seabird nesting and seal pupping sites. Park managers say the measures are intended to balance a rapidly growing activity with conservation duties and public safety.

Businesses in the self-styled coasteering heartland warn the move could add costs and uncertainty, potentially deterring visitors and squeezing smaller outfits. Several operators argue they already adhere to established safety standards and voluntary environmental codes, and contend that guided groups are more controlled and less damaging than ad hoc visits driven by social media.

Conservationists and rescue professionals point to repeated pressures on wildlife and the coast, as well as callouts to incidents among inexperienced participants. They say licensing would improve oversight, reduce disturbance in hotspots and help align the activity with the park’s ecological objectives, particularly where cliff ledges and intertidal flora are vulnerable to trampling and noise.

Pembrokeshire is widely regarded as the birthplace of coasteering and a pillar of Wales’ adventure-tourism economy, making any new restrictions closely watched beyond the county. Similar tools—permits, seasonal closures and quotas—are increasingly used to manage visitor numbers in protected areas from US national parks to Southeast Asian marine reserves, and the outcome here could set a precedent for other coastal activities.

A formal consultation is expected to shape the final rules and any designated no-go zones before the next peak season, with byelaws likely to require approval by Welsh ministers. Key tests will include how effectively the park can enforce conditions, whether activities are displaced to unmanaged spots, and how far the scheme supports both nature recovery and viable local businesses.
