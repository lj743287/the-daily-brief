EU lets child‑abuse scanning law lapse, prompting safety warnings

A temporary legal carve‑out enabling platforms to scan for grooming and CSAM has expired amid privacy concerns. Child‑protection groups fear a sharp drop in reports; lawmakers face pressure to reconcile safety, encryption and fundamental rights.

A temporary EU carve‑out that permitted platforms to scan messages for child sexual abuse material (CSAM) and grooming has expired, creating immediate legal uncertainty for firms that had relied on it. Lawmakers did not extend the measure, citing privacy concerns, leaving companies to reassess detection in the bloc from 3 April.

Child‑protection groups and several major platforms warn that reports to hotlines and law enforcement could fall sharply as tools are switched off or curtailed to avoid breaching the ePrivacy framework. They point to a previous lapse earlier in the decade, when detections dropped significantly before rebounding after legal cover returned. The fear is that cases will go unseen, slowing interventions that depend on timely platform reporting.

Privacy advocates and some MEPs counter that generalised scanning of communications—especially proposals for client‑side analysis on encrypted services—undermines confidentiality, risks false positives and sets a precedent for broader surveillance. Europe’s courts have repeatedly pushed back against indiscriminate monitoring, and data protection authorities have raised concerns about proportionality and legality.

In the background sits a broader, still‑unresolved child‑safety regulation. The Commission’s plan to mandate detection on some services has faced concerted opposition, particularly over end‑to‑end encryption. Member states are split on scope and safeguards, and the Parliament has pressed for a more targeted approach. The Digital Services Act, already in force, obliges large platforms to assess and mitigate systemic risks, but it does not itself authorise scanning private communications.

Operationally, fewer platform reports would strain an ecosystem that routes tips to national centres and specialist NGOs. Investigators already juggle volume, accuracy and cross‑border coordination. Smaller platforms, lacking legal teams and bespoke tooling, may suspend voluntary measures altogether in the EU until the legal path is clearer, while encrypted messengers reiterate that breaking privacy protections is not a sustainable safety strategy.

Attention now shifts to whether an interim fix can be found or whether the debate moves straight to the long‑term law after the European elections. Options include narrowly scoped warrants, better resourcing for targeted investigations, and age‑appropriate design measures. Any renewed bid for broad scanning is likely to invite legal challenge. In the meantime, companies are emphasising user‑reporting flows, metadata‑based signals, and education, while warning that these are no substitute for the tools they have just lost.
