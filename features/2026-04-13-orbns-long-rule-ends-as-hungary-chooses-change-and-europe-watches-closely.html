Orbán’s long rule ends as Hungary chooses change — and Europe watches closely

Hungary’s electorate has delivered a sharp rebuke to Viktor Orbán, elevating Péter Magyar from insider-turned-insurgent to the centre of power. The result matters far beyond Budapest: it weakens one of Moscow’s most useful voices inside the European Union and tests whether a protest movement can become a durable governing force.

For 16 years, Viktor Orbán appeared to have built something close to political permanence. He remade Hungary’s institutions, dominated the media environment and turned repeated election victories into proof that his model of national conservatism had won the argument at home and won admirers abroad. Now that era has been swept away by a challenger who only recently stood inside the same political machine he is accused of destroying.

Péter Magyar’s landslide is striking not merely because it ousts a long-serving leader, but because it does so through the familiar democratic route Orbán himself once used to ascend. Magyar, 45, is an ex-party insider who translated public frustration into a broad anti-establishment coalition, persuading a majority of voters that change was preferable to continuity. The scale of the defeat suggests this was not a narrow protest but a decisive rejection of the governing project as it had come to be understood.

That project was never solely Hungarian. Orbán became the European Union’s most conspicuous internal dissenter, obstructing consensus on Russia, migration, rule-of-law enforcement and sanctions policy. In Moscow, his survival was useful: it showed that a leader who challenged liberal democratic norms could still thrive inside the EU architecture. His removal will therefore be felt in Brussels and, perhaps more acutely, in the Kremlin, where his value lay less in hard power than in political cover.

Yet a landslide against an entrenched incumbent does not immediately answer the harder question of what comes next. Magyar’s rise has been fuelled by disillusionment with corruption allegations, state capture and an increasingly weary electorate; those are powerful motives for removal, but they are not themselves a governing programme. Like many anti-establishment movements, his challenge now is to convert the energy of resentment into the slower, more difficult work of administration, coalition management and institution-building.

That task is complicated by the legacy he inherits. Orbán’s system was not only personalised, but deeply embedded: loyalists in key posts, a political culture shaped by permanent campaigning, and an economic model that blended state direction with patronage. Unpicking such a structure, even with strong public backing, is difficult. If Magyar moves too fast, he risks destabilising institutions and frightening moderates; if he moves too cautiously, he risks disappointing the voters who delivered him power.

Europe’s mainstream leaders will welcome Orbán’s departure, but not because the continent’s problems disappear with him. Hungary has been both a symptom and a laboratory of the broader democratic malaise that has affected parts of Europe: polarisation, distrust in institutions, resentment at elites and the appeal of strongman politics. A change of government in Budapest is therefore encouraging, but it is not a clean victory for liberalism so much as a reminder that electorates can still reverse course when dissatisfaction becomes too deep to manage.

The geopolitical implications are also more immediate. A Hungary less inclined to frustrate EU policy could make European decision-making easier at a moment when the bloc is under pressure on security, energy and industrial competitiveness. But any new government will still have to defend Hungarian interests, including its dependence on external energy and its exposure to regional economic shocks. Orbán’s exit may change the tone of Hungary’s diplomacy before it changes its constraints.

For Moscow, the loss is symbolic as much as strategic. Hungary under Orbán offered Russia a valuable crack in the Western front: a member state willing to slow, dilute or complicate collective action. A new administration may not be uniformly hawkish on Russia, but even a modest reorientation in Budapest would matter. In Europe, symbolism is often policy’s first draft.

The more profound lesson, though, may be that long incumbency is not the same as inevitability. Orbán spent years making opposition appear futile, yet a rival emerged who could turn frustration into a governing majority. That should temper assumptions about political permanence elsewhere, especially in systems where control is strong but consent is thinning. It also poses a challenge to those who see anti-establishment movements as inherently transient: given the right conditions, they can become the new establishment.

Hungary now enters an uncertain but consequential transition. Magyar has won the right to try to define the post-Orbán era, but the burden of proof will arrive quickly. He must show that he can restore trust without merely inheriting the old habits in new language. If he succeeds, this will be remembered as one of the more significant democratic reversals in modern Europe. If he fails, Orbánism may yet outlive Orbán himself.
