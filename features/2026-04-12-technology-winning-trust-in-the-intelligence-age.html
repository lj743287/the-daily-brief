Winning trust in the intelligence age

Artificial intelligence is moving from laboratory feat to social infrastructure, but legitimacy has not kept pace. In 2026, companies, courts and capitals are vying to set the rules — and the public is judging them less by rhetoric than by lived experience.

The most striking development in technology this year is not a breakthrough model or a sparkling gadget, but a struggle for legitimacy. AI now sits in schools and surgeries, in policing and payment flows, yet consent for its spread is fragile. Polling across democracies shows wariness; artists and musicians are contesting the terms of use over their work; parents and teachers are looking for guardrails, not slogans. The question is no longer whether these systems are powerful, but whether their architects and overseers can prove they are worthy of trust.

Sensing the shift, the industry has pivoted from product demos to policy. One leading developer has urged firms to trial four-day weeks to adapt to automation, issued a people-first industrial policy paper, and opened a Washington office to court lawmakers and civil society. Others are bankrolling thinktanks and commissioning research to frame the narrative as opportunity rather than threat. This is not inherently cynical — ideas matter — but it is a recognition that technical prowess alone will not secure social licence.

Regulators and courts, meanwhile, are no longer bystanders. In the United States, a high‑profile AI company is suing Colorado over rules designed to curb algorithmic discrimination in housing, healthcare and finance, arguing the law violates free‑speech rights. In Europe, a temporary legal basis that allowed platforms to scan for child sexual abuse material has lapsed amid intense privacy concerns, with child‑safety groups warning of a cliff‑edge in reporting. Greece plans to bar under‑15s from social media next year. And after losing a landmark case related to social media addiction in California, the owner of Facebook has pulled adverts recruiting claimants for similar suits. The patchwork is uneven, but the direction is unmistakable: the era of light‑touch oversight is ending.

Trust is also shaped by the mundane and the personal. News that a former Meta employee is being investigated for downloading tens of thousands of private photos underlines the old truth that data risk is as much about people and process as code. Amazon’s decision to end support for pre‑2013 Kindles, preventing new e‑book downloads, has sparked anger from readers who feel digitally dispossessed. Such episodes feed a suspicion that platforms set terms unilaterally and that users carry the downside risk when policies or personnel fail. No number of white papers will fix that impression without visible changes in conduct.

The economics of attention are shifting at the same time. Businesses are scrambling to make their sites legible to AI search, re‑architecting information into structured, machine‑readable forms in the hope that conversational engines will cite them rather than answer from memory. This is more than another SEO arms race; it points to a world in which access to markets runs through proprietary models and interfaces. Against that backdrop, the strategic contest between the US and China — one seemingly ahead in general‑purpose models, the other with an edge in applied, embedded systems — is about standards as much as supremacy. Whoever intermediates the flow of facts will quietly set the norms for what counts as knowledge.

Authenticity is the other axis on which confidence is eroding. London’s mayor has warned of a disinformation blizzard painting the capital as a city in decline, a local case of a global phenomenon. Musicians are finding AI‑generated tracks posted to streaming platforms under their names; artists argue that their archives have been scraped without consent to train systems that now compete with them. An AI lab recently said it had built a model too risky to release on cybersecurity grounds; sceptics saw marketing rather than stewardship, though the claim still prompted attention from officials and MPs. Between grandstanding and genuine hazard sits a pressing need for provenance, auditing and redress that the current ecosystem cannot yet reliably provide.

Yet to focus only on harms is to miss why the stakes are high. For a dancer with motor neurone disease, brainwave technology and a digital avatar enabled a return to the stage and, with it, a sense of agency. Patients on social platforms report that peers and even strangers have spotted symptoms that sped their diagnoses, though such crowdsourced medicine plainly carries risks of error and anxiety. These vignettes are not mere curios; they show that the prize — augmenting human capability and care — is real. The task is to bring the baseline of reliability up to the level already glimpsed at the frontier.

What would it take to win trust rather than demand it? Start with measurement: publish comparable safety benchmarks and error rates, including for bias, and make post‑deployment audits routine. Clarify liability so that when systems mislead, individuals are not left to shoulder costs alone, and create safe harbours for good‑faith disclosure of failures. Require provenance signals for media, and build simple redress for creators whose identities or catalogues are abused. Tie child‑safety duties to enforceable transparency, not just slogans. And when platforms retire products, adopt sunset obligations that preserve users’ purchases and data. None of this will settle the world’s arguments about AI, nor should it. But legitimacy, like code, is engineered: earned in practice, not proclaimed on stage. If the sector can show its systems work for people — and if governments enforce the bargain — the intelligence age will have a foundation sturdy enough to carry its promise.
