The rules of the internet are being rewritten — and AI is speeding the edits

From Europe’s child-safety legal gap to Greece’s under-15s social media ban, from Meta’s courtroom setbacks to AI firms’ policy offensives, the boundaries of digital life are up for renegotiation. As artificial intelligence reshapes discovery, labour and power, governments, platforms and users are scrambling to keep up.

The internet’s governance is being pulled in multiple directions at once. Long‑running disputes about privacy, child safety and platform accountability now collide with the rise of artificial intelligence, which is transforming how people find information, how work is organised and who exercises gatekeeping power online. Across jurisdictions, the regulatory map is fragmenting, while the largest technology companies mount their own political campaigns to control the narrative — and the rules that will follow.

In Europe, a stark example of the trade‑offs came as the European Parliament allowed a law permitting platforms to scan for child sexual exploitation on their services to lapse. Child‑safety experts warn the legal gap will sharply reduce reports of abuse, echoing a reported 58% decline during a similar hiatus in 2021; privacy‑minded lawmakers counter that indiscriminate scanning risks normalising mass surveillance. The stand‑off encapsulates a decade‑old dilemma: when private, encrypted communication collides with the urgent imperative to detect harm, neither principle surrenders cleanly.

National governments are meanwhile testing stricter age rules. Greece plans to ban social media for under‑15s from next year, following related moves in France and Spain. Supporters frame such measures as overdue protections against addictive design and exposure to harm; critics question practical enforcement and fear overreach that could curtail expression or drive teenagers into harder‑to‑monitor corners of the web. As with many child‑safety proposals, efficacy will hinge less on headline bans than on design standards, data use limits and auditability — details that are often politically harder than declarations.

Pressure on platforms is rising from the courts, too. After losing a landmark social media addiction trial in California, Meta withdrew Facebook ads recruiting participants for similar lawsuits, while separately disclosing that a former employee is under investigation after being suspected of downloading 30,000 private photos. Together, these episodes underscore twin vulnerabilities: legal exposure over product impact on users’ wellbeing, and persistent risks around insider access to vast stores of personal data. The company insists on remedial measures, but the cases feed a wider reckoning over how far self‑policing can stretch in systems at global scale.

The civic sphere is no calmer. London’s mayor has warned of a disinformation “blizzard” portraying the city as in decline, a claim that speaks to the ease with which narratives can be seeded and amplified across networks. At a more intimate level, scammers are exploiting brand trust with convincing iCloud storage emails that trick users into divulging bank and personal details. And on TikTok, the crowd’s medical sleuthing has, in some instances, nudged users toward diagnoses missed elsewhere — a reminder that the same dynamics that can help can also harm, depending on accuracy, context and luck.

AI is not just another layer of technology atop this system; it is altering the political economy beneath it. OpenAI has urged firms to trial four‑day weeks to adapt to the AI era and has rolled out policy initiatives pitched as “people‑first”, even as critics note a broader push by major AI players to reframe public opinion amid growing scepticism. Anthropic, for its part, said it withheld a powerful cybersecurity‑relevant model from public release, a move that drew both concern and accusations of hype. In the US, Elon Musk’s xAI has sued Colorado over new rules to curb algorithmic discrimination, arguing the law infringes free‑speech rights. The message from industry is clear: we will help write the rules — and, if necessary, fight them in court.

The geopolitics are equally fluid. Reporting suggests China and the United States each lead in different parts of the AI race, with neither willing to let the other dominate; the contest itself could be transformed by fresh technical breakthroughs. Beneath that grand rivalry, a quieter power shift is playing out: AI‑driven search and assistants are becoming new gatekeepers. Businesses are already restructuring websites so machine agents can “see” them, shifting influence away from traditional search results towards opaque models’ syntheses. The risk is obvious: as traffic consolidates behind a few AI intermediaries, accountability for what surfaces — and what is suppressed — grows fuzzier.

What should come next is less a single law than a toolkit. Democratic guardrails that travel across borders — standards for age‑appropriate design, mandatory transparency around recommendation and detection systems, independent auditing of high‑risk AI, redress routes for users — would dampen the whiplash of patchwork rules without collapsing legitimate differences in values. The alternative is drift: episodic bans and expired mandates, headline trials and hurried PR, as harms metastasise faster than remedies. AI did not create the internet’s hardest problems, but it is accelerating them. The choices made now, under pressure and in public, will decide whether the next iteration of the web is more trustworthy than the last.
