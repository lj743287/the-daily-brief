The ceasefire that may not be a truce: how Iran’s war is reshaping the world economy

Donald Trump’s decision to ease back from immediate escalation has bought time, but not certainty. The conflict between the US, Israel and Iran is already feeding through to oil, jet fuel, inflation and supply chains, exposing how quickly a regional war can become a global economic event.

For now, the most important fact about the war between Iran, Israel and the United States is not that it has ended, but that it has not yet broadened. Donald Trump’s latest decision to pause further escalation has temporarily lowered the temperature after a frantic diplomatic day, yet the pattern is becoming familiar: threats of major military action, followed by retreat, followed by another period of anxious waiting. Markets and governments are left trying to price a conflict whose political direction remains unclear.

That uncertainty matters because the consequences are already visible far beyond the battlefield. Oil prices have swung sharply as traders react to every shift in rhetoric or military posture. Jet fuel costs are rising. Airlines are trimming schedules. Consumer inflation, which many central banks had hoped was gradually settling, is being pulled back into the frame by a conflict in the Gulf. A war that began as a regional confrontation is increasingly behaving like a global tax on movement, freight and household spending.

The mechanism is straightforward. The Middle East remains central to the world’s energy system, not because it produces every barrel, but because disruption there affects expectations everywhere. When shipping routes look vulnerable, insurance premiums rise. When refineries fear supply interruptions, fuel prices climb. When fuel rises, so do transport costs, business inputs and eventually the price of food and other essentials. The effect is not always immediate, but it is cumulative, and it reaches economies that are otherwise distant from the fighting.

Britain’s latest inflation figures offer a case study in that transmission. The rise in prices has been linked in part to higher fuel costs following the conflict, underscoring how quickly a geopolitical shock can complicate domestic economic management. Similar pressures are likely elsewhere in Europe and in import-dependent economies across Asia and Africa. For central banks, this creates an uncomfortable dilemma: they must judge whether the inflationary burst is temporary or the beginning of a more persistent squeeze on living standards.

Businesses are already acting on the assumption that volatility may last. Lufthansa’s decision to cut thousands of summer flights is a reminder that aviation is among the first sectors to feel the shock when fuel markets tighten. Airlines typically operate on thin margins and cannot absorb prolonged input-cost increases for long. If the conflict continues to inject uncertainty into energy markets, cuts in capacity, delays in route planning and higher fares could spread across the industry, with knock-on effects for tourism, trade and consumer demand.

The wider lesson is that modern conflict rarely stays confined to the theatre of war. The disruption can reach manufacturing, retail and even unexpected corners of consumer goods. Companies dependent on chemical inputs, shipping or energy-intensive production are vulnerable to price spikes and bottlenecks. That is why the impact of this war is not merely a matter for strategists and diplomats. It is also a test of how resilient the global economy really is after years of pandemic shock, supply chain disruption and inflation.

Politically, the war has also exposed the fragility of deterrence. Trump’s willingness to step back after escalating threats suggests an administration trying to preserve leverage without committing to a costly wider conflict. But repeated pauses can look less like strategy than improvisation. Iran, for its part, is likely to read US caution as evidence that Washington wants to avoid a prolonged war, though Tehran also knows that miscalculation could still trigger a far more dangerous phase. Israel’s calculations are no less complex, shaped by security concerns, domestic politics and the risk of overreach.

That is why the ceasefire, if that is the right word, should be understood as a breathing space rather than a settlement. Any durable de-escalation would require more than a pause in missile exchanges or a temporary easing of strikes. It would need a structure for talks, some form of verification and, perhaps most difficult of all, a way to persuade each side that restraint is more valuable than immediate advantage. At present, there is little sign of that architecture existing.

Meanwhile, the secondary consequences continue to broaden. Governments that depend on stable energy prices to manage budgets are exposed. Households facing higher transport and food bills will feel the war in ways no official statement can soften. International markets, which have become accustomed to treating geopolitical shocks as temporary, may have to reconsider the possibility that this one lingers and periodically worsens. The longer that uncertainty persists, the more it becomes part of the economic landscape rather than an interruption to it.

The danger, then, is not only that the fighting resumes, but that the world adapts to a new normal in which war in the Gulf is one more recurring source of instability. That would leave policymakers with fewer tools, consumers with less room to absorb shocks and markets with a fresh reminder of how fragile calm can be. Trump’s latest retreat may have bought time. It has not bought peace, and it has certainly not bought immunity from the war’s economic reach.
