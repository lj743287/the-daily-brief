Hormuz and the new energy shock: a chokepoint that could rewrite the inflation path

The Strait of Hormuz has again become a fulcrum of global risk. With tankers hesitant, airlines warning of fuel shortages, and forecourt prices rising, a fragile disinflation narrative is colliding with geopolitics that refuses to co‑operate.

The geopolitics of oil rarely moves in straight lines. Tense US–Iran dynamics and a fresh threat to blockade the Strait of Hormuz have turned the world’s narrowest energy artery into a market stress test. What had been a slow, uneven glide towards lower inflation has been disrupted by a familiar transmission mechanism: pricier fuel. In the United States, a jump at the pumps has already lifted headline inflation to 3.3%, while UK motorists are being told not to expect a meaningful retreat in costs. The conflict, as one analysis put it, has become a test of wills — and markets are having to price the consequences.

Hormuz matters because a large share of the world’s seaborne oil and key Gulf exports must pass its waters. Today the issue is not simply the risk of physical damage but the frictions that deter traffic: BBC analysis finds only a handful of vessels have crossed since the latest ceasefire effort, while tanker companies are being advised not to pay levies to Iran for ‘safe passage’. Add war‑risk insurance, legal uncertainty and questions over naval escorts, and energy logistics turn from routine into a high‑stakes calculation.

Aviation is an early barometer of refined‑product tightness. Europe’s airport operators have warned of ‘increasing concerns’ about jet fuel availability if disruptions persist. Airlines can hedge prices but cannot hedge molecules; should supply chains falter, carriers face higher costs, the risk of fuel rationing at certain hubs, and more complex refuelling strategies. Refiners, for their part, must juggle crude sourcing and product slates just as margins on middle distillates — diesel and jet — tend to widen in shortages.

The household and high‑street transmission is already visible. US inflation has re‑accelerated on the back of pump prices; in the UK, petrol and diesel have edged higher and motoring groups caution against hopes of swift relief. Farmers warn that even a ceasefire may arrive too late to prevent higher input costs from flowing through to food prices. In Ireland, fuel protests and blockades have idled petrol stations and forced an emergency cabinet discussion, underscoring the political sensitivity that attends every spike at the pump.

Asia is exposed in different ways. The region’s heavy reliance on Gulf oil collides with a heat‑driven demand profile that makes energy more than an industrial input; it is, literally, air‑conditioning. Singapore’s dependence highlights the challenge for many Asian economies: they face imported inflation when crude rises, and diminished competitiveness when electricity tariffs follow. With shipping flows tentative and inventories finite, energy policy becomes an exercise in resilience as much as procurement.

For central banks, the dilemma is as old as the oil trade: how to distinguish a temporary terms‑of‑trade shock from a shift that risks unanchoring expectations. Headline inflation can be pulled higher by fuel even as domestic demand softens. The risk is not merely dearer petrol but the second‑round effects on transport, food and services. Rate‑cut calendars that looked plausible a month ago may prove optimistic if refined‑product tightness endures into summer, especially in economies where wage growth remains firm.

The supply side offers only partial release valves. There are pipelines that bypass Hormuz and some strategic reserves to tap, but neither is a full substitute for smooth passage through the strait. Calls for convoy protection, guidance against paying unilateral tolls, and talks that falter all add to the uncertainty premium embedded in energy and freight. Much will hinge on whether credible security arrangements restore tanker confidence; absent that, longer reroutings, higher insurance costs and sporadic shortages will do the heavy lifting of tightening markets.

Three broad scenarios frame the next moves. If the standoff persists without resolution, jet and diesel tightness could deepen, airlines and logistics firms would pass on costs, and the disinflation process risks stalling. A managed de‑escalation that normalises flows would likely see prices ease, but a risk premium would linger given bruised confidence. An outright escalation that curtails shipments would bring rationing into view and sharply raise recession risks. For investors and policymakers alike, the lesson is plain: in this cycle, the path of prices may be determined less by spreadsheets than by a narrow strip of water off Iran’s coast.
