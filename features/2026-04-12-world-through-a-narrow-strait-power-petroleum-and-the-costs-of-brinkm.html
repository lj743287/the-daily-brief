Through a narrow strait: power, petroleum and the costs of brinkmanship

Donald Trump’s threat to blockade the Strait of Hormuz has raised the stakes in a contest of resolve with Iran, even as marathon talks failed to unlock a diplomatic path. The world’s energy artery is again a theatre for risk, and the longer it remains constricted, the higher the political and economic price everywhere.

Once more, the world’s gaze narrows to a waterway barely wide enough for the ambitions now passing through it. The US president’s threat to impose a blockade on the Strait of Hormuz has injected fresh peril into a standoff already defined by reprisals and deterrence. The risks are plainly higher; the underlying predicaments remain stubbornly in place: Iran’s capacity to absorb strikes and retaliate asymmetrically set against Washington’s tolerance for military, economic and political costs.

Hopes that urgent diplomacy might blunt the crisis have faltered. Historic, high-level talks between American and Iranian officials in Pakistan ran for hours without securing the reopening of Hormuz or a roadmap beyond a fragile ceasefire. The encounter exposed both the urgency and the limits of engagement: enough trust to meet, too little to trade meaningful concessions while shots are still being fired elsewhere.

The military calculus is unforgiving. A blockade, were one attempted, would test international law and allied patience as much as Iranian forbearance. Tehran has signalled it can weather punishment while harrying shipping and partners through deniable or dispersed means; Washington has signalled resolve without specifying how a blockade would be enforced, for how long, and at what price. In the confined geography of the Gulf, miscalculation is not a tail risk but a constant companion.

Energy markets do not wait for communiqués. With a chokepoint of global significance under strain, governments are scrambling for resilience. In Australia, the prime minister sought assurances in Singapore about refined fuel supplies, ministers warning of a long economic tail even if traffic through Hormuz resumes soon. From Asia’s import hubs to Europe’s forecourts — where protests and outages in Ireland have become a barometer of public anxiety — the squeeze on fuel is rippling into politics.

The crisis is not contained to the Gulf. On Israel’s northern frontier, strikes and reprisals between Israel and Hezbollah intensified even during a wider ceasefire, drawing public calls from Canberra for de-escalation on both sides. The pattern is familiar: local clashes amplify the risk of a broader arc of confrontation in which a spark in Lebanon or Syria can swiftly complicate diplomacy aimed at the Gulf itself.

Domestic politics shadow every move. In Washington, a show of strength abroad collides with the reality of household fuel bills and jittery allies; in Tehran, strategic patience and calibrated defiance are levers for both regime legitimacy and negotiating leverage. Seasoned former officials warn against assuming that a swift operation in one theatre offers a template for regime pressure elsewhere; the Middle East is already demonstrating the perils of such analogies.

Legitimacy and law will matter if the crisis deepens. Hormuz is an international strait through which the world’s lifeblood moves; freedom of navigation is not merely a slogan but the scaffolding of an interdependent economy. Any campaign that impedes commercial traffic risks isolating friends as well as foes, yet leaving the waterway hostage to violence and threats invites a slow erosion of the very norms that have kept it, mostly, open since the tanker wars of the 1980s and the scares of 2019.

What follows is likely to be incremental rather than dramatic: renewed shuttle diplomacy, partial maritime deconfliction, and pressure on regional actors to rein in their clients. Without tangible steps to ease passage through Hormuz, the world should plan for a protracted squeeze — higher prices, disrupted voyages, frayed alliances and the ever-present danger of an accidental clash. The channel remains narrow; so too does the margin for error.
