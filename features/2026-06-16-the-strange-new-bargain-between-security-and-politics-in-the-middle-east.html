The strange new bargain between security and politics in the Middle East

Donald Trump’s ceasefire and deal with Iran may have lowered the temperature in a region that has spent months on the edge of wider war. But the political shockwaves are only beginning, not least for Benjamin Netanyahu, who now faces a dilemma that is as domestic as it is strategic.

For a few anxious weeks, the Middle East seemed to be moving towards a confrontation that could have pulled in the region’s main military powers, disrupted global energy supplies and forced Washington into a far deeper commitment than it wanted. The ceasefire and deal announced by Donald Trump have, at least for now, interrupted that trajectory. Yet the significance of the agreement lies not only in what it may have prevented, but in the new political landscape it creates: one in which Iran has not vanished as a threat, Israel has not secured the outcome it wanted, and the region’s most important alliances have been exposed to strain.

At the centre of this new uncertainty is Benjamin Netanyahu. For years, the Israeli prime minister has made political and strategic capital from presenting Iran as the defining security challenge of the era. That argument has served him well in rallying allies, justifying military pressure and keeping attention fixed on the dangers posed by Tehran’s nuclear programme and regional networks. But a ceasefire brokered by Mr Trump leaves him in an awkward position. If the agreement holds, he cannot easily claim that military escalation was necessary. If it fails, he must explain why Israel did not secure a decisive advantage despite the moment of maximal pressure.

This is why the deal has the feel of a political nightmare as much as a diplomatic event. Mr Netanyahu has long depended on the idea that Israel must retain freedom of action against Iran, regardless of American preferences. Yet the deal suggests that, for now, Washington has chosen containment over confrontation. That does not mean the United States has abandoned Israel, or that it regards Iran as no longer dangerous. It does mean the White House has signalled that it sees limits to escalation, and that it is willing to impose those limits even on a close ally when wider interests demand it.

The economic consequences are immediate and global. Any reduction in the risk of regional war tends to ease pressure on oil markets, shipping routes and food prices, all of which have been vulnerable to shocks since the conflict intensified. Traders price not only present disruption but the possibility of future disruption; a ceasefire can therefore move markets almost as much as a battlefield victory. But the effect should not be overstated. The Middle East remains heavily armed, mistrust runs deep, and the infrastructure of commerce — from energy infrastructure to maritime corridors — is still exposed to any renewed escalation.

That is why claims of stability should be treated with caution. Ceasefires in the region have often proved less a resolution than a pause. Iran may calculate that time now works in its favour, allowing it to preserve leverage while avoiding immediate punishment. Israel may decide that a temporary lull is simply a chance to regroup. And the United States may hope that a managed stand-down buys room for broader diplomacy. None of those calculations is irrational; all of them could also unravel quickly. The deal reduces danger, but it does not remove the underlying confrontation over Iran’s strategic reach and nuclear ambitions.

For Mr Netanyahu, the domestic politics may be no less serious than the security questions. Israeli politics has become increasingly shaped by whether a leader appears strong enough to deter enemies and flexible enough to preserve alliances. A ceasefire arranged by an American president with whom he has often had a close relationship can be spun either as evidence of success or as a sign of lost control, depending on how events develop. If Israelis conclude that the government has traded away a clearer military payoff for a temporary diplomatic pause, the prime minister’s opponents will have a fresh line of attack.

There is also a broader lesson about the limits of coercion. Military pressure can degrade capabilities, shift calculations and signal resolve. It cannot, on its own, solve a political dispute between states that have spent decades defining each other as threats. Any durable settlement would need some combination of verification, restraint, regional guarantees and a way of managing the wider contest for influence across the Middle East. None of that is simple. But without it, any ceasefire is likely to remain contingent on the next perceived provocation, the next misread signal or the next domestic political need.

The immediate question, then, is not whether the region has been made safe. It has not. The real question is whether the ceasefire marks the beginning of a more disciplined phase of confrontation, in which all sides accept that the costs of open war are too high, or merely a tactical pause before the next round. The answer will depend as much on Washington and Jerusalem as on Tehran. And it will depend, above all, on whether leaders who have built careers around escalation can adjust to a moment when restraint may prove more valuable than confrontation.

That is what makes the agreement so consequential. It is not just a diplomatic manoeuvre between enemies. It is a test of political narratives that have framed the conflict for years. Mr Trump can present the deal as proof that force, leverage and deal-making can still produce outcomes where diplomacy alone has failed. Iran can claim it has preserved room to manoeuvre. Israel can insist that vigilance remains essential. But only one of those stories can be true in practice: either the region is moving towards a more stable equilibrium, or it is buying time before the next crisis. The evidence so far supports caution, not celebration.

For now, the ceasefire has done what diplomacy is often judged by first: it has interrupted immediate danger. The deeper verdict will come later, when markets have settled, political tempers have cooled and the parties decide whether this is a bridge to something more durable or simply a short reprieve. Mr Netanyahu, caught between strategic ambiguity and domestic pressure, may discover that surviving the ceasefire is harder than surviving the conflict.
